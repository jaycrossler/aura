# Military Address Culture — USAF and Space Force on Mars

## Summary

How U.S. Air Force and Space Force personnel address each other on Mars follows specific conventions that should be applied consistently across all dialogue in the series. The conventions reflect both formal military protocol and a specific tradition of compassionate informality toward junior officers — particularly lieutenants. This file is the canonical reference for who calls whom what.

## The "LT" Convention (Lieutenants)

**"LT" (pronounced "el-tee") is a friendly, familiar nickname for lieutenants** — used by  enlisted personnel and, often, by superior officers as a sign of warmth toward younger lieutenants - almost like they are a mascot for the unit. Lieutenants don't call each other "LT" but always by their first name. It is **not** a sign of disrespect; in this culture, it is the opposite. Calling a lieutenant "LT" rather than the full "Lieutenant" signals affection, mentorship, and a kind of protective camaraderie.

### Who Uses "LT"

- **Junior enlisted (Airmen, Guardians):** Use "LT" when addressing lieutenants directly, almost always paired with a salute or nod. Signals warmth and respect simultaneously. Common greetings: *"Morning, LT."* / *"LT, you got a sec?"* / *"Welcome to the base, LT."*
- **Senior enlisted (Sergeants, NCOs):** Use either "Lieutenant" or "LT" depending on situation. More formal in front of higher brass; warmer in routine work. Mentorship register often calls for "LT." *Good afternoon, Lieutenants.*
- **Superior officers (Captains, Majors, Colonels) toward lieutenants under their command:** May use "LT" in informal/operational settings as a sign of affectionate command. *"LT, walk with me a minute."* This is mentor-mode register.
- **Lieutenants toward each other:** **Do not use "LT" for peers.** They use first names. (See below.)

### Who Does Not Use "LT"

- **Lieutenants do not call other lieutenants "LT".** Peer-to-peer, they use first names. Rich calls Jace "Jace" — never "LT" — because they are equals. If in front of others in a formal setting, they will refer to each other by rank and last name *Lieutant Grant and I are reporting as ordered, sir."
- **Civilians do not use "LT".** They use "Lieutenant" or no rank at all.
- **Senior officers in formal settings** revert to "Lieutenant" — briefings, official functions, formal reprimands.

## Specific Patterns for Rich and Jace

This is the canonical address pattern for the two main lieutenant characters across Book 1:

| Speaker | Addressing Rich | Addressing Jace |
|---|---|---|
| Rich (peer) | — | "Jace" |
| Jace (peer) | "Rich" | — |
| Junior enlisted | "LT" / "Lieutenant Cullivan" | "LT" / "Lieutenant Grant" |
| Senior enlisted | "Lieutenant" / "LT" / "sir" | "Lieutenant" / "LT" / "sir" |
| Captain (their boss) | "Cullivan" / "LT" / "Lieutenant" | "Grant" / "LT" / "Lieutenant" |
| Colonel (base CO) | "Lieutenant" / occasionally "LT" in warmer moments | "Lieutenant" / occasionally "LT" |
| Civilians | "Lieutenant Cullivan" / "Rich" if friends | "Lieutenant Grant" / "Jace" if friends |

### The First-Meeting Exception

On first introduction, even between peers, formal address is appropriate. When Rich first meets Jace at the landing pad, Rich's opening line is **"Lieutenant Grant? I'm Rich Cullivan."** This formality breaks down within minutes. By the rover ride, they are using first names. By lunch, the friendship register is established and persists.

## Salute Conventions

Junior enlisted salute lieutenants when first encountering them in a duty setting (entering a workspace, meeting on a base path, formal contexts). Salutes are returned. Salutes are not constant — once two people have already greeted each other in the day, repeat salutes are not expected.

The combination "LT, [salute]" is a common, warm greeting on the base. Jace will hear this dozens of times during his initial tour and it will form part of his sensory impression of arriving on Mars.

## Branch-Crossing Address

Across Air Force and Space Force, address conventions are essentially the same. A Space Force Guardian addressing an Air Force lieutenant uses "LT" or "Lieutenant" the same way. A Space Force Lieutenant Colonel addressing an Air Force Lieutenant uses "Lieutenant" formally or "LT" informally. The service rivalry shows up in tone and banter, not in address protocol.

## Why This Matters for Prose

- **Dialogue authenticity.** Get the address pattern right and the military scenes feel real. Get it wrong and the reader trips over it.
- **Character relationships.** The shift from "Lieutenant Grant" to "Jace" in Rich's mouth marks a relationship moment. The persistence of "LT" from enlisted personnel after Jace's discharge — or its sudden absence — is a story beat in itself.
- **Compassion-as-culture.** The "LT" convention encodes a genuine warmth in the military culture of this universe. It is part of what makes Jace's loss of this world hurt so much when he is discharged — the people who called him "LT" no longer have a reason to.

## Post-Discharge Address

Once Jace is discharged, he is no longer "LT" or "Lieutenant" to anyone. Civilians who knew him use "Jace" or "Mr. Grant." Old base contacts who run into him may use "Jace" or, awkwardly, fall into "LT" out of habit and then catch themselves. This catch — the small social stumble of the old name no longer fitting — is a usable beat for emotional landing in the post-discharge arc.

- **The "LT" convention persists to military in the Belt** if Jace ever runs into former military there, they probably heard his story of being discharged.  American military will probably call him "LT" affectionately.
