# {Aura} Chronicles — Frontier Belt Transport System and Economy

## Core Concept

The primary transportation system connecting Mars and the asteroid belt is the Modular
Belt Transport, commonly called a Wheelship, Spinship, or simply a Belt Runner.

The design evolved from one simple realization: "People do not live in spaceships.
They live in buildings. The ship simply carries the buildings."

Rather than constructing unique spacecraft interiors, standardized habitat modules are
attached to a rotating habitation ring. The expensive propulsion systems remain in
service for decades while living spaces, cargo modules, hospitals, machine shops, and
laboratories can be swapped between vessels — and, in some cases, between a ship and a
station. See [technology/tech_modular_pod_canisters.md] for how this plays out at the
individual-character level.

This document describes the class-typical Belt Runner. The *Falcon* is a specific,
older, one-of-a-kind vessel within this family with her own exact figures — see
[ships/ship_falcon.md] for her actual dimensions, mass, and tank/berth counts, which
differ somewhat from the typical numbers below.

## The Bicycle Wheel Metaphor

Every spacer understands the ship through a simple analogy: "A bicycle wheel wrapped
around a nuclear tugboat."

The ship consists of three major sections, plus the modules that plug into the ring.

### The Spine

A long central shaft running the length of the vessel. Contains the main propulsion
systems, reactor, fuel tanks, heat radiators, engineering spaces, docking ports, and
cargo handling systems. The spine carries all thrust loads — the ship is pushed
through space by the spine.

### The Spokes

Three large structural spokes connect the spine to the habitation ring, carrying
elevators, ladders, pipes, power lines, and data networks. Traveling from the ring
toward the spine feels like climbing "uphill" toward zero gravity.

### The Ring

The ring is the ship's main street. Typical class figures: radius approximately 40
meters, diameter approximately 80 meters, rotation rate approximately 2.5-3 RPM,
artificial gravity approximately 0.3g. To residents, the outer wall is "down," the
inner wall is "up," and walking the circumference feels like walking a circular
neighborhood. Many crew members never enter the spine except for work assignments.

### The Modules

The ring contains standardized docking berths. Each berth accepts a roughly 13-meter
modular unit derived from the historical 40-foot shipping container standard. Modules
may be crew quarters, family housing, medical clinics, laboratories, machine shops,
livestock facilities, storage units, or emergency shelters. A typical configuration
runs in the range of 18-19 berths per side (36-38 total modules). Each module provides
roughly 65-70 cubic meters of internal volume, 35-45 cubic meters of practical living
space — crew often compare them to small apartments, tiny homes, or recreational
vehicles.

## Ship Mass (Class-Typical)

| Component | Mass |
|---|---|
| Structural ship | 1,000 tonnes |
| Hydrogen fuel | 500 tonnes |
| Argon propellant | 100 tonnes |
| Water reserves | 300 tonnes |
| Cargo & passengers | 100 tonnes |
| **Total departure mass** | **~2,000 tonnes** |

Most of the ship is not living space. Most of the ship is water, hydrogen, tanks, and
shielding. A common spacer joke: "The passengers are cargo attached to a flying fuel
depot."

(The *Falcon*'s actual breakdown — ~500t ship/ring structure, up to ~500t pods/cargo,
~1,000t combined water and argon — is older and somewhat differently proportioned; see
[ships/ship_falcon.md].)

## Propulsion

### Hybrid Nuclear Drive

By 2100, interplanetary transport relies primarily on hybrid nuclear propulsion.

**Nuclear Thermal Mode** — used for departure burns, course changes, and arrival
braking. Hydrogen is heated by a nuclear reactor and expelled through the engine
nozzle. High thrust, fast transit, reliable technology.

**Nuclear Electric Mode** — used during cruise. The reactor powers electric plasma
engines using argon propellant. Extremely efficient, low fuel consumption, continuous
acceleration.

### Transit Times

- **Mars to Belt, passenger service:** 110-140 days (roughly four months)
- **Freight service:** 8-18 months (lower fuel consumption)
- **Emergency courier:** 60-90 days (heavy fuel consumption, rare and expensive)

### Radiation Protection

The reactor and engines are mounted far aft on the spine. Protection relies on
distance, water tanks, hydrogen tanks, and dedicated shadow shields. The ring occupies
a protected radiation shadow. A common engineering saying: "Water is life support.
Water is ballast. Water is shielding. Water is fuel waiting for electricity."

## Economics of the Frontier Era (2105)

### Population

Approximate populations:

| Location | Population |
|---|---|
| Earth | ~9 billion |
| Moon | ~50,000 |
| Mars | ~10,000 |
| Asteroid Belt | ~1,000-2,000 |

The Belt consists of **five major stations** — Fortuna, Psyche, Juno, Sylvia, and
Odysseus — each anchored to a named asteroid, ranging from roughly 80 to 450 residents
each. Fortuna, the largest, holds approximately 350. See
[locations/location_asteroid_belt_overview.md] and
[factions/faction_belter_stations.md] for the full station-by-station breakdown.

### Economic Reality

The Belt has moved past pure survival. The early "can we live here at all" phase is
over — the stations are established, food and propellant production work, and people
are no longer dying to prove the concept. What the Belt is in now is a frantic
build-out phase: every station is racing to expand habitat, manufacturing, and
infrastructure capacity faster than its logistics and financing can comfortably
support. Shipments, financing rounds, and skilled labor are all chronically behind
demand. Nobody is starving. Everybody is behind schedule.

Most shipments still move from Mars to the Belt, not the other way around. A common
saying: "The Belt imports civilization and exports hope" — though belters increasingly
add, dryly, "and invoices."

### What Ships Carry Outbound

Habitat modules, machinery, electronics, spare parts, medical supplies, new settlers,
livestock, luxury goods. The settlements consume enormous logistical support.

### What Ships Carry Home

Precious metals, scientific samples, rare isotopes, high-value manufactured goods,
returning personnel. Most return trips are lightly loaded.

### Revenue Sources (Estimated)

- **Government support — 40%.** Strategic presence, scientific investment, political prestige.
- **Propellant production — 30%.** Water mining, hydrogen production, oxygen production.
- **Precious metals — 20%.** Limited exports, carefully managed to avoid market collapse.
- **Manufacturing — 10%.** Specialized spacecraft components, pressure vessels, structural assemblies.

### The Twenty-Year Vision

The settlements aim to become the industrial backbone of the inner solar system by
2125. A common political slogan: "Earth grows food. Mars grows people. The Belt builds
the future."

Expected developments:
- Belt population growth from ~1,000-2,000 to 10,000+
- Major exports: refined metals, pressure vessels, habitat sections, propellant,
  radiation shielding, spacecraft trusses
- **Mass driver networks:** automated launchers throwing cargo inward, captured by
  Mars and lunar facilities, moving millions of tonnes annually. Crewed ships
  increasingly focus on people, electronics, medicine, and high-value cargo.
- **Shipbuilding:** the long-term vision is entire spacecraft constructed in the Belt
  from raw asteroid material — steel, trusses, habitats, ships. "The Belt becomes the
  shipyard of humanity."

### Common Sayings

"The Belt is where rocks become ships." "Mass is money." "Hydrogen buys speed." "Water
buys survival." "Every kilogram launched from a planet is a debt someone must
eventually pay." "A ship leaves Mars carrying supplies. It returns carrying
possibilities."

## Disaster Scenarios (Reference)

**Spin Imbalance** — a module loses mass through leakage; the wheel becomes slightly
unbalanced; vibration, bearing stress, and increased maintenance follow. Crew scramble
to move water ballast and restore equilibrium. Left unchecked: structural fatigue,
spoke damage, ring failure. (This is the shape of the Falcon's Book 1 Ch. 2 crisis.)

**Bearing Failure** — the rotating ring depends on massive bearings; failure causes
heat buildup, seizure risk, and emergency spin reduction. In extreme cases the ring
must be stopped entirely, and everyone aboard suddenly loses gravity.

**Hydrogen Boiloff Crisis** — damaged cryogenic insulation accelerates fuel loss; the
ship may no longer have enough fuel to brake at destination. The crew faces a choice
between diverting, reducing payload, or calling for assistance.

**Reactor Shutdown** — the reactor trips offline; electric propulsion is lost, life
support is restricted, water recycling is reduced. The vessel enters survival mode.

**Propellant Market Collapse** — a major discovery floods the market with
platinum-group metals; expected profits vanish; settlements face bankruptcy; political
pressure mounts to abandon frontier operations.

**Delayed Supply Ship** — a transport misses its launch window; expected medical
supplies, replacement parts, and new personnel don't arrive for months. The colony
discovers whether it is truly self-sufficient.

**Ring Separation Accident** — a spoke suffers structural damage; the crew must decide
whether to evacuate the ring, stop rotation, or jettison modules. A nightmare scenario
feared by every spacer.

**Mass Driver Navigation Error** — a cargo package launched from the Belt deviates from
its capture corridor; a multi-billion-dollar shipment becomes a new asteroid, or worse,
ends up on an Earth-crossing orbit. The political fallout lasts decades.
