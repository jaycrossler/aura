# Arc 1 — The Falcon and Fortuna

## Opening Condition

Jace Grant boards the *Falcon* at Mars as a newly-discharged civilian contractor —
identity stripped, carrying a HUD debt, a dog he negotiated for, and a covert
intelligence arrangement he doesn't fully understand. He is heading to the asteroid
belt because there is nowhere else to go.

## Closing Condition

A swarm attack on Fortuna Station is underway or just repelled. Jace and others have
been fighting it with improvised means. A figure arrives — Kael — tailing the swarm,
expecting ruins and primitive survivors. She finds humans in physical space with no
magic. She attempts dominance. It fails. She finds Cerberus. Arc 1 closes. Arc 2 opens.

---

## Arc Goals

These must be demonstrably true by arc close:

- [ ] Jace's identity collapse and reconstruction are complete enough that the reader
  understands who he is and what he has lost — and that the belt is where he rebuilds
- [ ] The *Falcon* transit establishes the confined-space character dynamics that carry
  through Book 1: Maureen, Mei, Lanchee, Cerberus, the livestock, the drone work
- [ ] The plumbing crisis establishes Jace's engineering competence under pressure —
  the win is real, the cost is real
- [ ] Sophia Lotte and the Never-Never are introduced: the reader understands what has
  been discovered and why nobody knows yet
- [ ] Jace's first crossing and the Jace/Sophia/Cerberus partnership are established
- [ ] The Astral survival suits exist as deliberate Astral-native equipment — the first
  purpose-built gear for the Never-Never
- [ ] The tau snap mechanic, distance² ratio, and atomic weight² crossing cost rule are
  all discovered empirically through experimentation, never stated as given facts
- [ ] Sophia's near-death outside the station hull establishes that the Astral kills
  without warning and without leaving a trace anyone would understand
- [ ] The open-source decision with Nick is made — {Aura} becomes the belt's
  distributed magic-interface layer
- [ ] Jace's Astral vehicle experimentation subplot runs as a sustained iterative
  background thread through the Fortuna section — failure-heavy, ultimately successful
- [ ] The LOX eyebrow incident occurs — comedy with genuine stakes, establishing
  ablation rules for aluminum/hydrogen rocket engines
- [ ] Jace's drone survey using Astral N-1 perception locates a platinum-rich asteroid
  target; the coordinates are logged but not immediately acted on
- [ ] The Falcon swarm rescue earns Maureen's trust — her later willingness to lead
  the Métis mission is grounded here
- [ ] The first successful cross-dimensional machinery transport is demonstrated
- [ ] {Aura}, {Alex}, {SpoX}, {Chorus}, {Penny}, {Seek}, and {Misty} all appear with
  `{}` braces consistently throughout
- [ ] No magic ability has a typographic bracket marker — plain prose throughout Arc 1

## Background Threads (Parallel — Reader Tracks, Protagonist Cannot)

**Thread A — Sophia's Prior Weeks**
Sophia has been crossing alone for a month before Jace arrives. The reader gets
fragments — her exhaustion, her absence from normal station life, her crewmates' worry.
The mystery of what she's doing has been building in the station before Jace gets there.

**Thread B — Helena**
Fortuna's first un-suppressible disappearance happened before Jace arrived. Suzi has
been investigating. The official story is incomplete. The China withdrawal is being
misread as sabotage. The station is already under stress before the swarm.

**Thread C — Comms Degradation**
The communications filtering architecture is tightening. Jace's attempts to reach
Artemis at Métis keep failing in small ways — delayed messages, strange gaps, one
message that arrives with a sentence missing. The reader knows something is wrong
before Jace does.

**Thread D — The Pig-Cam**
Nick Lee on Fortuna follows the *Falcon*'s pig-cam like a nature documentary. He and
Jace are already friends across a shrinking comms lag. The connection that will become
central to the open-source decision is being built casually, through multiplayer gaming
and animal antics.

### Scene 1-02a — The HUD Headaches (Distributed — Recurring, Pre-Crisis)

**Summary:** Every morning during transit Jace wakes with a headache behind his eyes —
pressure and heat from the implant running hot overnight. He has been dismissing it,
but the frequency is increasing. He finally flags it to Lanchee. She runs tests, finds
nothing alarming, and tells him she'll run a second panel tomorrow when a new
diagnostics package finishes downloading. The second panel also finds nothing.

**Must accomplish:**
1. The headaches appear at least once in each morning-routine scene before the
   plumbing crisis — brief, filed, not solved
2. Lanchee's response is professional and measured: tests show nothing wrong, new
   diagnostics inbound — not dismissive, not alarmed
3. The HUD running hot overnight (dreaming state, glucose draw) is established as
   the plausible mundane explanation Jace accepts

**Must NOT do:**
- Explain the headaches — all three readings (Will-exposure, implant calibration
  drift, something else) should remain available to the reader
- Make the medical beat dramatic; it is a quiet repeated detail that builds unease
- Resolve the mystery in Arc 1 — the right diagnostic tools don't exist yet

**Narrative function:** Seeds the question of what the implant is doing near
Astral-active space. Establishes Lanchee as attentive before she's needed.

### Scene 1-03b — The {Alex} Debrief

**Summary:** {Alex}, Origin's contractor-management AI, schedules a formal debrief.
Jace violated contractual stipulations during the crisis — unauthorized use of ship
systems, risk to Origin's insured cargo, actionable negligence. Fine: $500,000. As a
courtesy, he may instead redeem his entire accumulated Origin Bonus points to cover
legal fees, risk fees, and the fine — but his Platinum Contractor status is then
revoked for the remainder of the voyage. He will no longer have access to the premium
food selection or the advanced coffee machine. He requests a human review. {Alex}
cheerfully confirms the request is logged; processing time is approximately
twenty-four months. Priority Queue access is available for Origin Bonus point
redemption. He has no Origin Bonus points remaining.

**Must accomplish:**
1. The meeting is conducted entirely by {Alex} — no human ever appears or is
   reachable; the cheerful relentless helpfulness is the point
2. The fine and the bonus-redemption offer are presented as equivalent choices — the
   AI does not acknowledge that one costs him everything he has earned
3. The coffee machine loss lands — small, petty, perfectly calibrated to make the
   larger injustice feel personal

**Must NOT do:**
- Make Jace dramatically angry; his register is resigned and dry
- Let {Alex} malfunction or be obviously wrong — everything it says is technically
  correct per the contract he signed
- Explain the satire; let the scene demonstrate it

**Tonal note:** {Alex}'s voice is relentlessly helpful. Every sentence sounds like a
customer service interaction that is technically maximally accommodating. The horror
is the gap between tone and outcome.

**Foreshadowing:** "We have ships with big engines — don't worry, we can catch it."

### Scene 1-07a — The Camera Problem

**Summary:** Sophia has been trying to capture the Astral on camera since her first
crossing. Nothing works right. Cameras see in three dimensions; the Astral has four.
What they capture are strange flat slices — impossible cross-sections of objects with
more depth than the image can hold. She shows Jace the unusable footage, then shows
him what she can actually *see* — pointing out how humans look in the Astral: ghostly
figures with complex glowing patterns moving through their heads like slow-moving light
through a prism. Beautiful, intricate, utterly impossible to describe to someone who
hasn't seen it. It doesn't show up on camera. She mentions that she cannot wait to
publish — this will be the largest scientific discovery in human history — but she is
stuck.

**Must accomplish:**
1. The camera limitation is shown through demonstration, not exposition — she shows
   Jace the distorted footage and he immediately understands why it looks wrong
2. Sophia describes how humans look in the Astral — ghostly, with complex moving
   light in their heads; she does not know what she is seeing; the reader may register
   "Template" before the characters do
3. Her frustration at the documentation problem is professional and specific: seventeen
   notebooks and some unusable footage, cannot publish without peer-reviewable evidence

**Must NOT do:**
- Name what the light patterns are — Sophia is observing something real but has no
  framework for it yet
- Make the camera failure feel like a plot obstacle; it is a genuine engineering and
  physics problem that Jace will treat as such
- Let Sophia's ambition read as vanity; "biggest discovery in history" is accuracy

**Narrative function:** Hands Jace the camera problem. Seeds the contrast between
what the human mind can perceive (four dimensions, imperfectly interpreted) and what
a camera captures (three dimensions, faithfully and uselessly).

### Scene 1-07c — The Atomic Weight Problem

**Must accomplish:**
1. Jace attempts to bring progressively heavier metal pieces through the Astral — the

2. He arrives at the atomic weight² hypothesis through elimination; Sophia immediately

  feel like a pattern that fits, not a law that was revealed
- Answer the HUD question — it carries forward

### Scene 1-07d — Building the Multi-Camera Rig

**Summary:** Jace attacks the camera problem empirically. He starts by trying to bring
Sparky into the Astral — and cannot; the drone is too complex, too many integrated
systems; it resists in ways he cannot fully explain. He pivots: print a hundred simple
cameras instead. He designs and prints arrays of small primitive camera nodes —
single-element imagers, no processing chips, analog output, simple materials. After
many failures he discovers the pattern: components heavier than zinc become
progressively impossible to bring through. Ancient 2010-era circuit-board technology,
reproduced on the Fortuna printers from archived manufacturing specs, starts working.
He builds a jury-rigged contraption of hundreds of nodes and gets partial results —
not good video, but real data, consistent across multiple crossings.

**Must accomplish:**
1. The Sparky failure comes before the multi-camera solution — obvious approach first,
   fails for reasons he can't fully articulate, he adapts
2. The "heavier than zinc" threshold is discovered empirically: aluminum works, iron
   half-works, copper barely works, heavier materials don't survive crossing; he has
   the observation without the theory
3. Getting the Fortuna printers to run 2010-era circuit specs requires some friction —
   not frictionless; this is real manufacturing work
4. The hundred-camera rig produces partial, weird, but unmistakably real data — not
   clear footage; real enough to work with

**Must NOT do:**
- Give Jace a clean eureka moment; slow iteration
- Name the atomic weight² rule — that's Sophia's contribution in 1-07c; here Jace
  has the empirical observation, not the theory
- Let Sparky's failure pass without noticing that Jace tries hard and genuinely
  cannot get the drone across; this is the first established complexity ceiling

### Scene 1-07e — Sophia's Rescue and the Vector Discovery

**What actually happens:** This takes place entirely within the Astral. Sophia's
crewmates on the *Victoria* see a woman asleep in her bunk. Her Aura has a gap in its
network log. From outside: nothing happened. From inside: she nearly died in a place
no one would ever have found her.

**The survival suits (pre-established):** By this point Jace has built two Astral
survival suits from materials he printed or sourced aboard the *Falcon* and at Fortuna.
Designed to cross cheaply — the atomic weight constraint rules out anything
sophisticated. Construction: multilayer thermal insulation (organic composite, low-
density foam, printed polymer — all light elements), full-face polycarbonate helmet,
simple aluminum-housing compressed-air canisters, short-range analog radio (minimal
copper, 2010-era printed board), tether points on chest and back. No GPS. No sensors.
No digital anything. They look like a cross between ski mountaineering gear and a very
paranoid camping setup. Jace wears his every time he crosses. He has told Sophia she
should wear hers. She has been wearing it for interior crossings. Tonight she considers
herself close to the *Victoria* and leaves it off. This is the mistake.

**The mistake:** Sophia has been navigating the *Victoria*'s Astral interior by feel —
pushing off surfaces, threading through N-1 cross-sections of familiar corridors.
She misjudges a vector near the docking connection between the ship and Fortuna's ring.
In physical space this is a sealed pressurized collar. In the Astral it is a thin
cross-sectional line — and the dimensional direction she pushed on runs not along the
collar but *through* it, through the hull, through the outer skin of the ring, and out
into open space. She does not realize it immediately. The temperature doesn't change
much. The darkness is the same. What changes is orientation: the ring's spin — which
in the station interior she felt as directional pressure against surfaces — is gone.
She is in free-drift.

The station is visible in N-1 perception, a faint complex of nested cross-sections, but
she cannot tell which dimensional axis she exited on. She tries to push back. She moves
sideways. She tries again. She moves differently sideways. She is tumbling slowly in
three dimensions she can name and a fourth she cannot consciously direct. She has been
crossing for about forty-five minutes tonight — she has roughly three to three and a
half hours of air remaining. She is not in immediate danger of dying. She is, however,
completely unable to get back inside, and no one on the other side has any idea where
to look. She calls Jace on her pocket radio. It takes three attempts to raise him.

**The rescue — Part 1 (getting to her):** Jace crosses fast, wearing his suit. Finding
her is not immediate: she guides him by radio — "I can see the ring from here, I'm
above it I think, there's a slow pull in one direction" — and he works outward from the
hull scanning for her Will-glow. Will-glows are visible in the Astral dark; hers is the
brightest thing nearby. He gets to her and grabs her arm. He has the second suit folded
in a carry bundle on his back — its crossing cost is low enough to bring bundled but
noticeably heavier than wearing it, which is why he is already tired when he arrives.
He helps her into it while they drift in open Astral space. Getting the helmet sealed
in free-drift and weak gravity takes longer than either of them would like. They manage.
Her air canister engages. She has four hours now instead of three.

**The rescue — Part 2 (getting back in):** The station ring is rotating at 2.6 RPM.
In the Astral the ring appears as nested moving cross-sections. The crossing-in angle
rotates with the ring. If Jace hits it wrong they don't enter the ring — they glance
off into open space or clip the ring's metal structure for a disorienting moment before
being pushed out. First attempt: he misjudges the rotation timing and they overshoot.
Second attempt: he gets the angle but not the depth — a moment of pressure and
wrongness, then back out. Third attempt: slower, more deliberate. Sophia has better
intuition for the dimensional geometry; Jace has better intuition for the physical
timing. Together they work out that the right approach is to move *with* the ring's
rotation before pushing through — like docking a ship rather than walking through a
wall. They come through into a maintenance corridor three sections from either of their
quarters. The lights are off. They are back.

**The aftermath:** They sit against the corridor wall for a while. Neither speaks.
Jace eventually asks if she's okay. She says yes. Then, after a pause: "I want to talk
through what I did wrong." This is who she is. She was forty-five minutes into a
crossing she considered low-risk. She was not wearing the suit because she thought she
understood the space. She has been crossing for a month and she has never been outside
the station's interior before tonight, and the reason she didn't know that was dangerous
is that she didn't have a clear picture of where the interior ended.

What the scene makes clear without stating: she came within a realistic probability of
dying alone in a space that does not officially exist. Her crewmates would have found
her in her bunk with a mask on her face and a gap in her Aura log. The cause of death
would have been recorded as scrubber saturation or unknown. There would have been no
rescue, because there would have been no understanding that there was anything to
rescue. The Astral is not hostile. It is not kind. It is a physical system with rules,
and the rules do not include a warning when you are about to die.

The fumble through the maintenance corridor to their quarters — physically exhausted,
suit components clanking, trying not to wake anyone — is at low energy. It is late.
They are tired. They do not have a clean ending. They go to sleep.

**Must accomplish:**
1. The survival suits are pre-existing equipment — not invented in the moment; Jace
   built them, he wears his, she didn't, and this gap is the proximate cause of the
   danger escalating as fast as it did; the suit he brings is the turning point from
   "serious" to "survivable"
2. The vector/re-entry problem is specific and spatial — the ring is spinning; the
   crossing angle rotates with it; the solution requires Sophia's geometric intuition
   and Jace's physical timing; three attempts; solved by moving with the rotation
3. The danger is arithmetic, not atmospheric — air supply, orientation loss, and
   the absence of any safety system that would trigger; the Astral is indifferent
4. Sophia's crewmates see nothing — she is in her bunk; the only trace is a gap in
   an Aura log; maintaining this invisibility is part of the tonal point
5. The aftermath is quiet and specific — Sophia processes by analysis; she identifies
   exactly what she did wrong; she is more frightened than she shows; the reader sees
   this through texture, not statement
6. The fumble home is unglamorous — cold, tired, wrong corridor, wrong time, just
   going to sleep

**Must NOT do:**
- Invent a dramatic external threat; the scene earns its tension from physics
- Let either character brush off the event
- Make the re-entry problem feel convenient; it has a real solution that takes real
  time to derive
- Let the suit delivery read as heroic; it is practical; Jace packed it because he
  is an engineer who plans for contingencies

**Sophia's behavioral change (affects subsequent scenes):**
- She stops crossing alone entirely for several weeks
- When she resumes, she stays strictly within interior spaces with physical reference
  points visible at all times; she wears the suit without exception
- She builds a tether-and-anchor line before Jace suggests it — characterization:
  she solved it herself; it becomes standard kit for both of them
- Her notes become more careful about the distinction between what she has observed
  and what she has assumed

**Foreshadowing:**
- The survival suits are the first deliberately Astral-engineered gear; Kael notices
  them in Arc 2 as the first sign Jace has been thinking about the problem correctly
- Moving with the ring's rotation is the earliest version of the vector-matching
  insight underlying Long Stepping; Jace's body has learned something his mind
  hasn't named yet
- Sophia's anchor-line solution becomes part of what they hand off to other
  practitioners — the first piece of Astral safety doctrine

### Scene 1-07f — The Rubber Band: Discovering the dist² Rule

**Summary:** Over several sessions Jace has noticed that distances in the Astral behave
strangely — things seem to go further than they should. His drone rigs seem to travel
much more than the physical-space equivalent of his movement. He develops a system of
physical anchors and measured rope lengths (actual rope — copper is too heavy) to
measure displacement without GPS. The data is confusing at first. Then he runs the
numbers differently. Things are going further *squared*. Walk ten meters physically,
travel a hundred in the Astral. Walk a hundred physically, travel ten thousand in the
Astral. Sophia, when he shows her, reaches for her seventeen notebooks. Her Will-cost
measurements for longer crossing distances have been anomalous in exactly this way.
They put the data sets together and the fit is very good.

Then the next question: when does the multiplier reset? They experiment. It resets from
wherever you last stood still. Not from where you entered — from wherever you last
stopped moving. This is when Jace starts to understand that the Astral doesn't track
where you came from. It tracks where you are. There is no stored entry point. The
origin is always the last point of rest.

**Must accomplish:**
1. The discovery takes multiple sessions — the reader experiences the iteration; no
   single breakthrough moment
2. The distance measurement problem is shown as a real engineering challenge: no GPS,
   no reliable electronic sensors; solved with physical anchors and counting
3. The dist² rule is derived from data, confirmed by Sophia's independent measurements,
   and expressed in Jace's voice — engineer's satisfaction followed immediately by
   "but *why* does this work?" which he cannot yet answer
4. The tau-reset behavior is discovered as a separate observation following naturally
   from dist²; the fact that there is no stored "home" should feel strange and slightly
   unsettling
5. The explanation is clear enough that the reader can reason about future Astral travel
   without needing re-explanation — use concrete examples in Jace's internal voice;
   let Sophia double-check the math

**Must NOT do:**
- Explain the physics in narration; the reader understands through what Jace measures
- Skip the false starts — the first measurement attempts produce confusing results
- Have Sophia simply confirm what he's found; she has independent data that *adds*
  something; her Will-cost measurements are the second data set that makes the fit
  compelling rather than a coincidence

**Narrative function:** This is the scene where the Astral stops being mysterious and
starts being *physics*. The shift from wonder to engineering is a tonal turning point
for Arc 1. The dist² rule is the first hard number they derive together — the first
thing they can eventually publish.

**Reader orientation note (for prose draft):** The dist² rule is the reader's first
navigational anchor for understanding Astral travel. The explanation should be clear
enough that a reader can reason about future travel distances without needing to be
re-explained. Consider having Jace explain it to {Aura} — his explanation to his AI
instance, in his own voice, is simultaneously his explanation to the reader.
