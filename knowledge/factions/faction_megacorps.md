# The Megacorporations

## Summary

By the story's present, a handful of megacorporations control most of the practical infrastructure of the human solar system. They build, own, and operate the cyclers, the ferries, the mining stations, the orbital habitats, the launch facilities, and substantial fractions of the surface infrastructure on Mars and Earth. Governments matter — but the corporations are where the money, the engineering, and the operational power live.

These corporations are the descendants and successors of the early commercial space firms (SpaceX, Blue Origin, Boeing, Rocket Lab, Chinese state enterprises like CASC, and others) plus newer entrants that emerged in the 2050s-2080s. By the late 21st century the field has consolidated into roughly six to ten dominant players, each with global or system-wide reach.

This file is an **overview**. Specific corporations should eventually get their own faction files.

## The Major Players (Working Sketches)

### "Stellar Dynamics" (working name)
Successor to SpaceX through several mergers and rebrandings. Largest single owner of cycler tonnage. Major stake in ferry operations. Headquartered nominally in Texas; effectively a multinational with strong U.S. ties. Heavy in launch services, transport, and orbital infrastructure.

### "Origin Industries" (working name)
Successor to Blue Origin. Smaller than Stellar Dynamics but extremely well-capitalized and patient. Specializes in habitat engineering, life support, and long-duration systems. Owns substantial Mars surface infrastructure.

### "Tianhe Cosmics" (working name)
Chinese state-affiliated megacorp. Tightly coordinated with Beijing. Major in cycler stake, lunar operations, and Chinese Mars infrastructure. Operates with the institutional discipline of a state enterprise and the agility of a competitive firm.

### "Bharat-Indo Spaceworks" (working name)
Indian-led, multinational. Newer than the American and Chinese majors. The fastest-growing player. Strong presence in the Indian-led Mars settlement and increasingly in the belt. Known for innovative governance models — partial worker ownership, multi-stakeholder boards.

### "Roscosmos-Volga" (working name)
Successor to Russian state space programs and several oligarch-owned firms. Smaller than the others. Strong in defense systems and specific high-margin niches. Tightly tied to Russian state interests.

### "Helios-EU" (working name)
European multinational with strong national-government participation. Strong in research, science, and high-precision manufacturing. Less aggressive than its peers; more politically embedded.

### Specialty / Niche Players
A long tail of smaller corporations specializing in narrower domains — Aura development and licensing (multiple firms), cybernetic implants (medical-tech specialists), specific mining technologies, agricultural systems, niche logistics. Some of these may become major story players.

## How the Corporations Operate

- **Distributed across multiple jurisdictions.** Most majors are domiciled in multiple legal regimes (Earth nations, Mars settlements, ad hoc orbital authorities) to optimize regulatory exposure and political access.
- **Cross-investment is normal.** Stellar Dynamics holds stakes in Tianhe Cosmics; Tianhe holds stakes in Helios-EU; everyone holds stakes in the cycler consortium. Public competition coexists with deep financial entanglement.
- **State relationships are transactional.** A corporation that opposes its home government on a specific issue may work with a foreign government on another. National loyalty is a marketing tool more than a constraint.
- **Operational autonomy is real.** Corporations operating in deep space make decisions on operational timescales that governments cannot match. By the time a national authority has formed an opinion, the corporation has already executed.

## What the Corporations Want

Different corporations have different cultures and priorities, but most are driven by:

1. **Long-term infrastructure ownership.** Whoever owns the cyclers, the ferries, the mining rights, and the habitat capacity holds the future.
2. **Regulatory capture.** Influence over the rules that govern interplanetary commerce is more valuable than direct subsidy.
3. **Talent and capability concentration.** The best engineers, the most experienced operators, and the smartest analysts are scarce. Acquiring and retaining them is competitive.
4. **Risk management at species scale.** Some megacorp leadership genuinely sees themselves as stewards of human survival and acts accordingly. Some are cynical. The mix varies.

## How They Will React to Magic

When magic returns, the corporations will respond in characteristic ways:

- **Stellar Dynamics** will move fast, try to identify and recruit sensitives, and probably get into trouble.
- **Origin Industries** will move slowly and study, then strike strategically.
- **Tianhe Cosmics** will coordinate with the Chinese state and try to establish state-corporate authority over magical practice in Chinese settlements.
- **Bharat-Indo** will partner with traditional practitioners and emphasize voluntary cooperation; this will turn out to be the most successful approach in the medium term.
- **Roscosmos-Volga** will try to weaponize.
- **Helios-EU** will publish papers.

These are sketches. Refine.

## Relations with Governments and Other Factions

The corporations are not above governments, exactly, but they are not below them either. The relationship is mutual capture. Senior corporate leaders move into government positions and back; senior officials retire to corporate boards. Earth's geopolitical fragmentation has made this rotation easier than it would be in a unified world.

In the belt, where governments are functionally absent, the corporations are even more powerful — they own the infrastructure outright. But the belters' increasing local autonomy, especially after the communications blackout, will erode this. By story's later arc, belter cooperatives and unions begin to reshape corporate operations from below.
