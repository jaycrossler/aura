# Origin Industries

## Summary

Origin Industries is the dominant solar-system logistics megacorporation at story start — the descendant, through several generations of mergers and rebrandings, of the early-21st-century Blue Origin and a constellation of smaller logistics firms acquired across the 2050s-2080s. Its identity, branding, and corporate religion is built around a single principle: **anything, anywhere, at any time — at the right price.** Origin promises that you can have whatever good you want, delivered wherever you are in the solar system, and the only question is what you are willing to pay for the speed.

Origin owns a substantial fraction of solar-system surface infrastructure on Mars and at Fortuna Station, operates much of the Mars-to-belt nuclear ferry fleet (including the *Falcon*), runs the Origin orbital catch network at Mars, and has a meaningful stake in the cycler consortium. It is patient, well-capitalized, and disciplined — less flashy than its rival Stellar Dynamics, but generally considered the better-run company.

In the story's present, Origin is also the first megacorporation visibly affected by belt operational anomalies, and the corporation that hires Jace Grant onto a ten-year contract he does not fully understand.

## The Time-Cost Pricing Model

Origin's core commercial proposition is its time-cost pricing model:

- **Need it in the next few hours?** A specialist courier picks it up from local fabrication or warehouse stock and delivers. Cost: extreme. Reserved for medical emergencies, executive priorities, and situations where money is genuinely no object.
- **Need it tomorrow?** Express logistics, prioritized shipping, in-system air-equivalent. Cost: high. The standard for serious business needs.
- **Need it next week?** Premium logistics. Cost: moderate. Most premium consumer purchases.
- **Need it next month?** Standard. Cost: ordinary. Most commercial freight.
- **Need it in six months or a year?** Bulk. Cost: low. Ferries can be loaded with optimal trajectories. Most belt-bound supply.
- **Need it in two years?** Cycler bulk freight. Cost: minimal. The cheapest way to move mass across the inner solar system.

The Origin promise is that you can choose any tier, that the prices are transparent, and that the delivery commitment is real. In practice this works most of the time. When it doesn't, Origin's customer service is famously responsive — and famously evasive about why.

This pricing model is one of the world-building cornerstones of the series. It creates economic texture (urgency costs money), cultural texture (belters plan a year ahead because they have to, Mars residents can think in shorter horizons because they can pay for it), and plot texture (the breakdown of Origin's reliability is one of the early signals that something is wrong in the belt).

## What Origin Does

### Surface and Orbital Logistics on Mars

Origin operates large fractions of Mars's surface freight, last-mile delivery, warehouse capacity, and orbital cargo handling. The Mars orbital catch network (which retrieves asteroid-belt material shipments) is largely Origin-operated. Mars residents interact with Origin daily without thinking about it — a package from Earth arrives via Origin; a delivery from a Mars manufacturer arrives via Origin; a shipment to a belt station leaves via Origin.

### Mars-to-Belt Ferry Operations

Origin owns or operates a significant fraction of the nuclear ferry fleet that runs from Mars to belt stations. The *Falcon* — Jace's ferry — is part of this fleet. The ferries carry settlers, livestock, infrastructure, and consumer goods to belt stations on roughly 4-5 month transits.

Ferry operations are commercially competitive but operationally cooperative — Origin coordinates with other ferry operators on safety, comms, and emergency response. This cooperation will matter when the comms blackout falls.

### Fortuna Station

Origin is the primary corporate operator at Fortuna Station, with substantial U.S. government backing. Fortuna's status as the inner belt's agricultural hub is largely an Origin commercial strategy — Origin wants the belt's food supply chain to anchor at a station where Origin holds the dominant position. The implications for Jace's eventual life there are significant: he is, structurally, an Origin employee in an Origin-managed environment.

### Cycler Consortium Stake

Origin holds a meaningful but not controlling stake in the cycler consortium. *Niña*, *Pinta*, *Santa María* are operated under shared international authority, but Origin's stake gives it influence over capacity allocation and priority traffic.

### Habitat Engineering and Life Support

Origin's historical specialty (inherited from Blue Origin's lineage) is long-duration habitat engineering. Origin builds and maintains substantial fractions of Mars surface habitat, the *Falcon*-class ferries, and Fortuna Station's living infrastructure. This engineering competence is real — Origin habitats are, on average, the most reliable in the solar system.

## Corporate Culture

- **Patient money.** Origin invests on multi-decade horizons and takes its corporate religion seriously: durable infrastructure, durable profit. Less prone to short-term thinking than its rivals.
- **Disciplined.** Origin's reputation among employees is "demanding but fair, slow to fire, slow to promote." Its hiring is methodical. Its negotiation culture is professional and not crooked, but it is the corporation's job to get good deals, and Origin does its job.
- **Quietly aggressive.** Origin moves carefully but does not move passively. When it identifies a strategic asset (a station, a contract, a person), it pursues with patience and resources.
- **Not cartoonishly evil.** Origin is not a villain in any simple sense. It is a competent megacorporation operating in its own interest. It will exploit asymmetric information (as it does with Jace). It will not destabilize a station for short-term profit. The reader should feel that Origin is *real*, not that Origin is bad.

## Relations With Other Factions

- **Stellar Dynamics:** Direct competitor. Mostly cordial, occasionally tense. Origin and Stellar coordinate on safety standards and emergency response, compete on pricing, occasionally poach each other's talent.
- **Tianhe Cosmics (Chinese state-affiliated):** Cooperative on infrastructure (cyclers, orbital traffic), competitive on belt operations (especially around Psyche and the metal trade).
- **Bharat-Indo Spaceworks:** Friendly. Origin has commercial relationships with the Indian-led Mars settlement and Indian-affiliated belter stations.
- **U.S. Government:** Tightly entangled. Origin and the U.S. military / intelligence community have substantial mutual investment. Mr. Sylvester's intelligence operation runs partly through Origin infrastructure (Mei is an "Origin rancher" on her cover). Origin tolerates this cooperation because it generally aligns with Origin's interests.
- **Belter cooperatives:** Increasingly tense. Origin's commercial dominance at Fortuna is starting to chafe against the emerging belter political identity. This will be a major story arc in later books.
- **The Smuggler / informal economy:** Origin formally opposes; informally tolerates. The Rat moves shipments through Origin facilities with the quiet acquiescence of mid-level Origin staff who find informal channels useful.

## How Origin Will React to Magic

When the magical anomalies become undeniable, Origin's response will be characteristic: study, then strike strategically. Unlike Stellar Dynamics (which will move fast and probably get burned), Origin will take time to understand what is happening. Once it does understand — likely after the blackout begins to lift — Origin will move to acquire, recruit, and structure magical practice within commercial frameworks. Whether this works is a later-books question.

Origin is also positioned to be the corporation that *could* become a real antagonist if any single megacorp does — its competence and patience make it dangerous in ways that Stellar Dynamics's flash-and-grab style is not.

## Story-Specific Hooks

- **The contract with Jace.** Origin's ten-year contract is not unusually exploitative by industry standards, but it is a meaningful debt-and-time obligation that locks Jace into the belt. His eventual relationship with Origin — employee, debtor, possible thorn — is one of the through-lines of the series.
- **The merchant's supply chain.** When Origin's belt operations falter and the merchant in the bar scene loses her quotas, Origin is the visible cause without being the underlying cause. This complicates the reader's relationship with the corporation.
- **The communications filtering.** Origin's corporate communications infrastructure is part of the multi-actor filtering architecture that shapes what reaches Jace from his sister.
- **The Sylvester arrangement.** Mei's cover as an Origin rancher and Jace's eventual contract with Origin are both expressions of the U.S. government's working relationship with Origin. The corporation is not innocent of intelligence cooperation; it is a participant.
