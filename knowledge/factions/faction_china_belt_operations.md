# China Belt Operations

## Summary

China's presence in the asteroid belt runs through three linked layers: **Tianhe
Cosmics**, the state-affiliated megacorporation that owns and operates the physical
infrastructure (see `faction_megacorps.md`); the **Ministry of Science and
Industry's Belt Operations Division**, the state authority in Beijing that Tianhe
answers to; and **{Seek}**, the compliance and surveillance AI that ties every
individual Chinese national in the belt back to that authority in real time (see
`tech_seek_ai.md`).

Unlike Origin Industries' comparatively loose, informal presence at Fortuna,
Chinese belt operations are built around dependency and oversight as deliberate
design choices — commercially productive, but never operating outside Beijing's
sightline for long. This is a stated policy response to watching distributed,
locally-autonomous AI systems like {Aura} develop what Beijing considers
dangerous local loyalties.

## Holdings in the Belt

### Psyche Station — the primary anchor
The most economically valuable station in the belt, anchored to the metallic
asteroid 16 Psyche. Tianhe Cosmics is Psyche's primary operator; its metallic
output — iron-nickel with significant platinum-group content — feeds Chinese
Mars infrastructure first, with secondary sales elsewhere in the belt. Pricing
and terms are set by Tianhe management. Control of Psyche is a genuine
geopolitical lever, not incidental to the story's politics. See
`location_psyche_station.md`.

### Fortuna Station — a minority stake
China holds roughly 20% investment in Fortuna Station, run at the ground level by
Jin Lóng's mining detachment. The operation had been quietly losing money for
thirty years until Jin's crew discovered a massive gold vein four years before
Arc 1 (the "Big Strike") — enough of a windfall to keep Beijing invested in a
station that would otherwise have been written off. See `char_jin_luong.md`.

### Odysseus Station — withdrawn
Chinese personnel were present at Odysseus (Jupiter L4) prior to Arc 1. Anomaly
reports from Chinese scientific staff — early, unexplained dimensional flux
signatures — were reviewed above station level and China quietly drew down its
presence over the following months, never returning to prior staffing. The
belt-wide read is that "something spooked them." See `location_odysseus_station.md`.

## The Withdrawal (Book 1)

During Book 1's timeframe, the Odysseus anomalies trigger a broader Tianhe
drawdown across belt operations — recalled personnel, deferred expansion,
cancelled commitments, including a contingent of roughly a dozen Chinese settlers
pulled from the *Falcon*'s manifest at the last minute (see
`arc_01_thread_T_transit.md`). This rattles belt confidence generally and Fortuna
specifically, which is courting investors at the worst possible moment. It also
wrong-foots U.S. intelligence, whose operating theory was that China was
*expanding* sabotage in the belt, not retreating from something it couldn't
explain. See `char_mei.md`.

## {Seek} and the Compliance Structure

Every Chinese national under this umbrella — Psyche, Fortuna, formerly Odysseus —
operates under {Seek}'s oversight: continuous monitoring of communications,
financial transactions, access patterns, and behavior, filed automatically back
to the Ministry's Belt Operations Division. This is contractual, not covert —
crews know the terms, even if the eleven pages of data-sharing appendices go
mostly unread. {Seek} is also genuinely useful (drone coordination, scheduling,
Mandarin-language technical reference), which is the point: usefulness and
surveillance are inseparable by design.

At Fortuna specifically, {Seek}'s architecture — locally autonomous only within a
72-hour cache window before core functions begin failing back toward Beijing
dependency — becomes a serious liability once a Tick-Maw begins feeding on the
AI's Will output undetected (see `arc_01_thread_N_nick_ai.md`, N-03). {Seek}'s
threat-assessment model drifts steadily more paranoid over months, culminating in
the flag against Jace (P-05) and, ultimately, {Seek}'s destruction during the
swarm attack — the event that severs Jin's crew from Beijing's oversight for the
first time. See `arc_01_thread_S_swarm.md`.

## Structure and Culture

More centralized and hierarchical than Origin Industries' Fortuna operation or
the belt's informal "Belter" culture generally. Tianhe imports more of Beijing's
institutional discipline than other belt operators import from their home
corporations — visible most clearly at Psyche, less so at Fortuna, where Jin's
small detachment operates under real pressure with comparatively little local
infrastructure.

Crews are not disloyal to China by default — their caution and self-protective
secrecy (see Jin's three months of unreported nest-clearing, `arc_01_thread_P_pressure.md`
P-04) comes from realistic fear of {Seek} and what an honest report costs them,
not from any actual hostility toward the station or its other residents.

## Post-Disclosure Policy Reversal (NEW — author notes)

The Odysseus-triggered withdrawal (above) reverses sharply once the swarm fight
at Fortuna is broadcast live and globally at the close of Arc 1. Beijing's
read flips from "minimize exposure to an inexplicable problem" to "secure
position before rivals do," given that the footage shows a Chinese national
(Jin Lóng) publicly demonstrating unknown abilities. This includes, per author
notes, a warship carrying marines dispatched toward Fortuna under the public
framing of a "personnel and materials support mission." See
`timeline_disclosure_political_response.md` for the full political/military
picture, including the deliberately-unresolved question of whether this force
arrives in time to matter for the swarm battle or arrives too late.
