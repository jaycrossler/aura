# The Belter Stations

## Summary

The Belter Stations are not a single political entity. They are a loose network of four to five major mining and habitation stations distributed across the asteroid belt, each anchored to a major asteroid, each tied loosely to one or more Earth/Mars factions, and each developing its own local culture. At story start, "Belter" identity is a description, not a political identity — but that is changing rapidly, and the communications blackout will accelerate the change.

Total belter population at story start: approximately 1,500-3,000 across all stations. Each major station holds 150-500 people. Smaller mining outposts and waypoints hold dozens.

## The Major Stations (Working Sketches)

Each of the following should eventually have its own location file. Working profiles:

### "Demeter Station" (working name)
The largest belter station. American-anchored, with significant corporate (Stellar Dynamics, Origin Industries) involvement and U.S. government backing. Population approximately 300-500. Strong agricultural and livestock infrastructure (which is why it receives livestock-primary ferry shipments first). Diverse non-American minority population.

This is the **likely destination of Marcus's ferry**. Demeter is in growth phase, actively building out station capacity, integrating new ferry shipments into expanding habitat. Marcus arrives as part of a substantial settler wave.

### Smaller outposts and waypoints
Various smaller stations, fuel depots, and prospecting outposts scattered across the belt. Many are operated by single corporations or small consortia. Population varies from a handful to a few dozen each.

## Inter-Station Relations

Despite different national/corporate anchors, the major stations cooperate substantially:

- **Safety standards.** Universal protocols for emergency response, life support failures, and environmental hazards. Set by an informal Belt Safety Council that all stations participate in.
- **Trade.** Stations exchange specialized goods, expertise, and labor. Demeter supplies food and livestock; Tianjin supplies manufactured goods; Ashok supplies specialty chemicals and software; etc.
- **Communication networks.** A belt-wide low-bandwidth communication network operates independently of Earth-Mars comms. When the blackout hits, this network becomes critical.
- **Scientific cooperation.** Belt-wide research collaborations exist and intensify under the blackout.

Tensions also exist:

- **Resource claims** to specific asteroids and orbital regions.
- **Labor poaching** between corporate operators.
- **Regulatory disputes** when one station's practices affect another.
- **Cultural friction** between station populations as they develop distinct local identities.

## The Emerging "Belter" Identity

At story start, "Belter" is mostly a descriptor for someone who lives in the belt. Belters identify primarily by their station, their corporate employer, or their national origin.

By later in the story arc — accelerated by:
- The communications blackout
- Shared experience of magical phenomena
- Corporate behavior that benefits Earth-Mars over belters
- Cross-station cooperation in crisis

— "Belter" becomes a genuine identity. Belters begin to see themselves as a single people with shared interests, distinct from Earth, Mars, and corporate stakeholders. This shift is the political backbone of the series.

## Governance

At story start, no formal belt-wide government exists. Each station has its own governance — typically a corporate management structure with limited resident participation, sometimes a more democratic council, sometimes a hybrid. The Belt Safety Council is informal and limited.

Over story time, this will change. Governance experimentation in the belt becomes one of the most consequential political developments of the era — a laboratory for new forms of political organization under conditions Earth and Mars have never faced.

## Economy

- **Mining** — primary industry. Asteroid metals, rare earths, ices, and specialty materials.
- **Processing and manufacturing** — secondary industry, growing.
- **Agriculture and life support** — essential for autonomy. Demeter leads in livestock; other stations specialize in different food production methods.
- **Services** — medical, education, technical training, increasingly self-sufficient.
- **Trade with Mars** — major export of materials and processed goods; major import of specialized goods, settlers, breeding stock, and (controversially) management.

## How Belt Politics Will Develop

Story arc projection:

- **Early:** Station-by-station identity, with cooperation through informal networks.
- **Communications blackout:** Belt-wide cooperation accelerates as stations coordinate without Earth/Mars input. First belt-wide council meetings occur.
- **Magic emergence:** Belter sensitives become a cross-station community. Traditional cultural divides erode.
- **Eventual political crisis:** Earth/Mars attempts to reassert control over the belt face organized resistance. Independence movements emerge.
- **Long-term:** A belter political identity that is genuinely new — not American, Chinese, Indian, etc., but synthesis-cultural and grounded in local conditions.
