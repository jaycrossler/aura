# Progression Signals — Prose Writing Guide

This guide defines how magic advancement appears on the page in *The Aura Chronicles*.
It applies to prose drafts, scene files, and StoryBot output alike. Level numbers,
tree names, and explicit advancement announcements belong in the KB — not in the story.

When writing or reviewing a scene that contains a magic working, advancement moment,
or new skill use, run the checklist at the bottom of this document.

---

## The Core Rule

**A character's skill level never appears in prose.** Advancement is shown through
observable, physical, and relational signals. The reader builds a model of where
each character is without being told. When the reader's model is correct, they feel
smart. When the character does something that exceeds their model, they feel
surprised. Both are good. Neither happens if the level is stated.

---

## The Four Signal Types

### Signal 1 — Effort

The physical and mental cost of a magical working is the most direct progression
indicator available. Track it consistently across appearances of the same skill.

**What effort looks like at early levels:**
- Physical fatigue out of proportion to the physical act
- Headache afterward (crossing-related), or during (concentration tax)
- Hunger or thirst disproportionate to the time elapsed
- Minor disorientation, especially after Transfer or crossing
- Hands or limbs that feel wrong afterward — heavier, clumsier, slightly numb
- Sleep that comes without warning

**What effort looks like as a skill matures:**
- The same working that used to require sitting down is now done standing
- The same working that required total concentration is now done mid-conversation
- The headache is gone; what's left is a mild tiredness
- The tiredness is gone; what's left is a minor awareness that something happened
- The awareness is gone; the working is reflexive

**DO: Track effort continuously.** If Jace moves his coffee mug in chapter 3 with
obvious strain, and moves it in chapter 20 without thinking about it, the reader
tracks that delta without being told. This is invisible progression.

**DO NOT: Skip the cost when the cost is shrinking.** The absence of a cost is as
narratively significant as the presence of one. Both need a line.

**DO NOT: Attribute progression to a single dramatic moment.** Skills get cheaper
through repetition. If a skill was expensive last chapter and is cheap this chapter,
something should account for it — weeks of practice mentioned, an orb absorbed, a
teaching moment passed.

---

### Signal 2 — Ceiling

What the practitioner can attempt tells the reader where they are. What still fails
tells them where the ceiling is. Both are needed.

**Ceiling at early levels — failure is total:**
> She tried to move the copper block. Nothing happened. Not even a shimmer.

**Ceiling at mid levels — failure is qualitative:**
> She got the edge of it moving. Then the thread broke and it thudded back.
> Progress. She wrote it down.

**Ceiling at developed levels — failure is about duration or precision:**
> She could hold the copper block in transfer for about twenty seconds before the
> atomic weight started pushing back. Thirty seconds now, maybe. Two months ago
> it was five.

**Ceiling comparisons between characters (show, don't explain):**
> Kael moved the iron block in about four seconds. Jace stared at it.
> He'd been working on copper for six weeks.

**DO: Show the practitioner attempting things that fail.** Failure demonstrates
where the ceiling is without stating a number.

**DO: Let the failure mode evolve.** The same task failing in a different way is
progression. "Nothing happened" → "I lost it" → "I held it but not long enough"
is a complete arc told entirely through failure.

**DO NOT: Have characters succeed at everything they attempt.** Success without
failure gives the reader no reference point for where the ceiling is.

**DO NOT: Have characters attempt things far below their current ceiling except
for character-specific reasons (teaching, exhaustion, demonstrating to observers).**
A practitioner who has been moving copper blocks for months does not struggle to
move a mug unless something else is wrong.

---

### Signal 3 — Observer Reactions

Other characters — especially Kael — calibrate the reader's sense of what is and
is not remarkable. Use them precisely.

**Kael's reaction arc across the series:**

*Arc 1:* She doesn't observe closely. Her assessment is "unbalanced, primitive."
She notes the Transfer as a data point, files it, doesn't comment.

*Arc 2 (early teaching):* Her reactions are corrections, not assessments.
She says "again" and "less force" and "you're gripping it wrong." This is
normal teaching. It implies normal-speed learning. The reader should read this
as: Kael is not surprised yet.

*Arc 2 (Kael recalibrates — copper block test):* She asks him to Transfer a
copper block. He manages it. She asks how long he's been working on copper.
She asks more questions about the plumbing crisis than he expects. She does not
explain why. The reader sees the recalibration in the questions, not in any
statement of surprise.

*Arc 3:* She has stopped being individually surprised by Jace. She is now
surprised by Sol humans as a category — which is a different and more significant
shift.

**Writing observer reactions:**

**DO:** Let Kael's recalibrations show in what she asks, not what she says. More
questions than expected. Questions about method, not result. The specific questions
that indicate she is updating a model, not just gathering data.

**DO:** Let other practitioners' reactions contrast with Kael's. A galactic
practitioner encountering Jace for the first time reacts with Kael's Arc 1
dismissal. A galactic practitioner who has spent time near humans reacts with
confusion that something doesn't fit their model. These are signals to the reader
that the humans are further along than they look.

**DO NOT:** Have Kael express admiration or surprise in dialogue. She is a 300-year-old
Woven scout. She has seen a great deal. She processes anomalies as data points, not as
emotional experiences. Her surprise is observable in behavior (changed questions,
changed approach, extended observation) — not in exclamations.

**DO NOT:** Make observers stupid to make the human practitioners look impressive.
Every observer who doesn't immediately recognize how remarkable the Sol humans are
is working from an incomplete model, not from a failure of intelligence. Their
framework doesn't have a category for what they're seeing. That is different from
being wrong.

---

### Signal 4 — Comparison Demonstrations

Demonstrated capability differentials between characters communicate relative
development without any statement being necessary.

**DO: Stage comparative demonstrations.** When Kael and Sophia both attempt
the same task, the reader sees the gap without being told it exists. The scene
does the work.

**DO: Let the outcome do all the talking.** No character needs to comment on
what the comparison showed. If Sophia can't move the copper block and Jace
manages it in thirty minutes and Kael manages it in four seconds — the reader
has three data points and can build a relative map.

**DO: Include the observer's reaction to the demonstration in their behavior,
not their dialogue.** After the copper block test, Kael does not say "you are
more skilled than I expected." She asks when he started working on copper.
The question is the reaction.

**DO NOT:** Have characters narrate what the comparison means. If Jace thinks
"I realized I was better at Transfer than Kael" — that's the author stating
the level, just in character voice. The demonstration is the statement.

## The {Aura} Log as Advancement Signal

{Aura}'s logs provide a legitimate secondary advancement signal that doesn't
require in-scene announcement.

**What {Aura} logs appropriately:**
- Observations about Will cost relative to prior baseline: "Estimated Will
  expenditure for Transfer operation: 14% of baseline measurement, compared to
  61% on first documented event." This is {Aura}'s voice, not a level statement.
- Capability expansions: "Transfer ceiling: organic-alloy composite successfully
  crossed on third attempt. Previous ceiling: single-alloy. Flagged for pattern
  documentation."
- Comparative observations: "Practitioner B Transfer ceiling: personal kit only.
  Practitioner A Transfer ceiling: includes large organic-alloy assemblies.
  Ceiling differential: significant."

**What {Aura} does NOT log (in early Arcs):**
- Congratulations, assessments of progress, encouragement
- Level numbers or tier labels
- Anything that reads as a tutorial notification

**The log voice:** {Aura}'s logs are scientific and precise but not warm.
They should feel like a very good field instrument making notes. The reader
gets the information; the characters get it later when they read the log.

## Sub-Template and Cognitive Layer Signals

[Self Template] and its sub-templates require specific signal handling because
the skill is invisible unless deliberately referenced.

**Effort signals for [Self Template]:**
- Absence of the familiar crossing headache — noticeable by what isn't there
- Recovery time from crossings: "He expected to sleep four hours. He woke up
  after two, hungry, functional." No explanation needed.
- Surviving something that should have been worse: "He ran the math afterward.
  Without the Template reinforcement, the atomic tension from that crossing
  distance would have taken him a week to recover from. It took a night."

**Sub-template signals:**
Sub-templates shouldn't be announced; they should appear when they're used.
The first time Jace accesses a stored tool configuration, don't explain what
he's doing. Show him going still for a moment, then producing something precise
from Will and effort. Let Kael's reaction (or absence of reaction, depending on
arc) do the work.

**The Cognitive Layer — never name it in early prose:**
Before Jace and {Aura} have developed their taxonomy and the bracket convention
is established, the Cognitive Layer should not be named. Characters experience it
as: stored impressions, the sense that something is *there* to be accessed,
the act of referring to an internal record. It feels like excellent memory with
a physical texture to it — not like a dimension they're aware of visiting.

## Will Affinity Signals

When a practitioner absorbs a fire-aligned orb (or any type-aligned orb), the
absorbed Will has an affinity that colors their next workings. This is subtle in
the early arcs and should never be explained directly to the reader.

**DO: Let the affinity bleed through in small ways.**
- Food manifested on fire-Will runs slightly warm. Doesn't need explanation.
- Transfer operations powered by fire-Will may have a faint heat signature where
  the object crossed. {Aura} notes this as an anomaly.
- A practitioner running on cold-aligned Will (if such a creature is ever
  encountered) finds their crossings slightly colder than usual.

**DO NOT: Have characters understand the mechanism in early arcs.**
Kael has vocabulary for this. She calls it "Will flavoring" or "resonance tint."
She would not explain it without being asked, and early-arc Jace doesn't ask the
right questions yet.

## Advancement Checklist for Scene Review

Run this checklist on any scene containing a magic working or advancement moment:

### Before Writing
- [ ] What skill(s) are being used in this scene?
- [ ] What is the current level of each skill (from the most recent character sheet)?
- [ ] What was the cost of this working last time it appeared in a scene? Has enough
      time/practice/orb absorption elapsed to justify a cost reduction?
- [ ] Is this an advancement moment (first-time success, new ceiling reached, orb
      absorbed)? If so, which of the four signals will carry the information?

### The Level Test
- [ ] Search the draft for: the character's level number, any word like "advanced",
      "leveled", "improved to", "reached", "unlocked". If any appear in narration or
      character interiority — flag for revision.
- [ ] Does any character state in dialogue what level they or another character is at?
      (Kael will not say this. No character should.) — Flag if so.

### The Effort Test
- [ ] Is the effort cost of this working consistent with where the character currently
      is (per character sheet)?
- [ ] If a working that was expensive is now cheap — is that reduction visible on the
      page? (Even one sentence.)
- [ ] Does the scene show an attempt that fails, or only attempts that succeed? If
      every attempt succeeds, add a ceiling moment.

### The Observer Test
- [ ] If Kael (or another experienced practitioner) observes this working, does her
      reaction reflect her current position in her recalibration arc?
- [ ] Is her reaction shown in behavior and questions, not in dialogue statements?

### The Comparison Test
- [ ] If multiple practitioners attempt the same task in this scene, does the
      outcome communicate relative capability without any character narrating it?

### The {Aura} Log Test (if applicable)
- [ ] If {Aura} logs this scene's events, does the log use observational language
      (cost percentages, capability descriptors, comparative notes) — not
      congratulatory or level-stating language?

### The Sub-Template Test (if applicable)
- [ ] If a sub-template is accessed, is it shown in action rather than announced?
- [ ] Is the Cognitive Layer named or explained before the bracket convention is
      established in-story? (If yes — flag. Use sensory/experiential language instead.)

### The Will Affinity Test (if applicable)
- [ ] If the character recently absorbed a type-aligned orb, does their current
      working have a subtle affinity tint? (This is optional at low levels but adds
      texture.)
- [ ] Is the affinity tint explained to the reader, or just present? (It should
      just be present.)

## Quick Reference — DO and DO NOT

### DO
- Show effort as physical cost: fatigue, hunger, headache, disorientation, sleep
- Show the absence of old costs as the practitioner develops
- Let failure demonstrate ceilings — "couldn't start" → "lost the thread" → "held
  it briefly" → "sustained it"
- Use Kael's questions (not her statements) as recalibration signals
- Stage direct demonstrations between characters and let the outcome carry the
  information
- Use {Aura} logs for precise capability observations in observational language
- Let sub-templates appear in use before they're named or explained
- Let Will affinity tint workings subtly without explanation

### DO NOT
- State level numbers in prose or character interiority
- Use words like "advanced," "leveled," "unlocked," "reached the next level"
- Have Kael express admiration or surprise in dialogue
- Have any observer narrate what a comparison demonstrated
- Skip the cost when the cost is shrinking (absence is a signal; show it)
- Have every attempt in a scene succeed (ceilings need demonstration)
- Name the Cognitive Layer before the bracket convention is established
- Explain Will affinity tinting in early arcs
- Name the skill trees in prose ("he was developing his Templating tree")
- Have {Aura} congratulate or encourage progression
