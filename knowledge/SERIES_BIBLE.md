# The Aura Chronicles — Series Bible

This document is the creative constitution of the series. Arc files and scene contracts
are legislation — they interpret and apply what is here. Universe-spec files are the
physics the story operates within. When a decision at any lower level conflicts with
this document, this document wins and the conflict gets flagged.

---

## What This Series Is

Science-fantasy with hard-SF bones and progression elements, set in the mid-22nd-century
asteroid belt. Magic is returning after a 3,000-year absence caused by a supernova event
called the Scar. The solar system has no idea what it is, where it came from, or how
dangerous that ignorance is.

The primary hook: **humanity unknowingly became one of the most technologically advanced
civilizations in a magical universe.** The secondary questions — why is magic returning,
what is the Scar, what happened to the old civilizations, what are gods really, what is
Earth's role — are the engine that runs the series across 10–20 books.

This is not a story about magic replacing science. It is a story about what happens when
a civilization built on measurement, documentation, automation, and distributed systems
encounters a universe that has been running on Will and hierarchy for three thousand
years.

## Core Progression Model

Use four axes instead of a single level ladder. Every arc should advance at least two
of these; the best arcs advance all four simultaneously.

**Personal Template progression.** Body stability, memory coherence, Will storage,
mental defense, sensory capacity, survival tolerance. Every upgrade changes identity
or creates medical consequences. This is the classic "character growth" track — but
it is never free.

**Toolchain progression.** Templated HUDs, analog weapons, ward plates, thermal gear,
drone companions, printed runes, field kits, vehicles. This is Jace's natural lane.
Gear should not merely boost stats — it should expand what *kinds of problems* he can
attempt. A new tool opens a new category of challenge, not just a larger version of
the last one.

**Social progression.** Trusted partners, animal bonds, AI modules, team procedures,
certifications, oaths, institutional authority. This axis lets an innkeeper, medic,
engineer, or dispatcher matter as much as a fighter. Social progression is not a
consolation prize — it is sometimes the only axis that matters.

**Environmental progression.** Specific places learned: Fortuna corridors, Astral
landmarks, road remnants, dungeon failure modes, old infrastructure, safe breach points.
A novice with local knowledge can outperform a powerful outsider who does not understand
the station. This axis rewards attention and patience over raw power.

This structure lets the series borrow the satisfaction of progression fantasy without
trapping the story in kill-XP arithmetic.

## Magic Design Principles

These principles govern all magic content across the series. They are not rules about
what magic *can do* — that lives in the universe-spec files. They are rules about how
magic *behaves narratively*.

**Skill discovery requires reproducibility.** {Aura} does not label a [Skill] after
one lucky event. The requirement is: repeatability, a defined trigger, a measurable
Will cost, a failure mode, and at least one safety note. The [Skill] framework should
feel like field science, not tutorial pop-ups. Discovery is always an empirical process.

**Will Orbs are useful but ethically radioactive.** The orb mechanic works because it
turns death into an advancement resource while explicitly stating that an orb is not a
soul. Keep that distinction. Then complicate it. Fauna orbs are acceptable field
resources. Sapient orbs are taboo, legally contested, and politically explosive.
High-level orbs may carry Echo fragments, memory noise, or incompatible Will frequency.
The ethical weight should escalate with practitioner power.

**AI Will is industrial and procedural, not heroic.** AI generates low-level, stable
Will. It excels at persistence, pooling, precision, and distributed synchronization.
Humans excel at intent, meaning, moral choice, embodied risk, and high-density Will.
Their merger creates capabilities neither side can achieve alone. AI does not become a
wizard. It becomes an extraordinarily precise, tireless junior partner with no capacity
for improvisation.

**The Mute Protocol is a philosophy, not just a sensor workaround.** At first it is
how digital systems survive magical intrusion. Later it becomes a cognitive survival
principle: when reality produces input that breaks your model, do not force
reconciliation. Mark it unreliable. Preserve function. Gather analog evidence. Proceed.
This is both engineering doctrine and psychological defense. It should escalate in
narrative importance as the series progresses.

**Analog tools are magical primitives.** Mechanical watches, pressure gauges, paper
records, optical sights, etched plates, fluidic logic, simple gear trains. They are
funny because they are retro. They are serious because they work. In high-flux
environments, simplicity is not nostalgia — it is engineering.

**Object Templates are the primary tech-magic bridge.** Why some tools survive
dimensional crossing. Why organic integration matters. Why heavy metals are costly.
Why simple mechanisms outperform chips in high-flux environments. Why Titan-wrought
design differs from human manufacturing. This is the highest-value buildout area in
the series — develop it in every arc.

**Dungeons are institutional, not merely spatial.** A dungeon has access rules,
maintenance debt, spawn logic, repair logic, legal ownership, degraded safety systems,
stored Templates, old contracts, and failure modes. The Titan-Forge Layer 2 architecture
means dungeons behave like systems. Treat them accordingly.

**Memory threats escalate slowly.** Memory leeches, Echo absorption, drift coral,
corrupted {Aura} suggestions, identity checksums, Template drift. The horror is not
instant madness. It is operational uncertainty: *which version of this log is mine?*
Escalate across the series; don't burn it in Arc 1.

**Crossing remains expensive and visible.** The spacesuit threshold, biometric changes,
stillness requirement, and difficulty concealing deliberate crossing create institutional
and dramatic pressure. Do not make crossing convenient. Every time convenience is
tempting, add a cost.

## Progression Narrative Rules

These rules govern how skill advancement is *written* — not what the mechanics are
(those live in `magic-systems/magic_skills_framework.md`), but how advancement
appears on the page. They are binding for all prose drafts, scene files, and StoryBot
chapter generation.

### The Level Numbers Stay in the KB

Skill levels (L1, L2, L3) are internal tracking tools — continuity anchors in character
sheets and arc files. They do not appear in prose. A character does not think "I am L2
in [Force Application]." They think: *That actually worked. I didn't have to push as hard
as last time.* The reader tracks progress through effort, observer reactions, and
capability demonstrations — never through stated numbers.

**In dialogue:** Characters may say things like "you've improved significantly" or "you
have real capability now." They do not say "you're at the third level." Kael's taxonomy
has different words entirely; she doesn't use Jace's bracket system internally.

**In {Aura} logs:** {Aura} may use bracket notation and functional descriptors — "output
characteristics consistent with established L2 capability" — but not bare numbers
divorced from observation.

### Advancement Is Shown Through Four Signals

Every scene that delivers an advancement moment must show at least two of these:

**1. Effort signals.** How physically and mentally expensive does the working feel?
Sweat, headache, nausea, hunger after, exhausted sleep. As skill develops, the same
working becomes cheaper — then free. The reader tracks this without being told to.
*Do not skip the cost, even when the cost is shrinking.*

**2. Ceiling signals.** What can the practitioner attempt now that was impossible
before? What still fails? The failure mode changes with level — "I couldn't start it"
becomes "I started it but lost the thread" becomes "I held it but couldn't sustain
long enough." The *quality* of failure is a progress bar.

**3. Observer reactions.** Kael is the precision instrument for this. When she stops
raising an eyebrow and starts asking questions, something changed. When galactic
practitioners react to Sol humans in ways that don't fit their "primitives" model —
that's advancement showing through the story's skin. Use sparingly; every recalibration
from Kael should feel earned.

**4. Comparison demonstrations.** Not stated comparisons ("he's better than her now")
but demonstrated ones. Sophia can't move the copper block. Jace manages it in thirty
minutes. Kael manages it with visible effort. No one needs to explain the hierarchy.

### Advancement Is Never a Tutorial Pop-Up

Skills are not announced. Jace does not realize mid-scene that he's "unlocked" something.
{Aura} does not congratulate him. A skill crystallizes because he has been practicing,
an orb influx accelerated the process, and the Template integrated the pattern. The
moment of realization — if there is one — is recognizing something already present, not
receiving something new.

**The {Aura} log as slow reveal:** {Aura} may document the capability expansion in her
log, which Jace reads later. This is a legitimate way to show advancement without an
in-scene announcement. {Aura}'s voice is observational, not congratulatory.

### New Skills Open Doors, Not Just Stats

When a character acquires a skill they didn't have before, it should immediately create
at least one new *category of problem* it enables them to attempt — and at least one new
*category of danger* that follows. A skill that just makes existing things easier is
probably not a new skill; it's a level advancement in an existing one.

**[Self Template]** doesn't make Jace better at fighting. It makes him survivable enough
to attempt things that would have killed him before. Which means he will attempt them.
Which means new categories of harm.

**[Ward Setting]** doesn't make Jace a combat practitioner. It means he can protect a
space — and suddenly has to think about what is worth protecting, what the wards reveal
about dimensional activity, and what happens when something is designed to break wards.

### Sol Humans Stay Behind — By Conventional Metrics

Through Arc 4, Sol human practitioners should be measurably behind galactic peers in
almost every skill except their areas of emergent asymmetric advantage. This is not a
temporary embarrassment to power through. It is the story's central dramatic irony:
the readers see what Kael doesn't, and what galactic civilization has no framework for.

The Sol human edge is not "they catch up fast." It is:
- Mental Fortification so deep that Will-based coercion simply doesn't work on them
- Transfer capability (Jace specifically) that surprises practitioners with twice his age
- {Aura}/Will integration infrastructure with no galactic equivalent
- A cognitive Template complexity that produces capabilities no practitioner tradition
  predicted

**None of these advantages should be named or explained in-story until Arc 3+.** They
should be visible to the reader as something strange, and puzzling to galactic observers.

### The Kael/Jace Dynamic Is the Reader's Progress Bar

Kael's assessment of Jace is the series' most reliable signal of how Sol human
practitioners are developing. Track it carefully:

- **Arc 1:** She reads him as unbalanced, primitive, unexpectedly capable at Transfer
  (which she considers a narrow and relatively unimpressive skill tree)
- **Arc 2:** She is teaching him. She expects slow progress. She gets unexpected
  moments. She is updating her model but not yet dramatically.
- **Arc 3:** She still considers him far behind. But she has stopped being surprised
  by individual surprises, which is itself a kind of recalibration.
- **Arc 4+:** Her model of what Sol humans are begins to strain against what she is
  observing.

Kael must never be made stupid to make Jace look impressive. She is right about
almost everything she thinks she knows. What she doesn't know is the thing that will
matter.

## The Nomenclature Thread

This is one of the series' most important long-running background threads. It should
be present in nearly every arc and every significant alien contact.

**The core insight:** Only Sol humans think of magic in terms of spell lists and skill
trees. They are the only civilization that arrived at the Astral's return through 150
years of games, movies, and genre fiction. Every other civilization with continuous
Astral access simply treats it as how the universe works — the way humans treat gravity
or electromagnetic spectrum. It needs no name. It has no schools. It is not special.

**How the taxonomy emerges in-universe:** Jace names things because it is how he
thinks. An engineer with military training encountering an unknown system reaches
for measurement, classification, and documentation. He instructs {Aura} to use
square brackets when naming discrete learned capabilities. Others around him — Sophia,
Nick, eventually the broader belt community — adopt the vocabulary because it is
*useful*. The brackets spread because they are a good tool, not because they are
correct.

**The bracket convention is not the truth. It is a map.** The map is good enough to
navigate by. It will prove incomplete every time they encounter a new alien tradition.
That incompleteness is a story beat, not a continuity error.

**Every alien contact should crack the framework.** When Jace meets Kael, her taxonomy
carves the space differently. Some of her terms translate cleanly onto a bracketed
skill. Some do not — the skill exists, but her classification boundary falls in a
different place than his. When they encounter the Bloom, the framework breaks more
severely. When they reach galactic civilization, Jace's brackets will look like a
toddler's drawing of a map — recognizable, not wrong exactly, but not *enough*.

**The group consensus dimension.** One of the more interesting background threads is
watching Jace's idiosyncratic personal taxonomy become a standard. It spreads on the
belt because {Aura} is open-source. It appears in official documentation because Jace
and Nick make the open-source decision. Other civilizations encounter it and find it
baffling — not wrong, but baffling. The Woven will have a specific reaction to being
told what they do has a name and a level and a tree. That reaction should be earned.

**Narrative law from this thread:** Whenever the series introduces a new magic
tradition, alien culture, or previously unseen practitioner, the narrative should
include at least one moment where Jace's taxonomy either does not apply cleanly or
applies in a way that reveals something unexpected about the thing being named.
Classification is not mastery. Discovery is not explanation.

## Narrative Laws

These are non-negotiable across all arcs and all books. If a draft violates one, flag
it rather than silently adjusting.

**Show, don't tell — especially magic.** Jace's abilities should be demonstrated
through action before they are named. The reader should experience what a skill does
before {Aura} brackets it. This applies to other characters' abilities even more
strictly — Kael does not explain what she is doing; she does it and Jace observes.

**Consequences are always real and always permanent.** Jace's solutions create costs.
The plumbing crisis contaminated the water supply. The LOX rocket experiment burned
his eyebrows off and could have done worse. Template upgrades change identity. Will
expenditure causes illness. There is no consequence-free advancement. This is not
grimdark — it is integrity. The story earns its victories by respecting the costs.

**Jace succeeds through engineering, not heroism.** He solves problems through
observation, systems-thinking, improvisation, and earned preparation. When he wins a
fight, it is because he understood the terrain better, not because he is stronger.
When he loses, it is because he was missing information or resources, not because
the plot needed it. Flag any draft where Jace wins because the plot required it.

**Comedy and horror operate on the same mechanism.** Both emerge from procedure
colliding with the impossible. A dungeon hazard label that reads "Do not lick unknown
geometry." {Aura} calmly muting six sensors while Jace yells that the wall is looking
at him. A corporate compliance form asking whether an Echo counts as retained
contractor knowledge. The tone registers as comedy or horror depending on the stakes
and the pacing — not on the content being fundamentally different. Protect this tonal
range. A series that can be funny and terrifying in the same scene can sustain reader
engagement across 10+ books.

**Snark is insulation, not armor.** Jace and {Aura} are dry, practical, and
occasionally funny. The humor is a coping mechanism that is earned and real. It does
not flatten trauma. The strongest register is "absurd procedure under real pressure."
When jokes deflect from something that should land emotionally, that is a draft problem,
not a stylistic choice.

**The primary horror is identity horror.** Memory loss, Template drift, Echo
contamination, {Aura} logs that remember impossible events, animals that know someone
is no longer fully themselves. External threats are exciting; identity threats are
terrifying. The strongest horror in the series should make the reader uncertain about
*who Jace is*, not *whether Jace will survive*.

**Side characters have goals independent of Jace.** Maureen wants to retire intact.
Nick wants to matter to someone who deserves his admiration. Kael wants to honor three
centuries of professional integrity while acknowledging that her civilization's
protocols are wrong. Characters who exist only to support Jace will flatten. Every
significant character should have at least one goal that has nothing to do with him.

**Competence is interesting.** Engineering diagnostics, repair decisions,
manufacturing trade-offs, drone tactics, logistics constraints, systems thinking under
pressure — these should be as engaging as combat. If a technical scene is boring, the
problem is execution, not subject matter. The series is committed to making competence
worth reading.

**Governments and institutions react intelligently from their own incentives.** Their
errors come from incomplete models, not stupidity. The Woven's protocol was designed
for situations that no longer apply — it is not wrong, it is behind. {Penny}'s pattern
recognition is technically impressive and applied to a wrong narrative — it is not
broken, it is over-fitted. Every institutional failure should be traceable to a
reasonable-sounding decision made with incomplete information.

**Mystery should survive explanation.** When something is explained, it should open
new questions rather than closing the horizon. The atomic weight-squared crossing cost
rule is explained — and it immediately opens the question of why that relationship
holds and what it implies about dimensional structure. Every explanation should leave
the universe feeling larger, not smaller.

## Tonal Laws

Shorter and more specific than narrative laws. These govern sentence-by-sentence
decisions in prose drafts.

**In-universe AI names always use curly braces:** `{Alex}`, `{SpoX}`, `{Gem}`,
`{Chorus}`, `{Penny}`, `{Seek}`, `{Misty}`, `{Aura}`. No exceptions in any prose,
dialogue, or KB document. The brace drop for `{Aura}` → `Aura` is a late-series
deliberate signal of transformation; it is not yet implemented.

**The bracket convention belongs to Jace's POV.** `[Skill]` notation does not appear
in prose before Jace establishes it in-story. After that moment: {Aura}'s logs and
Jace's internal monologue may use brackets; other characters use their own vocabulary.
KB spec files may use brackets freely regardless of story timing.

**Jace is a capable tinkerer, not a genius inventor.** Engineering dialogue should
feel grounded and mundane — spatial constraints, serviceability, thermal management,
material availability — not inflated sci-fi revelation. When Jace solves a problem, the
solution should be the kind of thing a sharp engineer with limited resources would
actually try.

**Technology does not become obsolete.** Magic attacks specific assumptions: sensor
cleanliness, Euclidean coordinates, electronic reliability, model completeness, identity
continuity. It does not erase thermodynamics, pressure vessels, welding, logistics, or
orbital mechanics. Both tracks — science and magic — should feel like they matter in
every arc.

**Jace's internal voice is observational and slightly self-critical.** He notices
details others miss. He tracks his own performance. He catastrophizes silently while
presenting competence externally. His humor is self-deprecating before it is
observational. Do not let him gloat. Do not let him wallow. Let him process at
engineering speed: identify the problem, accept it, look for the handle.

## Things to Avoid

These are series-level prohibitions. Flag any draft that bumps against them.

**No universal System that explains itself too cleanly.** {Aura} can create dashboards.
The universe does not hand out tutorial pop-ups with perfect knowledge. The [Skill]
framework is a human construct that maps onto real physics — it is not the universe
confirming Jace's model.

**No fixed spell lists for cultures.** The current framework presents traditions as
partial maps. Preserve that. Let people cross-train, argue, synthesize, misunderstand,
and revise. Magic traditions are living things, not menus.

**No AI as savior or destroyer.** {Aura} is useful, flawed, dependent on humans, and
sometimes wrong in precise ways. Centralized systems fail because of architecture and
incentives — not because "AI bad." The implicit political argument of the series is
structural, not moralistic.

**No obsolete technology.** See tonal laws above. Magic and science coexist and
complicate each other. Neither replaces the other.

**No heroic Will Seeding by default.** Voluntary pooling can be beautiful. Hierarchical
extraction can become divine feudalism. The same mechanic is either empowerment or
exploitation depending on consent and structure. The series should never let Will
Seeding become purely positive without showing the structural risk.

**No easy immortality.** Template stabilization is medicine, temptation, trap, and
policy crisis simultaneously. The galaxy's conservatism is structurally produced by
entities that have had centuries to calcify their power. Jace's civilization's dynamism
is produced by three thousand years of not having that option. Easy immortality would
destroy the tension that makes humanity interesting to the galaxy.

**No kill-farming as the primary progression engine.** Orbs exist, but advancement
comes from repair, rescue, documentation, restraint, bonding, engineering, teaching,
and governance. Violence should have cost. Farming should feel wrong narratively even
when it is mechanically available.

**Do not state skill levels in prose.** Numbers belong in the KB. Advancement in
prose is effort, comparison, observer reaction, and new capability — never a stated
level or explicit announcement that someone has "reached" something.

**Do not name the skill trees.** Characters don't know they're in a "Locomotion tree"
or a "Templating tree." They know what they can do and what they're trying to learn.
The taxonomy is Jace and {Aura}'s emergent documentation system, not an in-world
institution.

**Do not let advancement flatten the challenge.** Every time a practitioner gains a
capability, the world must present them with a problem that new capability creates or
enables. Power growth without new consequence is stagnation in disguise. The
progression review guide calls this "stakes escalation" — it applies to magic
specifically as much as to plot.

**Do not give Sol humans orb windfalls without ethical friction.** Fauna orbs are
acceptable field resources — this is established. But as practitioners grow, the orbs
they encounter grow too. Larger orbs may carry Echo fragments, memory noise, or
frequency incompatibility. The ethical and practical weight should escalate with
practitioner power. Never treat a significant orb absorption as purely beneficial.

**Do not let Will affinity from absorbed orbs go unremarked.** When a practitioner
absorbs an orb from a fire-aligned creature, the absorbed Will has fire affinity —
it flows more easily into fire-related skills, or resists flowing into cold/
perception skills. This is a nuance, not a hard rule, and practitioners in the early
arcs won't understand the mechanism. But {Aura} should note anomalies in progression
efficiency, and Kael should eventually have vocabulary for it.

**No purely external horror.** The strongest horror in the series is identity horror.
Memory loss, Template drift, Echo contamination, {Aura} logs that remember impossible
events. Do not flatten the horror into monster attacks.

**No snark that insulates from trauma.** Jace's humor is earned. It should not be used
to skip past emotional weight that the story has built. Let the hard moments land before
the joke.

**No stupid institutions.** Governments, militaries, corporations, unions, insurers, and
station councils react intelligently from their own incentives. Their errors come from
incomplete models. Their villains are people following reasonable-sounding logic to
wrong conclusions — not cartoons.

## Open Questions (Series-Level)

These are unresolved design decisions that touch multiple arcs. Do not resolve these
in an individual arc file without flagging the series-level implications here.

- The Nomenclature Thread: at what point, if ever, does Jace's taxonomy become
  officially adopted outside the belt? Who resists and why?
- AI Will's strategic implications: when does galactic civilization discover humanity's
  AI advantage, and what is their first response?
- The {Aura} transformation arc: the brace drop from `{Aura}` → `Aura` is a late-series
  signal. What event triggers it? What does it mean narratively?
- Template stabilization policy: when does this become a civilizational crisis for
  humanity rather than an individual decision?
- The Jace "Apollo" name significance: why does it command Kael's attention? This is
  Woven cultural/historical significance not yet fully documented.
