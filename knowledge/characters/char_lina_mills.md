# Lina Mills

## Summary

Lina runs day-to-day operations at the Mars dog breeder facility in the U.S. settlement. She is the person Jace deals with during his hard-months visits — the handler, the explainer, the one who stops charging him entry fees after his third week. She is warm and direct in the way that people who work with animals tend to be: she has limited patience for indirection and no patience for cruelty, and she expresses both efficiently.

She and Jace develop a real warmth across the weeks of his visits. It doesn't tip into romance — not quite, and not for lack of possibility. She is leaving. Her parents decided before Jace's discharge that they were done with Mars, and she is going with them. The rotator that takes people back to Earth will carry forty people this cycle, twice the usual number. Lina's family is in that forty.

She is one of the few people during the hard months who simply is glad to see Jace when he shows up — no agenda, no pity, no management. She just is. That is not a small thing when everything else is hard.

---

## Her Role at the Facility

Lina manages daily animal care, client relations, waiting lists, and visitor access. She knows the Mars dog breeding program thoroughly — she can walk you through the herding × pug cross, explain the skeletal-density targets, predict which puppy in a litter will have the strongest temperament. She is not the scientist; she is the person who turns thirty years of science into daily care, which is the harder job.

She notices Jace's attachment to the boy puppy before he acknowledges it himself. She doesn't name it. She just stops charging him the entry fee.

When Origin Industries confirms the shipping schedule — the mother and female puppy to Fortuna, the boy puppy to be sold separately on Mars — she tells Jace directly, and she also tells him her concern:

> Lina: "He needs his mother longer. I've pushed back on the timeline with Origin. They don't care — breeding stock has a launch window and that's that. He'll be okay. Probably. But separating him early isn't ideal."
>
> Jace: "How early?"
>
> Lina: "Four to six weeks before I'd want. He'll bond to whoever takes him. It just means whoever that is needs to be someone steady. Someone consistent."

She looks at Jace when she says "someone steady." She doesn't say anything else.

## The Yoga Classes (Pivotal Scene)

At some point during the recurring dog-breeder visits, Lina invites Jace to her low-gravity yoga class. The class is held in a lower-pressure training dome in the civilian section of Terminus — a dome that runs at slightly reduced gravity (approximately 0.2g rather than Mars's standard 0.38g). The reduced gravity enables poses and flows that would be impossible at Earth-normal.

Jace, who has a rigid and disciplined exercise practice, has never done yoga. He is skeptical. He goes because Lina asks and he doesn't have many places to be.

### What Happens in the Class

The class is small — eight to ten people, mostly civilian residents who've been on Mars long enough to have found the lower-g dome a relief. The instructor is a Martian-born woman in her thirties who moves like she was designed for this gravity.

Jace is bad at yoga. He is strong and inflexible and his body has been trained for force rather than flow. He follows along at the back of the class, quietly embarrassed by how difficult stillness is. Lina, beside him, is good at this — she has been practicing for a year.

The pivotal moment comes during a long held pose near the end of the session — something that requires stillness, extension, and breath. The lower-g environment means the pose can be held for much longer than it could be at higher gravity, and the instructor keeps the class in it for what feels to Jace like too long.

He is holding the pose, breathing slowly, and his hands are extended — palms forward, fingertips toward the far wall.

And he feels something.

It is not pain. It is not numbness. It is a faint warmth — or not exactly warmth, something more like pressure, like a current — at the tips of his fingers. Persistent. Steady. He looks at his hands. They look normal. The feeling continues.

He doesn't say anything during the class. Afterward, walking back through the dome corridor with Lina:

> Jace: "Does that happen a lot? The... I don't know. Did you feel anything in your hands during the last hold?"
>
> Lina: [looks at him curiously] "Feel anything like what?"
>
> Jace: "Like... there was something there. At my fingertips. Like a current."
>
> Lina: [pause] "Some people say they feel energy moving in low-g yoga. I've read about it. Usually people who do it for a while. You're new."
>
> Jace: "I'm probably just tired."
>
> Lina: "Maybe."

She says it the way people say *maybe* when they mean *not necessarily.*

### What This Scene Is

This is the first time Jace's own potential magical sensitivity manifests. He doesn't have a frame for it. He writes it off as tiredness and the strange physical conditions of the low-g dome. But the reader — who has seen the fractal powder in the sewer, the duck attack, the drone screen glitch — is watching something larger arrive quietly.

The fact that it happens during stillness, in low gravity, with Lina present, is important: **magic is not coming to Jace when he is in control. It is coming to him when he is still.**

This pattern will persist throughout the series.

## Her Departure

Lina leaves Mars approximately two to three weeks before the Artemis smuggling sprint. By the time Jace assembles the list and meets the Rat, she is in transit back to Earth. They exchange contact details. The comms situation makes it uncertain whether messages will go through consistently. They part warmly, without ceremony — Lina is not the kind of person who makes goodbyes larger than they are.

After her departure, Jace visits the dog facility less frequently. The boy puppy is still there. The handler (TBD character) lets Jace come in without a fee in Lina's memory.

## Voice and Manner

- Direct. Has dealt with enough indirection from corporate handlers and anxious dog buyers to have no patience for it.
- Warm in a matter-of-fact way — she doesn't perform warmth; she just has it.
- Good at reading what people aren't saying. She knew Jace was discharged before he told her, and she never asked him to confirm it.
- Physically graceful in low gravity (the yoga practice). Slightly ungainly at Earth-normal or higher; she has Mars-adapted her body over three years.
- Always has animal hair on her clothes. Does not care.
- Has a laugh that is slightly too loud for the situation. She finds this about herself and does nothing about it.
- Writes letters. Actual letters — she has a small stock of paper that she uses for people who matter. If she writes Jace after her departure, it will be on paper, and if the comms situation is what it is, it may arrive months late.

---

## Story Function

**Connects the dog breeder visits to a human relationship.** Without Lina, Jace's time at the facility is a man sitting with animals in depression. With Lina, it is a friendship during his loneliest stretch — which makes the absence of her departure a real loss.

**Seeds the separation-early concern.** Her explicit worry about the puppy being separated before he should be makes Jace's Origin negotiation ask — *can I take him with me?* — partly an act of honoring Lina's concern as well as his own attachment.

**Delivers the magic foreshadowing.** The yoga scene is where Jace first feels something that isn't normal. Lina's response (*maybe*) leaves it unresolved in the right way.

**Embodies the departure wave.** Her family leaving on the overloaded rotator makes the "people are leaving Mars" detail personal rather than statistical.

**A warmth that doesn't tip over.** Not every relationship in Jace's life becomes a love story. This one stays in the almost-space. That is its own kind of truth about frontier life.
