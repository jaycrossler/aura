# Jace Apollo Grant

## Summary

Jace Apollo Grant is a recently-discharged United States Air Force officer on Mars whose career and identity collapse in the opening chapters of Book One. He arrived on Mars excited, decorated, and certain of his future — and within weeks was diagnosed with a degenerative bone disease, stripped of his commission, and left with a million-credit debt and no way home. After scraping by on menial labor, he signs a ten-year contract as an animal handler aboard a Mars-to-Belt livestock ferry. He is the reader's POV into a solar system on the edge of transformation.

He is not yet the protagonist his story will require. He is competent, loyal, and brave in narrow military senses, but rigid, sheltered, and naive about almost everything outside that frame. He is a pretty boy who doesn't think of himself that way, an obsessive nerd who masks it with a competent-officer presentation, and a young man whose deepest need is to be needed. He doesn't know any of this about himself yet. The story will break and remake him.

## Background

Born in Virginia to a comfortable middle-class family — Dawn and Chris Grant, both retired by story start, both formerly working for various government and public-service institutions in suburban Virginia. The Grant family is not wealthy but is solidly stable, and Jace grew up in the kind of environment that constantly pushed excellence — academic achievement, athletic discipline, the polite ambition of professional-class American Virginia. He did well. He was raised to.

Showed early aptitude for systems thinking and mathematics; recruited into Air Force ROTC in college on a scholarship that did not fully cover tuition, leaving him with substantial student debt by graduation. The plan was always clear: serve twenty years, have the military pay it off. He commissioned as a second lieutenant and specialized in drone command and control.

Selected for the Mars Detachment of the U.S. Air Force after a competitive process. The Mars assignment is prestigious, technically demanding, and lucrative. The cybernetic HUD implant — a current-generation advanced military unit with confidential upgrades — is provided as part of his role. He volunteers for the surgery eagerly.

He spent the four-month voyage to Mars aboard the *Pinta*, recovering from the implant surgery (which he had only a short time before departure) and cramming drone operations courses. The ship was crowded, dirty, and uncomfortable — 150 people packed into a vessel not designed for pleasure. He barely noticed. He was preparing.

By the time he steps onto Mars, the HUD has been active for several months but he is still genuinely new to it. He has been falling deeply in love with the device — part tutor, part web interface, part game system, the complete amplification of his inner geek — and he has been spending money on it that he does not have. Close to a year's wages on upgrades, premium app subscriptions, exclusive courseware, and entertainment. He plans to pay it all off through his Mars tour bonuses. He has already started planning the next-generation upgrade for when he makes Captain.

## Arrival on Mars

Jace arrives on Mars at peak golden-boy confidence — tall, blond, fit, decorated, and certain. The low gravity is disorienting; he acclimates awkwardly and with military discipline.

His sponsor, Lt. Rich Cullivan, picks him up at the shuttle pad and drives him through the U.S. Mars settlement. Through Rich, Jace gets his first real look at Mars: the dust-red sky, the dome architecture, the Mars-born population taller and lankier than the Earth-born he is used to, the constant ventilation hum, the surprising hunger for plants. During the drive, Rich asks how the new HUD is treating him; Jace lights up about it, mentions casually how much he has spent on upgrades, and lays out his "I'll just pay for the next model when I make Captain" fiscal plan with bright confidence. Rich shakes his head. Jace doesn't notice.

At the base, other soldiers salute him. He is treated well. He tours the drone control center — a vast, humming facility that flies surveillance and security drones across the entire Martian surface — and feels the genuine thrill of having arrived somewhere that matters. During the tour, he notices through a secure-area window a young Chinese-looking woman and a nondescript man in a meeting with the base commander. He is told they are Intel liaisons he will meet in due course. He files this away.

That evening, he hits the gym in his quarters wing for his standard PT routine. He is the strong one in the room — Earth-density bones, full Earth-trained musculature, in a population of Mars-adjusted bodies that are taller and lighter. He registers, with quiet pride, that several people are watching him. A young woman exercising nearby smiles at him. He smiles back. The exchange is forty seconds of nothing and confirms everything he has been raised to believe about his place in any room he walks into.

Back in his quarters, he calls family. His parents — Dawn and Chris in Virginia — are happy and proud, telling him he's "finally paying off that college debt." His younger sister (middle name Athena) is still at university; she congratulates him too. He tries to reach his older sister (middle name Artemis), who is stationed at Odysseus Station in the Jupiter trojans — but the connection fails, which is unusual. He assumes it's latency. It isn't just latency.

He sleeps well. The medical checkout is in the morning.

## The Discharge

The medical checkout finds a degenerative bone disease — a recently-classified condition, genetic in origin, with no known cure. The Mars Detachment's current-generation imaging suite resolves diagnostic signatures that older Earth-side equipment misses; the disease produces small structural anomalies and cavity formations in the bone matrix that the imaging team informally calls "bubbles." The condition causes progressive brittleness over decades.

The doctor is clear: on Mars, with daily mandatory exercise and the lower gravitational load, Jace will be functionally fine for a long time — possibly decades. *"You can live a long, healthy life on Mars."* But returning to Earth, where bone load is more than twice what it is here, would accelerate the disease catastrophically. Five to ten years on Earth and his bones would begin failing in ways no treatment could fix.

He is honorably discharged on Mars.

The terms: two years of twenty served means only 10% of his college debt is covered. He owes approximately **one million credits**. The medical stipend and severance together do not cover this. No return cycler berth is provided — that is a benefit for soldiers who complete their tours. He has thirty days to vacate base housing.

The doctor also tells him she has filed screening requests for both his sisters — the disease has a genetic component. Athena's screening, run by her university health service, came back clean. The good news of the day. The request to Odysseus Station for Artemis has been filed but is going slowly: *"The signal to Odysseus has been bad lately."* Both Jace and the doctor know what they are not saying.

The HUD is a further wound. It is a confidential military-grade unit with upgrades that cannot be left in civilian hands. The Air Force informs him it will be deactivated remotely. He watches the overlay go dark during the discharge meeting. A civilian commercial license can be provisioned in its place — functional, but stripped of the military Aura, the security clearances, and the configurations he has spent months building. He says nothing. He says "copy" when they ask if he understands.

He walks out. He has no job, no passage home, a million-credit debt, a degenerative disease that turns going home into a slow medical death sentence, and an inert lump of metal in his head where a year's wages of upgrades used to live.

## The Hard Months

Jace spends the next weeks on the margins of the U.S. Mars settlement, taking whatever work he can find. Cleaning sewers. Fixing broken gear. Running errands for the civilian contractors who now barely notice him. The settlement that saluted him two months ago treats him as invisible. He loses some weight. His daily exercise drops from 2-3 hours to whatever he can fit between gigs. He starts spending evenings at a working-class bar near the launch facility — *The Long Burn* (working name) — where he eats cheap food, listens to dock workers and ex-military argue about the latest belt rumors, and slowly begins to register that something is genuinely wrong out there. Deaths at Odysseus. Quarantine speculation. Sabotage theories. A merchant whose Origin shipments are not arriving and whose business is failing.

His parents offer to sell their house in Virginia to help. He tells them not to. Artemis — when he finally reaches her, the connection patchy and degraded — hears the situation and immediately offers her savings: 50,000 credits. She also has a request. She needs some specific items purchased on Mars and delivered to a contact of hers at the launch facility. She doesn't explain exactly what or why. Jace, low on options and trusting his sister, agrees.

The items are unusual. He doesn't ask too many questions. The contact at the launch facility — a small, ratty-looking man, dark-haired and eyebrowed, with bad teeth and an easy smile, whom Jace later learns the locals call **the Rat** — adds the package to an outbound shipment without comment. In exchange: money, and a promise of further work.

## The Smuggler and the Job

Over the following weeks, the Rat becomes an unlikely lifeline. He is more than the launch-facility loader he appears to be — he is a fixture across the dock, the bar, and the fabrication district, an opportunistic small-time fixer who likes to think of himself as a Robin Hood and takes a 20% cut on every contract he facilitates. He plays small to stay invisible. He sees Jace as useful and Jace's situation as Artemis's debt to repay.

When an emergency dropout opens on an upcoming asteroid run — a contracted animal handler who abruptly broke his agreement and left Mars, one of many incidents tied to the growing unease around belt strife — the Rat flags Jace for the slot. The certification process for animal handling is normally months. The Rat compresses it to weeks through a combination of bribery, unofficial documentation, and genuine instruction. Jace learns the basics: feeding, monitoring, cleaning, canister operations, veterinary first response. He is not good at it yet. He is good enough.

During the certification, on a shift cleaning out animal pens at the Mars livestock farm, Jace meets a working-breed mother dog and her recent litter. He learns the mother and one female puppy are scheduled for the next Origin shipment to Fortuna Station as breeding stock; the boy puppy is being sold on Mars. Jace, hollowed by his own circumstances, finds the coming separation unbearable. He names the puppy **Cerberus** on the walk back from the pen, half-jokingly continuing the Greek-name pattern of his family. He does not yet plan to do anything about it. He does not yet have a plan to do anything about anything.

During the same training period, he encounters the Chinese-looking woman from the base. She is now dressed as a civilian farmer. When he approaches her with recognition, she cuts him off with a flat look: *Don't.* He doesn't. But she finds him later.

## The Origin Negotiation

Jace's interview-and-negotiation with Origin Industries is a contract conversation he loses without understanding he is losing it. Without his HUD active, he has to use a clunky civilian tablet to research Origin's history and standard rates beforehand; during the meeting itself he is operating without Aura assistance, mentally calculating slowly while a smooth Origin representative frames the conversation around Jace's deficiencies (too tall, eats too much, limited animal experience) rather than Origin's desperation. The compensation offer is structured to back-load almost all of the meaningful payment to the end of a ten-year contract.

Scrambling for any small leverage he can think of, Jace remembers the puppy. He asks if Origin can include Cerberus on the manifest with him. The Origin rep visibly weighs the request, pretends to check, and agrees. Jace closes the deal with the dog secured.

What Jace does not see — but the reader does, in a brief tag scene — is that the Origin rep just successfully negotiated the contract roughly 200,000 credits below what Jace could have gotten if he had pushed harder. The rep's boss congratulates him and laughs: *"That's probably the most expensive dog in human history."*

Jace walks out feeling like he held his ground. Cerberus is on the manifest. The reader knows what he doesn't.

## The Intel Deal

The woman from the gym-base meeting room turns out to be Mei. Her handler — the nondescript man — introduces himself as Mr. Sylvester. They know more about Jace than is comfortable. Sylvester is the head of U.S. military intelligence at the Mars Detachment, and he is sympathetic in a way that feels practiced but is not entirely false. He understands Jace's situation. He has a solution.

The deal: Jace provides occasional assistance to Mei during the belt posting — keeping her cover intact, passing information if asked, being available if she needs backup. In return: his HUD is reactivated under a "civilian contractor" designation (the military-grade firmware, partially restored — he loses access to the military Aura and the secure circuits, but regains his apps, his games, his courseware, his media library, and the day-to-day enhancement he has come to depend on), and he receives supplemental payments credited against his debt.

The HUD piece is what sells it. Jace is not making a calculated intelligence-asset decision; he is making a decision to be himself again. His own augmented self has been gone for weeks, and he has felt the absence in every menial gig and every botched negotiation. The Sylvester offer gives him back the version of himself he believes in.

He agrees. He is also genuinely inclined to help. He believes in cooperation. He has not yet learned that "be helpful to your country's intelligence service" can mean very different things in different contexts. He will later come to regret this.

## Departure

Jace boards the *Falcon* — the nuclear ferry affectionately called "Barnyard 1" or the Chicken Coop by its crew — bound for Fortuna Station. He has a ten-year contract, a reactivated HUD running in civilian-contractor mode, a barely-sufficient animal handling certification, a puppy named after the three-headed guardian of the underworld, and one conversation with his family before the communications lag makes real-time calls impossible.

He does not know, as he boards, that the anomalies around the belt are not sabotage. He does not know that Mei suspects as much. He does not know that his older sister Artemis, at the farthest station in the human solar system, has already seen things she cannot explain.

## Personality

**Surface:** Disciplined. Punctual. Quietly proud. Dry sense of humor that he uses defensively. Polite to strangers, warm to those he trusts. Believes in hierarchy, competence, and following the chain of command. Not great with people in unfamiliar social registers — formal contexts feel rehearsed; casual contexts feel awkward; his comfort zone is the structured-team environment of military duty.

**Underneath:** Quietly afraid that without his uniform he is no one. Quietly resentful of the privilege of those born with money. Quietly idealistic — believes the project of human spaceflight is the most important thing humanity is doing. Obsessively nerdy about his interests (HUD configurations, drone systems, gaming, courseware) in a way he masks with the competent-officer surface.

**Needs to be needed.** This is the truest thing about him and the thing he is least aware of. He measures himself by being the person other people rely on. Take that role away — through the discharge, through the loss of his unit, through his civilian invisibility — and he doesn't know how to value himself. The Sylvester offer works partly because it makes him useful again. The Cerberus moment works partly because the puppy needs him.

**Shy, especially about his looks.** He is conventionally attractive in ways he genuinely does not register. Compliments slide past him; flirtation has to be very direct before he sees it. He grew up with the inner self-image of an awkward nerd that being tall and blond and fit has overwritten on the outside, and the gap between his self-image and how others see him is large.

**Contradictions:** Trained to make decisions under pressure but uncomfortable with ambiguity. Believes in cooperation but distrusts politicians and corporations. Tells himself he is rational but is in fact a person who has chosen to believe in things — country, service, the Air Force — and will need to choose new things when those fail him.

## Voice and Mannerisms

- **Speech patterns:** Concise, slightly formal, military-cadenced. Uses callsigns and acronyms unconsciously. Reverts to plainer speech when emotional.
- **Verbal tics:** Says "copy" instead of "okay" when concentrating. Says "negative" instead of "no" when stressed. Asks "how can I help?" reflexively.
- **Body language:** Stands like an officer. Has trouble slouching. Habitually checks angles and exits when entering a room.
- **Under stress:** Becomes more formal, more precise, more controlled. The crack only shows in private.
- **With his Aura:** Talks aloud to it as if to a colleague, not as if to a tool. This will become important.
- **With Cerberus:** Plain, unguarded, the version of himself he doesn't show to people. The dog gets the truest Jace.

## Goals and Motivations

- **Surface goal:** Pay off his debts. Survive his ten-year contract. Maybe go home — or at least achieve the genetic conditioning that would make Earth survivable again.
- **True goal:** Find a place where he matters again. Restore the sense of purpose he lost when the uniform came off.
- **Need:** Learn to define himself by relationships, not roles. Learn that the universe is larger than his categories. Learn that being needed by something does not have to mean being used by it. Magic will demand all of this.
