# [TBD] — The Aura Engineer

## Summary

The Aura engineer is the person who builds, distributes, and maintains the personal Aura AI instances that proliferate on Fortuna Station and across the belt. Aura is not a corporate product. It is a **cobbled-together stack** — open-source language models from the past hundred years, plugin frameworks, agent runtimes, fine-tuning utilities, voice and vision modules, and personal-history integration tools, all wired together into something that runs locally on consumer-grade implant hardware. The engineer is the person who wires it together for individual users in exchange for trade goods.

He (placeholder) operates in the gray market. There is no station policy explicitly authorizing what he does, but no one stops him because everyone benefits. By Book One's end, his services are functionally infrastructure. By Book Three, he is one of the most important people on the station — and he resents it.

## What "Aura" Actually Is

Aura is **not a single AI**. It is a curated stack with about a hundred and fifty interlocking components, each of which can be swapped, updated, or replaced. The stack includes:
- A local-inference language model core (rotating set; the engineer keeps three or four current options).
- Personal-history memory layers (the user's own data, episodically stored and indexed).
- Sensor fusion modules (HUD cameras, biometrics, environmental sensors).
- A federation protocol for talking to other Auras across the belt comms net.
- Plugin slots for specialized capabilities (engineering, medical, navigation, magical-anomaly detection — the last one emerges over the course of the series).

The stack is open. The engineer's value is in **integration and tuning**. Anyone could in principle build an Aura. In practice, almost no one does, because doing it correctly takes years of accumulated craft and access to a curated upstream of components that are mostly extinct, partially deprecated, or hidden in old archives.

## How He Got Here

(Working backstory — open to revision.)

The engineer was originally an open-source AI infrastructure developer in the late 2080s and early 2090s, when the major model labs collapsed under regulatory and economic pressure and a small underground community kept the tools alive. He emigrated to the belt after a falling out with a corporate employer (specifics TBD — possibly an IP dispute, possibly an ethical objection, possibly both).

He brought with him a personal archive: source code, model weights, documentation, training data, and the social-trust connections to a distributed network of similar archivists. The archive is one of the most valuable single objects in the belt and the engineer guards it with quiet seriousness. Several factions have tried to obtain it; he has handled each attempt without violence and without giving up the archive.

He builds Auras for individuals because that is how he funds the larger work — keeping the archive alive, contributing to the federated belt-comms infrastructure, training new junior maintainers (he has two apprentices by the middle of Book Two).

## How Jace Meets Him

Jace's gaming-friend-best-friend (`/characters/char_jace_best_friend.md`) introduces Jace to the engineer shortly after arrival on Fortuna. The HUD that the U.S. Air Force gave Jace, originally reactivated through the deal with Mei and Sylvester, is hardware-capable but software-locked. The engineer can unlock it and install Aura.

The transaction takes the form of trade goods (Jace pays partly in things he brought up — small luxuries, specific minerals, some debt-paper) plus a service exchange — Jace agrees to bring back specific samples or sensor readings from his upcoming mining expeditions. This trade-and-favor pattern is how the engineer's whole economy works.

Installing Aura on Jace's HUD takes weeks (the install is partly performed in transit, then completed and tuned on the station). The result, by the time it stabilizes, is one of the more powerful Aura instances on the station — because Jace's HUD is **military-grade hardware** and the engineer has not had previous opportunities to deploy on that level of substrate. He treats Jace's installation as a research project as much as a commission.

## What He Knows About Magic

The engineer notices the anomalies before most people. Aura instances are starting to flag patterns the engineer cannot explain — sensor readings that recur across stations, behavior that suggests the Auras themselves are detecting something the human users aren't. He is one of the first to articulate, internally, that *the system is detecting a real phenomenon, not malfunctioning*.

He does not believe in magic at the start of Book One. By the end of the series, he is the person who built the infrastructure for the Synthesis Movement to actually *function* across stations — federation protocols, anomaly databases, cross-tradition translation tooling. He becomes magic's IT department. He hates being called that, because it's accurate.

## Personality

- **Surface:** Quiet, distractible, fiddly with his hands, evasive about anything personal, generous with what he knows in his domain.
- **Underneath:** A deep, patient steward of something he considers larger than himself. Not religious, but reverent about the project of preserving and extending tools the species needs.
- **Tells:** Looks past you, not at you, when he is thinking. Goes quiet rather than confrontational when he disagrees. Will install your software for free if you can demonstrate you genuinely tried to do it yourself first.

## Voice and Mannerisms

- Speaks in fragments and references the listener has to parse. Apologizes for it sometimes.
- Refers to old programming languages and dead frameworks the way other people refer to literature.
- Uses precise technical terms with no condescension; assumes the listener will keep up or ask.
- Goes silent when running an inference; comes back as if no time had passed.
