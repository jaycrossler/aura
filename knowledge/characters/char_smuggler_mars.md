# The Rat (the Mars Launch Facility Smuggler)

## Summary

Known to most who use his services as **the Rat** (a nickname earned partly from his physical appearance and partly from the way he takes a small bite — twenty percent — out of every contract that passes through his hands), the man who facilitates Jace's belt contract is one of the most consequential minor figures in Book 1. He looks, at first glance, like another launch-facility loader: small, slightly hunched, dark-haired, ratty-toothed, dressed in working clothes that have seen better decades. He is not. He is one of the most connected informal-economy operators in the U.S. Mars settlement, a quiet network hub spanning the launch facility, the fabrication district, the dock bar at the Long Burn, and a substantial fraction of mid-level corporate staff who find informal channels useful.

He has worked for three different official employers in twenty years and has been simultaneously building a parallel business throughout. He sees himself as a Robin Hood figure — helping individual humans find their way around the systems built to control them — and takes a 20% cut of every contract he facilitates. The Robin Hood self-image is sincere. So is the cut.

He is not a bad person. He is also absolutely not someone Jace should fully trust.

## Background

His name is unknown to almost everyone who deals with him. The Rat is not a name he chose; it is one that stuck. He has worked in and around the Mars launch facility for most of his adult career, accumulating relationships, favors, and information. He is unmarried, lives modestly (modestly enough to maintain the loader-in-his-forties cover), and has a private accumulated wealth that would surprise anyone who has met him. He spends almost none of it.

Artemis Grant has used the Rat's services before. He trusts her in a specific bounded way — she follows through on her commitments, asks reasonable questions, and does not try to negotiate his cut. When she sent him a request to help her brother, he honored it. He also calculated what was in it for him.

## Role in the Story

**Receiving Artemis's package.** When Jace delivers the package, the Rat takes it without comment, pays Jace, and files him as potentially useful. He notes Jace's military bearing, the obvious recent discharge, the fragility of his finances, and the sister's introduction. All of this matters.

**Recurring presence at the bar.** The Rat is a fixture at the Long Burn (working name) — visible to the reader across multiple bar scenes, easy to miss for a reader not paying attention. He works the room quietly, brief conversations, small exchanges, the kind of network maintenance that looks like nothing if you aren't looking.

**Facilitating the Origin slot.** When the emergency dropout opens on the Origin belt run, the Rat calls in a contact, gets Jace the slot, and runs a compressed animal-handling certification. The compression involves bribery, unofficial documentation, and genuine instruction — the Rat actually knows enough about animal handling to teach the basics, which Jace registers with surprise.

**The 20% cut.** On every piece of money flowing through Jace's contract — Artemis's payment, the Origin signing bonus, the Sylvester supplemental — the Rat takes a quiet percentage. He is transparent about this with Jace once they have a working relationship. *"I take twenty percent. That's what I do. You can argue, but you'll lose."* Jace pays without arguing. The price feels fair.

## The Robin Hood Self-Image

The Rat genuinely believes that he is helping. He sees the megacorporations and the governments as institutions that exist to extract value from individual humans, and he sees himself as the friction in that machine — slowing the extraction, redirecting some of it back to the people doing the actual work. He has helped:

- Belters get supplies that would otherwise be illegally restricted.
- Mars residents get medical care or equipment outside the bureaucratic queue.
- Workers escape contracts that have become exploitative.
- Families on Mars connect with relatives in the belt when official channels failed.
- Discharged military personnel like Jace land on their feet.

He charges everyone twenty percent. He is consistent.

## Where the Self-Image Falls Short

The Rat's Robin Hood narrative has gaps. He has also helped:

- Move contraband he chose not to inspect closely.
- Smuggle items whose purpose he half-suspected and decided not to confirm.
- Open doors for clients he later regretted.
- Take cuts on transactions where the "small individuals" he was helping were not as small as he told himself.

He is not malicious. He is opportunistic. The line between the two becomes thinner as the stakes rise. As the belt situation deteriorates, the Rat's network becomes more valuable, more compromised, and more morally ambiguous. He is well-positioned to be both helpful and dangerous to Jace as Book 1 progresses and the series continues.

## Personality

**Surface:** Friendly in a low-key way. Easy to talk to. Doesn't push. Appears to be listening more than he is, which is a trick — he is listening *more* than he appears to be.

**Underneath:** Calculating constantly. Tracks debts, favors, vulnerabilities, and leverage in real time. Takes a small specific pleasure in the work itself — there is craft to being a fixer, and he respects it.

**Contradictions:** Sees himself as a friend of the small operator while operating at scales the small operator cannot conceive. Sincerely believes in his Robin Hood role while taking enough cut to be quietly wealthy. Distrusts institutions while functioning as one within his network.

## Voice and Mannerisms

- **Speech:** Quick, low-volume, slightly nasal. Uses fewer words than the people he talks to. Does not waste sentences. Has an actual sense of humor that surfaces when he is comfortable.
- **Body language:** Always slightly underweight in his clothes. Constant small movements — adjusting his collar, checking his cuffs, glancing past whoever is talking to him to scan the room.
- **With Jace:** Patient. Does not condescend. Treats Jace as an adult who is making his own decisions, even when those decisions are not great. This is more disorienting to Jace than any of his other recent humiliations.
