# Scene — Low-G Yoga with Lina (The Fingertips)

## Summary

Lina invites Jace to her low-gravity yoga class. He is skeptical and goes anyway. The class is held in a reduced-gravity sub-dome at approximately 0.2g. Jace is bad at yoga. Near the end of a long held pose, he feels something at the tips of his fingers — a faint current or pressure that he cannot explain and cannot stop feeling. He asks Lina about it afterward. She says *maybe.* He files it under tiredness.

This is the first time Jace's own magical sensitivity surfaces. He does not recognize it. The reader begins to.

## Function in the Story

- **Primary:** First hint of Jace's personal magical aptitude — magic is coming to him in stillness, in a low-g environment where his normal physical control is reduced and his body is doing something genuinely unfamiliar.
- **Secondary:** One of the few genuinely warm beats in the hard months. Jace is bad at something; Lina is good at it; he doesn't mind being bad at it in front of her. This is a character note.
- **Tertiary:** Establishes the low-g dome as a real civilian Mars infrastructure that makes later references feel grounded. Also establishes that low-g yoga is a real thing that people do.
- **Structural:** The stillness-as-portal idea — magic arriving to Jace when he is quiet and extended, not when he is fighting or running — is a pattern that will recur throughout the series.

## Setting

A small sub-dome within the civilian section of Terminus, purpose-built for training and exercise in reduced gravity. The dome runs at approximately **0.2g** — just over half Mars standard. The floor is padded and lightly tacky for grip. The ceiling is high enough to stand and extend arms fully without floating. The lighting is warm and indirect; the air pressure is slightly lower than standard.

At 0.2g, the human body feels uncannily light. Walking is a careful, slow-motion experience. Falls are slow and soft. Held yoga poses that would be unsustainable at Earth-normal can be maintained for several minutes.

The class is small: eight to ten people, almost all civilian long-term residents. The instructor is Martian-born (born on Mars, raised at 0.38g, feels 0.2g the way Earth-born people feel a warm bath). She moves like the gravity is optional.

## Beats

### Beat 1: The Invitation (~1 week before the scene)
Lina mentions the class casually, almost as an afterthought, during one of Jace's dog visits.

> Lina: "I do yoga in the low-g dome on Tuesdays. You should come. It would help your flexibility."
>
> Jace: "I don't do yoga."
>
> Lina: "I know. That's why you should come."

She doesn't push. He shows up on Tuesday.

### Beat 2: Entry and Calibration
Jace enters the dome and immediately has to recalibrate. He's been on Mars long enough that 0.38g feels normal; 0.2g feels wrong in a specific way — like the floor isn't committing to him. He takes three steps and catches himself on a wall.

Lina, beside him, walks across the space like she grew up here.

> Lina: "You'll adapt in about five minutes."
>
> Jace: "What if I don't?"
>
> Lina: "You'll adapt in about ten minutes."

The instructor welcomes him without ceremony. He is unrolling a mat at the back. He is already aware that he is going to be the worst person in this class.

### Beat 3: The Practice
He is bad at yoga. This is not surprising — he has a two-hour-per-day exercise practice built entirely around force, endurance, and control. Flexibility and flow are different muscles. His body resists the poses in ways that Jace, who is used to his body cooperating, finds faintly embarrassing.

Lina, on the mat beside him, is good. She has been doing this for a year. She doesn't correct him; she doesn't look at him; she just does the practice. This is the right call. He appreciates it.

The low-g environment changes the poses significantly. Extended positions that would collapse under Earth or Mars gravity are sustainable for long periods. Inversions that require tremendous core strength at higher g can be held with gentle effort here. The instructor spends time in holds — long, patient holds where the only work is breathing.

Jace, who is not accustomed to stillness being the hard part, finds the holds more difficult than the movements.

### Beat 4: The Held Pose (The Pivotal Beat)

Near the end of the session, the instructor guides the class into a long extended pose — a standing forward position with arms extended, palms forward, fingertips toward the far wall. Back straight, feet planted, breathing slow. A sustained hold.

The class goes quiet. The only sounds are breath and the sub-dome's gentle ventilation.

Jace is in the hold. He is breathing. His body is still — genuinely still, in a way that his normal exercise practice almost never requires. In low gravity, the effort to hold the position is minimal. There is almost nothing to do. His mind, which is usually occupied by telemetry, game overlays, scheduling, and the low hum of Aura notifications, has very little to process.

And then his fingertips do something.

It is not pain. It is not numbness. It is a faint warmth — or not quite warmth. More like pressure. Like something is pressing back against his fingertips from the air in front of them. Like a current that is not wind. Persistent, steady, and very specific to his fingertips — not his palms, not his wrists, just the tips.

He keeps breathing. The feeling stays.

He looks at his hands without moving them. They look completely normal — slightly pale from the reduced blood pressure in low-g, but otherwise unremarkable. The feeling stays.

The pose ends. The class flows into a closing sequence. The feeling fades over about two minutes.

### Beat 5: The Corridor Conversation

Afterward, walking back through the dome corridor toward the main settlement:

> Jace: [beat] "Does that happen a lot? The — did you feel anything in your hands? During the last hold?"
>
> Lina: [glances at him] "Feel anything like what?"
>
> Jace: "I don't know exactly. Something at my fingertips. Like a current. Like pressure, but from nothing."
>
> Lina: [a pause that is slightly too long to be nothing] "Some people say that. In the low-g practice, when you hold long enough. I've read about it. Usually it happens to people who've been doing it for a while."
>
> Jace: "I've been doing it for forty minutes."
>
> Lina: "I know."
>
> Jace: "I'm probably just tired."
>
> Lina: "Maybe."

She says it without irony. She also doesn't agree with him.

### Beat 6: The Walk Home

Jace walks back to his apartment. He is not thinking about magic — he does not have that word available yet for his own experience. He is thinking about whether he ate enough today (he didn't), whether the Artemis send will go through tonight (it won't), whether he should apply for the Ceres job that posted this morning (he will; it will disappear before he hears back).

He is not thinking about his fingertips.

But somewhere underneath the list: he noticed. And the noticing has been filed, even though he has not filed it consciously.

## What This Scene Plants

### For the Reader
By this point in the book, the reader has seen:
- The fractal powder in the sewer (magic residue)
- The drone ops screen glitch (Day 1)
- The duck attack at the Bird Dome (Cerberus's early warning)
- Belt stations going dark (the outbreak accelerating)

The reader is building a picture Jace cannot yet see. The fingertips beat confirms it: whatever is returning to the solar system, Jace is not just a bystander. He is a person this thing is finding.

### For Jace's Arc
- The pattern of **stillness as portal** is established: magic arrives to Jace when he is quiet, extended, and not fighting anything. This contrasts with his normal mode of being. The series will return to this.
- His immediate reframing ("probably just tired") is characteristic: Jace files anomalies under mundane explanations until the explanations stop holding. This is a trait and eventually a flaw.
- Lina's *maybe* is the first time another person has responded to Jace's anomaly experience with something other than dismissal. She doesn't explain it. She doesn't confirm it. She leaves it exactly as open as it should be.

### For Cerberus
Cerberus is not present in this scene — he is at the breeder facility. But the timing matters: Cerberus has been growling at nothing during Jace's recent visits, and the dog-breeder staff have noted that the facility dogs are restless. The same wave that touched Jace's fingertips in the low-g dome is what Cerberus has been sensing for days. The two of them are picking up the same signal from different angles.

## Character States

### Jace (entry → exit)
- **Entry:** Wary, skeptical, slightly embarrassed about doing yoga at all
- **During class:** Frustrated by his own inflexibility, then quietly absorbed by the low-g environment, then briefly overwhelmed by something he has no name for
- **Exit:** Filing the experience as "probably tiredness," warmer toward Lina, with something stored in a drawer in his mind that he will open much later

### Lina (entry → exit)
- **Entry:** Comfortable, welcoming, looking forward to the practice
- **During class:** In her element, not performing for Jace
- **Exit:** Noticeably careful in how she answers him. She has read enough about what people experience in low-g practice to know that some experiences are more than fatigue. She doesn't push it. She knows she is leaving in a few weeks. She says *maybe* and means *pay attention.*

## Dialogue Notes

- Lina does not explain the fingertips experience to Jace. Her restraint is the right instinct — and also the right story choice. The ambiguity is load-bearing.
- Jace's "I'm probably just tired" should read as half-convincing — he almost believes it. The reader should almost believe it too, until they don't.
- The instructor has no lines that need to be written. Her presence is ambient.
