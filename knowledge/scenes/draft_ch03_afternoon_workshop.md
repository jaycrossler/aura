# Chapter 3 — Afternoon Workshop

> **Epigraph (CONFIRMED — assigned in spec_chapter_epigraphs.md, transit track):**
> *"Nobody warns you how much video games suck across long distances. When it takes 12 seconds for your shot to land, first-person shooters don't work. Light-speed lag doesn't care about your reflexes; you learn to love turn-based strategy, or you learn to lose to physics."*
> — Jace Grant, *Falcon* transit logs

The machine shop smelled like warm polymer and burnt ambition. Jace had claimed a corner of the bench two months ago and hadn't given it back.

"Thing One — junction schematic, left overlay."

The diagram bloomed in his peripheral. He slid under panel B-7, seal replacement in hand, Thing One hovering behind him with its camera on the gap his knuckles kept finding. Four minutes of awkward. New seal seated clean.

"Log it. Thirty-day flag."

*LOGGED.*

He worked through the queue — a degraded rotor on Drone 44 that turned out to be a microfracture he found with his thumb, a filter clog in ring segment twelve tracing back to a loose baffle plate two segments upstream. Thing One crawled the duct. Thing Two handled the toilet fan. Small fixes. The ship accumulating quiet improvements nobody noticed until they weren't there.

Somewhere in the middle of the rotor job he tried to push a custom diagnostic routine to Thing One and got the same answer he always got: *MODIFICATION NOT AUTHORIZED. ORIGIN FIRMWARE INTEGRITY REQUIRED.* He'd written better diagnostics than the stock package back in the service. The drone would run them fine. The drone was not allowed to run them. The drone was not, in any way that mattered, his.

"Yeah, yeah," he told it. "Integrity."

The workbench corner was where the other project lived — the one that answered to nobody's firmware. Calling it a project flattered it. It was a tray of prototypes in various stages of ambition and failure: a sensor board that worked, a comms module that worked when warm, a hover-jet assembly that had, three weeks ago, shorted with a crack and a smell like lightning and a fountain of blue-white sparks that had sent Cerberus vertical and named the whole enterprise in one stroke. *Sparky.* Not a drone. A direction. Someday, a machine that would run his code because it was his — and, though he only half-admitted this part, a body for the kind of AI Nick kept evangelizing about across the light-lag, the open stacks, the ones you could actually own.

Today's contribution to the direction: a game controller, printed in the good plastic, because his hands were starting to hate the glove interface and a man needed standards. He set the print, and while the bed warmed he queued his turn against Nick.

*new controller??* Nick sent, twelve minutes later, having apparently deduced this from Jace's improved input cadence alone. *finally. you can't pilot a mech with dignity on gloves. when you get here we're doing full interactive mode. epic punch fights. I'm designing the arena already*

*How big is this arena?*

*you'll see. also I finished your mech. it's SHORT. you'll see why that's funny when you meet me*

The controller came off the bed warm and slightly translucent, and fit his hands like an apology from the universe. He lost the evening's match anyway — slower, per Nick, which counted as progress — while Cerberus dismantled a packing foam block at his feet with the single-minded professionalism of the very young.

The coal behind his left eye glowed through all of it, familiar as the water in the walls.

That night he dreamed of crystal again — a hallway of it, bright and layered and impossibly deep, everything transparent to everything else — and woke on Day 3 of the week to the alarm with the dream already sliding away, leaving only the vague conviction that he had been somewhere with a great deal more geometry than a spaceship should have.
