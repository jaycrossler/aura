# Chapter 2 — Morning Rounds

> **Epigraph (staged — author-approved 2026-07-15; add to spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but the most sophisticated piece of equipment on a livestock ferry is the pig. We had six. They knew it."*

The headache arrived before the lights did, the way it had every morning for a month.

Jace lay still and did the inventory. Pressure behind the eyes: present. Heat along the left temple, where the implant sat: present. Sleep mask still sealed over his nose and mouth, because he was the kind of man who read the decompression statistics and then followed the recommendation everyone else laughed at: present. He peeled the mask off, dry-swallowed his anti-radiation tab and the anti-nausea one that kept it company, and gave the headache sixty seconds to declare its intentions.

It faded, like always, to a warm coal behind his left eye. The HUD had been running hot overnight — that was Lanchee's best theory, and the second panel's best theory, and the theory had the great advantage of requiring nothing further from anyone. He'd stopped flagging it. The dreams it left behind — glittering, geological, gone by breakfast — he'd stopped flagging too.

There was a message from Saul waiting, time-delayed from Mars, attached to yesterday's hull job:

*Good cure on R7-04. You torqued the corner anchor a little hot but it'll hold. Watch the adjacent section — the polymer gets brittle when the temp cycles hard. Leave it another day before you load it.*

He listened to it twice. The ship's owner, forty million kilometers behind them, reviewing seal cures like a man tending a garden by mail. Jace tagged the adjacent panel for inspection and filed the note without responding — Saul's replies arrived after the moment had passed anyway. He'd started narrating his own completed jobs slightly more than strictly necessary, on the theory that somebody sunward was listening. He would deny this if asked.

Maureen still hadn't linked his drones to {Falcon}. Something about certification. In practice: her ship, her rules, his problem — every job a manual tag, every fabrication a personal errand.

"Up, boy. Time to potty."

Cerberus executed his flip — comatose cockroach to alert predator in a third of a second, low gravity making it look slightly too easy — and fixed Jace with the focused intensity of a dog who had been awake for some time and was simply waiting for permission to exist.

The bathroom was empty. The dog waste station he'd rigged in the corner had been used, mostly; there was a substantial turd beside the grate with the confident placement of something that had aimed and then simply declined.

"Looks like your sister."

Cerberus sniffed it and shivered.

"Yeah."

He pulled up the fabrication queue in his HUD, tagged a 90mm circulation fan for the toilet module, and assigned Thing Two to collect it once printed — forty minutes to fab, unlinked from {Falcon}, invisible to the ship, living only in his own task list. A small kingdom, but his.

They found Mei in the galley, feeding a scrap of egg to Siren off her own plate. Cerberus barked and hurled himself at his sister; Siren stepped aside with the timing of a matador, and the puppy hit the counter with a yelp and an expression of profound personal betrayal. The siblings collapsed into their standard negotiation — all theater, no damage, considerable noise.

"Morning. Everything good?"

Mei didn't look up. "The pigs are up to something."

Jace nodded. The pigs were always up to something. Nick's message from two nights ago was still pinned in his queue — *Screwdriver figured out the feeder latch again, I watched her do it, what is she* — which meant the pig-cam had beaten him to his own morning report. Again. Half the belt watched those pigs. He had made the mistake, early on, of reading the channel comments, and had learned that he himself appeared in them under the tag "mask guy," with a small but loyal following.

"Take Siren on the run?"

He answered with a mouthful of coffee — real coffee, Platinum-tier perk, the one unambiguous good in his contract. Mei turned and walked aft. Eleven words this morning. One more than yesterday. At this rate they'd have a conversation by Jupiter.

He glanced down the corridor toward Lanchee's cabin on the way out. Door closed, a voice — hers, low, professional — carrying faintly through. Already in consultation with somebody, somewhere. He'd passed her twice this week, once at the coffee machine at 0500, once in the corridor where they'd both stepped aside politely and said nothing. She knew things about Fortuna's sick bay that Fortuna's own staff probably didn't know yet. He knew she took her tea black. Four months in a can with someone was apparently no guarantee of anything.

The run: three laps of the ring in the weighted compression suit, one small brindle dog doing independent circuits around him, occasionally misjudging the Coriolis on a corner and cannoning off a wall with no loss of dignity whatsoever. The water shushed along beside them in the walls, compensating, always compensating.

Behind his left eye, the coal glowed on, patient.
