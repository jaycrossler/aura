# Chapter 14 — Down to the Asteroid

> **Epigraph (staged — author-approved 2026-07-15; add to spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but the human brain can file absolutely anything under 'I was just tired.' Walking through a wall. A woman pulling oxygen out of the air. I know its limits now. Its limits are two text messages."*

The shuttle to the surface left at 0640, and Jace made it with ninety seconds to spare, unshowered, wearing yesterday's base layer, carrying a nutrient bar he had no memory of picking up.

He had slept, by his own reconstruction, about forty minutes.

The two messages sat in his HUD where he'd pinned them — *Made it back to the Victoria. Told you bodies remember. — S* and *You still owe me dinner* — and on the walk to the shuttle bay he had read them eleven more times, because they were the entire load-bearing structure of his sanity this morning and he wanted to make sure they were torqued to spec. Everything else was deniable. The brightness, the layered walls, the woman pulling an oxygen bottle out of the air with her teeth gritted like a powerlifter — a brain could invent all of that, given hypoxia and stress and a month of crystal dreams. A brain does not receive timestamped text messages from its own hallucination at 0247, care of the *Victoria*'s comm relay.

Unless she'd also been a dream. Unless he'd sent them to himself. He checked the routing headers in the shuttle queue, actually checked them, standing there swaying with his bar in his hand, and the headers were clean, and a small hysterical voice in the back of his head said: *congratulations, you have now forensically authenticated your own magical girlfriend's text messages,* and he told the voice that she was not his — that it was a debt dinner — and the voice said *sure* and he boarded the shuttle.

Cerberus was not with him. Dogs didn't ride the surface shuttles, and after last night the idea of taking Cerberus anywhere near a pressure boundary made something in Jace's chest close like a valve, so the dog had been delivered at 0615 to Nick's pod, along with the stuffed pig and a feeding schedule Nick had accepted with the gravity of a man receiving launch codes. *("He had a rough night," Jace had said. Nick had looked at the dog, who was radiant, and at Jace, who was gray, and said, "Sure. HE did.")*

The descent took eleven minutes and Jace spent all of them with his forehead against the viewport.

3 Fortuna came up at them slowly — the gray shoulder of the world, sunlit along its terminator, the manufacturing district resolving out of the dimness in rectangles of floodlight and the slow constellation of the robot hives. He had seen it from the survey boat. He had seen it, he now suspected, from somewhere else too. What he had never done was stand on it, and when the shuttle grounded and the crew cycled out through the surface lock into one-fiftieth of a gravity, Jace took his first step onto an actual asteroid and had to be caught by the elbow like a tourist.

"First-timers bounce," said the crew lead, unbothered. "Everything's clips and lines out here. You're the drone man?"

He was the drone man. The day's work was real and he was grateful for it the way you're grateful for a handrail: the fabrication district's hive had flagged eleven maintenance exceptions in a week — drift in the crawler fleet's navigation solutions, two units parked and sulking, one charging frame browned out — and somebody from ops had to come down, walk the installation, and reconcile what the machines said with what was true. Jace walked it. He clipped to the guide lines between the floodlit factory sheds, moved hand over hand past the smelter's radiator field, crouched at the dead charging frame and traced the fault to a connector that had cold-cycled one time too many, and for four hours he was just an engineer on a rock, doing the job, in a hard vacuum silence so total that his own pulse was the loudest thing on the asteroid.

Except.

Except he kept testing the world. He couldn't stop. Every panel he touched, some background process noted: *solid.* Every wall of every shed: *solid.* He caught himself, twice, pressing his glove flat against a bulkhead and leaning, very slightly, with a fractional and horrified curiosity — and the bulkhead pushed back the way matter had pushed back his whole life until last night, and he could not decide if the feeling that followed was relief.

He thought about the fourth asteroid, too. Standing on this one. He didn't want to, and did anyway: the gouges, the spool-wound channels, the assay that came back *emptied*. Last night a woman had pulled a steel bottle through a wall between worlds because mass was the cost and effort was the currency. Something with more effort than a tired physicist could spend, he thought, looking down the floodlit kilometer of the district, might pull other things through other walls. He filed the thought where he was now filing a category of thought: a new folder, unlabeled, growing.

"You're quiet," Kim said over the crew channel, from the crawler bay. It was not solicitude; it was an operational observation, flagged like a sensor drift.

"Long night."

"Mm." A pause, comm static, the sound of her recalibrating something. "The connector fix was clean." Then, because the universe kept its books in strange currencies: "There's still dog hair in intake filter six."

"I'll clean it on my own time."

"Yes," Kim said. "You will."

The shuttle lifted at 1710. Fortuna Station came down at them out of the black — spine and rings and scaffolds, windows lit, the whole improbable town he lived in hanging over its rock — and Jace watched it grow and made himself say the true thing, privately, in the space where he kept his reports honest:

*Last night happened. I walked through walls. There is another world folded into this one, close enough to touch, and a woman has been going there alone every night for weeks with nothing but spare oxygen bottles and a notebook, and tonight I'm having dinner with her, and I am going to sit in a cafeteria like a normal person and talk about the single largest discovery in the history of the species over gray soup.*

He looked down at himself. Yesterday's base layer. Forty minutes of sleep. A nutrient bar wrapper in his thigh pocket he'd been carrying for eleven hours.

He went home. He showered. He collected his dog from Nick, who reported that Cerberus had eaten well, destroyed nothing, and spent forty minutes at 1400 staring at a blank wall — "just, at the wall, man, both of us sat there looking at the wall, I checked it with three sensors, there's nothing in the wall" — and Jace scratched his dog's ears and said, "Yeah," in the tone that was becoming their word for it.

Then he put on his cleanest shirt, which was a relative term, and went to buy a woman dinner for saving his life.
