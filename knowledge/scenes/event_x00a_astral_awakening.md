# X-00a — Astral Awakening

> **Proposed epigraph (staged — new entry for spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but the first human to cross between dimensions
> in three thousand years was carried most of the way by a dog."*
> — attributed narrator voice, dry-snark register, status: staged

---

His eyes open on chaos. Not darkness — the opposite. A trillion points of light scattered through space, all vibrating at slightly different frequencies. There is no discrete wall ahead of him, no floor beneath. There are *layers*. The bulkhead of his quarters is there — he recognizes its rivets, its pressure seams — but he's also seeing *through* it to the corridor beyond, and through that to the section beyond, all occupying the same visual space, bleeding into each other like a double-exposed photograph where every exposure is real.

He can't breathe.

Cerberus is on his chest — and Cerberus is a nightmare of clarity. Jace sees his dog's shape, the outline he knows, the weight pressing down — and he also sees *inside*. Lungs heaving like overworked bellows. A heart hammering far too fast. Blood moving through vessels like crimson threads lit from within. The dog has Jace's face mask gripped in his jaws, and the mask is *wrong* — half here and half somewhere else, its far edge blurred and doubled like it's being dragged through frosted glass, and Cerberus is hauling on it with everything he has left, which is almost nothing.

Jace's lungs are burning. Panic does what nothing else could: it cuts through the overload. His hands close on the mask beside his dog's jaws and he *pulls* — and something gives, some resistance he has no name for, and the mask comes the rest of the way through, whole and solid and real. He seats it. The seal takes. Air.

Cerberus collapses against his chest.

The dog is limp. Lungs still moving — Jace can *see* them moving, shallow and fast — but the eyes are half-closed and the legs won't answer. Jace rips his own mask back off, presses it over the dog's muzzle, watches the luminous bellows inside his dog's ribcage deepen, one breath, two, three, while his own vision starts to tunnel. He takes the mask back, drags two breaths, returns it. Trade. Trade. Trade.

It isn't sustainable. Cerberus's fitted mask — the dog-sized emergency rig — hangs on its hook by the hatch, and when Jace grabs for it his fingers close on that same frosted-glass wrongness, the thing both there and not there. So he does what he did before, not understanding what he did before: he plants himself and *hauls*. The resistance is enormous, like deadlifting something bolted to the floor of the universe. And then it tears free all at once and the little mask is in his hands, solid, and he's sealing it around Cerberus's muzzle with shaking fingers, watching the cartridge indicator flick to green.

He lies there a long time, one hand fisted in his dog's fur, feeling the heartbeat slow toward something survivable. Two hours, seventeen minutes on his own readout. Same window on Cerberus's pack. Not a death sentence. A countdown.

*He pulled it for me first.* The thought arrives and won't leave. The dog spent himself dragging Jace's mask across whatever gap this is, before pulling anything for himself.

It's cold. Eight, nine degrees below where the station holds it. His breath fogs the inside of the mask. Cerberus shivers against him.

His HUD is useless. The clock reads a timestamp that isn't a time. The radio icon cycles between SEARCHING and a null-glyph he's never seen. Twice while he watches, the whole overlay stutters, dies, and reboots into safe mode, offering him a diagnostic menu with the serene confidence of a system that has no idea it's the least of his problems.

*Stroke,* he thinks. *Radiation event. Something cooked my visual cortex.* The theories are terrible, but they're the only frameworks he has, and an engineer with a bad framework beats an engineer with none. He needs people. Medical. He gathers Cerberus and makes for the hatch.

His hand passes through the button.

Not a dream-hand — his fingers are there, solid to his own touch, but the button doesn't register. He presses harder and his hand sinks into the fascia like wet clay before easing back out. He stares at it. Tries again. Same.

The panic that hits then is the kind that makes you stupid and bold at the same time. He puts both palms flat against the hatch and *pushes*.

It isn't solid.

The metal doesn't yield the way metal yields. It's like pressing into still water, except the resistance comes from everywhere at once. And when he commits his weight, everything *shifts* — the corridor rotates, not his inner ear but the geometry itself, the hatch tipping to an angle, the walls stretching in a direction that doesn't have a name — and then he's through, Cerberus clutched to his chest, both of them stumbling into the corridor.

The same corridor. But deeper. He can see the length of it curving away in both directions at once, the panels behind the panels, cable runs inside the walls glowing faintly with data. He closes his eyes — worse; the brightness loses its anchor to anything he recognizes — opens them, picks one rivet, and breathes. In four. Hold four. Out four.

He walks.

It's midnight, station time, and the residential spine is asleep — and he can *see* that. Through the walls on either side, the sleepers glow. Person after person laid out in their bunks, soft luminous shapes, hearts pulsing like slow beacons, breath moving through them in tides. One woman two doors down sits up awake, a tablet's glow flat and strange against her radiance, watching some media he can't hear. A couple shares a late meal in their quarters, forks moving, mouths moving, silent as fish in an aquarium. He looks away, feeling like a trespasser, and then can't stop looking, because they are everywhere and the walls hide nothing.

Every few minutes his hand finds Cerberus's mask without being asked — the dog keeps shoving his nose at things, at the glowing sleepers, at the seams in the deck, and every sniff torques the seal a few degrees off true. Jace resets it each time, checks the indicator, resets it again. Cerberus tolerates this with the long-suffering patience of a dog who has decided his human needs something to do with his hands.

Near hydroponics, a cleaning robot works the deck in slow circles, cloth attachment humming. He crouches. "Hey. Hey, can you—" His hand goes through it. The ghost of resistance, something vast and indifferent occupying the space where his fingers are. It polishes on, oblivious.

Maybe the robot is broken. Maybe *he* is. He stops theorizing. Conserves oxygen.

The food zone. Night crews eat there; it's never truly empty. If there are people anywhere, they're there. He leans into the next sealed door, lets the rotation happen — it's getting easier, not less wrong but more automatic — and steps through.

The cafeteria is bright in the overlapping way everything has been bright. He can see the prep station and through it, the storage racks beyond, the circulation air moving in slow visible rivers. At a table near the middle sits Torres — Torres, three days off the survey run, wide awake at midnight exactly as he'd predicted on the boat, his body still holding zero-g time — glowing softly, slowly forking food into his mouth, scrolling a tablet with his thumb. Jace stands two meters away and says his name. Nothing. Waves. Nothing. Torres scrolls, chews, scrolls, radiant and utterly elsewhere.

And then Jace sees her.

At a corner table, a woman — and she is not glowing. She is *sharp*. Solid. Three-dimensional in a room full of light-sculptures, sweat on her temple, arms shaking, straining at empty air like she's bench-pressing nothing at all.

She hears him breathe. Her head snaps up.
