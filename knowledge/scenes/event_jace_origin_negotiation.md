# Scene — The Origin Industries Negotiation

## Purpose

The contract that puts Jace on the *Falcon*. A negotiation he is losing without realizing he is losing it. Establishes Origin's institutional posture (corporate, panicked-but-disciplined, ready to bleed Jace for a quick fix) and Jace's diminished bargaining position (no HUD, no Aura intelligence on Origin's actual position, naïve about the negotiation as a *negotiation* rather than a job interview). Plants the Cerberus moment. Sets up the tag scene that confirms for the reader exactly how badly Jace lost.

## Position in Sequence

After the Smuggler has flagged Jace for the emergency slot and crash certification has begun. The negotiation closes the deal that boards Jace on the *Falcon*. After this scene, Mr. Sylvester reaches out and the intelligence deal happens.

## Setting

An Origin Industries office in the U.S. Mars settlement. Could be a satellite office near the launch facility or a downtown corporate building. Decor is corporate-functional, not lavish — Origin Industries is patient money, not splashy money. A small conference room. Glass wall to a hallway. A potted real-Earth-plant on a side table (status symbol). A wall screen displaying live ferry traffic data.

## Characters Present

- Jace Grant (POV)
- Origin Representative (TBD — name and characterization to develop; mid-career, smooth, has done this hundreds of times, not cruel but absolutely working for the company)
- Briefly off-stage / in tag scene: Origin Rep's boss

## Pre-Scene Context (Important)

- Jace has been in the hard months for several weeks. He is thinner, more tired, and his confidence has cracked but not collapsed.
- His HUD is *deactivated* — he is operating without his usual augmentation. To prepare for the meeting, he had to use a clunky civilian tablet to look up Origin's history, the contract terms, the standard rates. He is not used to this. The tablet is slow. His pre-research is patchy.
- He has no Aura assistance during the meeting itself — only what he could memorize beforehand.
- He has, that morning, finished a shift cleaning out animal pens at the Mars livestock farm — including the pen with the dog mother and her puppies. He met the boy puppy and learned the family is being separated. He named the puppy Cerberus on the walk to this meeting. He has not told anyone about the dog.

## Emotional Beats

1. **Walking in.** Jace in the cleanest of his civilian clothes, which still don't fit right. He sits down across from the Origin rep. They shake hands. The rep is friendly and slightly hurried — which Jace reads as professionalism but is actually pressure.

2. **The setup.** The rep explains the situation in broad strokes: Origin has a high-value belt run launching within days. A critical animal-handler crew member dropped out at the last possible moment. The cargo cannot wait for a full hiring cycle. They need someone capable, available, and willing to sign for a ten-year posting on short notice. Jace's name came up.

3. **Jace tries to project competence.** He talks about his military background, his discipline, his ability to learn fast. The rep nods politely. The rep already knows all of this; he has Jace's full record. He is letting Jace perform the parts of the interview Jace expects to perform.

4. **The ask, reframed as a problem.** The rep sympathetically notes Jace's "limitations." He is taller than ideal — the livestock cylinder gravity is gentle but the ergonomics favor smaller crew. He eats more than the standard caloric allowance, especially with his glucose-interface HUD (which the rep, of course, does not yet know is deactivated). His animal experience is limited to childhood dogs. *"These are real concerns, Jace. We're going to need to be creative on the compensation side."*

5. **Jace tries to push back.** He counters that he is willing, that he can train, that his discipline compensates. He does not realize that the rep has just successfully framed the conversation around Jace's deficiencies rather than Origin's desperation. The rep nods, takes notes, looks concerned.

6. **The offer.** A figure that sounds large to Jace — and, frankly, will pay off most of his debt — but is structured to back-load the payment to the end of the ten-year contract. He gets an upfront stipend, monthly draws against expenses, and the bulk on completion. If he washes out, he gets nothing.

7. **The HUD-less negotiation.** Jace does mental math without his Aura. He's slow. He doesn't have access to Origin's standard rates. He doesn't know that emergency replacements, on a high-value run with a panicked client, typically command 25-40% premium over baseline. He feels the offer is fair because his reference point is *desperate*, not *informed*. He says he'd like to think about it.

8. **The rep gently squeezes.** *"Of course. Take an hour. Just understand we have other names on the list, and the launch window won't wait."* This is largely true. It is also leverage.

9. **Jace's pivot — Cerberus.** Scrambling for any leverage or addition he can think of, Jace remembers the puppy. He explains, awkwardly, about the dog. About the mother shipping out, the boy puppy being sold on Mars, the family being separated. He asks if there's any way Origin can include the puppy on the manifest with him. He frames it as a small request. He is half-embarrassed asking.

10. **The rep considers.** A pause. The rep weighs it visibly — pretending to weigh more than he is. The dog is functionally free; the cargo manifest can absorb a puppy without difficulty. *"That's an unusual ask, Jace. I'd have to check with operations."* He pretends to do so via tablet. *"...okay. We can include the dog. Consider it part of the package."*

11. **Jace closes the deal.** With the dog secured, Jace agrees to the offer. They shake hands. Jace leaves feeling like he held his ground and got a small win on the dog.

12. **The tag scene.** After Jace leaves, the rep walks down the hall to his boss's office.

> *"How'd it go?"*
> *"He signed."*
> *"Number?"*
> *(The rep tells him.)*
> *"Below ceiling?"*
> *"About two hundred under. He could've pushed for more if he'd held out an hour. Too rattled to see it."*
> *"What flipped him?"*
> *"He wanted me to add a dog to the manifest. A puppy. He asked for it like it was a real concession."*
> *(The boss laughs.)*
> *"That's probably the most expensive dog in human history."*

The boss approves the rep's commission and tells him to take the rest of the afternoon. The rep grins and goes.

## Tone and Pacing Notes

- **The negotiation should feel like Jace is doing okay.** The reader knows it isn't. Jace's confidence is brittle but visible — he is performing the negotiation he expected, which is the wrong negotiation.
- **The HUD absence should *show*.** Subtle: Jace blinks at numbers, mentally calculates, hesitates a beat too long, reaches for his tablet once, decides not to in front of the rep. With his HUD active, he would be silently consulting a real-time analysis of Origin's negotiating posture. Without it, he is unarmed.
- **Cerberus matters.** The dog is the only authentic moment in the meeting. Jace asking for the puppy is the first time he speaks from somewhere true. The rep notices. The reader notices. Origin profits.
- **The tag scene is short and devastating.** Two paragraphs maximum. Lands like a knife.

## What This Scene Establishes

- **Origin Industries' character.** Not cartoonishly evil. Just disciplined, well-trained, and absolutely working for the company. The rep is a professional. The boss approves the deal. This is how a competent megacorporation operates.
- **Jace's bargaining position.** Naïve, desperate, HUD-less, lonely. The reader sees the asymmetry.
- **The HUD as plot device.** Jace's loss of the HUD has tangible costs in real situations. This sets up Sylvester's offer to reactivate it as a *meaningful* lever, not just a sentimental one.
- **Cerberus as the only thing he gets.** The contract is a loss disguised as a save. Cerberus is a real save disguised as a small win. The book's larger pattern.

## Lines to Consider Preserving

- *"That's probably the most expensive dog in human history."* — the closing line. Reader-facing. Land it precisely.
