# Scene — The Origin Industries Negotiation

## Purpose

The contract that puts Jace on the *Falcon*. A negotiation he is losing without realizing he is losing it. Establishes Origin's institutional posture (corporate, panicked-but-disciplined, ready to bleed Jace for a quick fix) and Jace's diminished bargaining position (no HUD, no {Aura} intelligence on Origin's actual position, naïve about the negotiation as a *negotiation* rather than a job interview). Contains the Cerberus purchase — not a free manifest add-on, but real money Jace spends with clear eyes. Sets up the tag scene that confirms for the reader exactly how badly Jace lost the contract itself.

## Position in Sequence

After the Smuggler has flagged Jace for the emergency slot and crash certification has begun. The negotiation closes the deal that boards Jace on the *Falcon*. After this scene, Mr. Sylvester reaches out and the intelligence deal happens.

## Pre-Scene Context

- Jace has been in the hard months for several weeks. Thinner, tired, confidence cracked but not collapsed.
- His HUD is *deactivated* — he prepared on a clunky civilian tablet. His pre-research is patchy.
- He has no {Aura} assistance during the meeting itself.
- He has, that morning, finished a shift at the dog breeder facility. He has spent weeks with the litter whose mother died. He knows what he is going to do about the male puppy. He knows what it costs. He has done the math. He is going to do it anyway.
- He named the puppy **Cerberus** on the walk to this meeting.

## Emotional Beats

1. **Walking in.** Jace in the cleanest of his civilian clothes, which still don't fit right. The rep is friendly and slightly hurried — which Jace reads as professionalism but is actually pressure.

2. **The setup.** Origin explains the situation: high-value belt run launching within days, critical animal-handler crew member dropped out at the last moment, they need someone available and willing to sign immediately. Jace's name came up.

3. **Jace tries to project competence.** He talks about his military background, his discipline, his ability to learn fast. The rep nods politely. He already knows all of this.

4. **The ask, reframed as a problem.** The rep sympathetically notes Jace's "limitations" — height, caloric needs, limited animal experience. *"These are real concerns. We're going to need to be creative on the compensation side."* Jace has just been repositioned in this conversation without realizing it.

5. **Jace tries to push back.** He counters that he is willing, that he can train, that his discipline compensates. He does not realize that the rep has already framed the conversation around Jace's deficiencies rather than Origin's desperation.

6. **The offer.** A figure that sounds large to Jace but is structured to back-load almost all meaningful payment to the end of ten years. Upfront stipend, monthly draws, bulk on completion. If he washes out, he gets nothing.

7. **The HUD-less negotiation.** Jace does mental math without his {Aura}. He doesn't have access to Origin's standard rates. He doesn't know that emergency replacements on a high-value run typically command 25-40% premium over baseline. He says he'd like to think about it.

8. **The rep gently squeezes.** *"Of course. Take an hour. Just understand we have other names on the list, and the launch window won't wait."*

9. **Jace's pivot — Cerberus.** Jace brings up the dog. Not impulsively, not as a cheap ask. He explains the situation: orphaned litter, the female going to Fortuna as breeding stock, the male he has been visiting for weeks. He wants to bring the male puppy with him. He offers to pay for the puppy's place on the manifest. He names a number — real money, a significant portion of his upfront stipend, money he will feel the absence of. He is not asking for a favor. He is buying the dog.

10. **The rep considers.** The rep weighs it briefly. Taking money for something the manifest can absorb at minimal cost is straightforwardly good for Origin. *"That's an unusual request. Let me check with operations."* He pretends to do so via tablet. *"...we can make that work."*

11. **Jace closes the deal.** With the dog confirmed, Jace agrees to the contract. They shake hands. Jace leaves having overpaid for the contract and paid real money for the dog. He is at peace with the second thing.

12. **The tag scene.** After Jace leaves, the rep walks down the hall to his boss's office.

> *"How'd it go?"*
> *"He signed."*
> *"Number?"*
> *(The rep tells him.)*
> *"Below ceiling?"*
> *"About two hundred under. He could've pushed for more if he'd held out an hour. Too rattled to see it."*
> *"What flipped him?"*
> *"He wanted to buy a dog onto the manifest. Paid for it himself. An orphaned puppy from a dead mother, apparently. The sister's already going up."*
> *(The boss shakes his head, half amused.)*
> *"That's probably the most expensive dog in human history."*

The boss approves the rep's commission. The rep grins and goes.

## Tone and Pacing Notes

- **The negotiation should feel like Jace is doing okay.** The reader knows it isn't. His confidence is brittle but visible — he is performing the negotiation he expected, which is the wrong negotiation.
- **The HUD absence should *show*.** Subtle: Jace blinks at numbers, mentally calculates, hesitates a beat too long. With his HUD active, he would be silently consulting a real-time analysis of Origin's negotiating posture. Without it, he is unarmed.
- **The dog purchase is the only authentic moment in the meeting.** Jace asking for the puppy — and paying for him — is the first time he speaks and acts from somewhere true. The rep notices. The reader notices. Origin profits on the contract; Jace wins on the dog.
- **The tag scene is short and not entirely dismissive.** Two paragraphs maximum. "Dead mother" is in there now — the boss is not entirely without feeling, even if the punchline lands the same.

## What This Scene Establishes

- **Origin Industries' character.** Competent, disciplined, not cartoonishly evil. The rep is a professional. This is how a megacorporation operates.
- **Jace's bargaining position.** Naïve, desperate, HUD-less, lonely. The reader sees the asymmetry.
- **The HUD as plot device.** Jace's loss of the HUD has tangible costs in real situations. Sets up Sylvester's offer to reactivate it as meaningful.
- **Cerberus as a real decision, not an impulse.** Jace knew what it cost. He did it anyway. The reader has watched weeks of attachment at the breeder facility — and the mother's death — and understands what this means before Jace can say it.
