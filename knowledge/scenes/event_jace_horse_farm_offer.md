# Scene — The Horse Farm Offer

## Summary

Late in his hard months, Jace receives a video call from his parents in Virginia. They have been quietly talking it over for weeks and have made a decision: they are willing to sell the family horse farm to pay off Jace's million-credit Mars debt. Dawn does most of the talking. Chris is mostly silent. The offer is genuine, painful, and total. It would clear Jace's books. It would also end the farm — and the working life Chris has spent forty years building, and the place Jelly lives, and the fields Jinx grazes, and the home Athena would come back to between college terms.

Jace refuses.

The refusal is the **driving motivation** that pushes him toward the Origin Industries asteroid contract. He cannot let his parents lose the farm because of him. Origin's contract — bad as the terms turn out to be, dangerous as the belt is — pays his debt over the contract term. It is the only path he can see that keeps the Virginia farm intact.

## Function in the Story

- **The motivational hinge.** This scene is the reason Jace signs the Origin contract. Without this beat, the Origin negotiation is just "a hard guy makes a hard deal." With this beat, it is "a son refuses to let his parents pay for him." That changes the moral weight of everything that follows.
- **Stakes-on-Earth.** The Virginia farm becomes a *stake*, not just a setting. The reader now has skin in keeping it intact. Future references to the farm carry weight.
- **The unspoken irony.** Jace cannot return to Earth (the bone disease). The farm he is preserving is a place he will never visit again. He is choosing to protect a home he has already lost. Neither he nor his parents fully say this aloud during the call — but the reader sees it.
- **Chris's silence.** Chris is a man who would, in fact, sell the farm for his son. The fact that Dawn does most of the talking — and that Chris's quiet is not assent but the deepest kind of grief at what he is willing to do — should land in the room.
- **Athena's absence.** Athena is in college. Her room at the farm is implied. The farm is partly being preserved for her too. The scene does not need to say this; the reader will feel it.

## Setting

Jace's small post-discharge apartment in Terminus. Modest, cheap, sparsely furnished. He has been slowly losing things — sold the better HUD accessories, sold the gym bag, sold one of the two pairs of running shoes. The room is not depressing exactly; it is just *thin*.

The video call comes in the evening Mars-time. Earth-side, Dawn and Chris are calling from the farm kitchen. The window behind them shows the late afternoon Virginia light. A horse can be heard, very faintly, in the distance.

## Beats

### Beat 1: The Call Comes
The call request lands in Jace's HUD interface. Mom and Dad together. Two-person frame on the call, which is unusual — they usually call separately. Jace has been bracing for this kind of call without knowing it. He accepts.

> Dawn: "Hi sweetheart."
> Jace: "Hi Mom. Dad."
> Chris: [nods]

A beat. The pleasantries don't quite connect. Dawn looks at Chris. Chris looks at his hands. Dawn looks back at Jace.

### Beat 2: The Soft Open
Dawn opens carefully. She asks how he's eating. (Rice, mostly. He doesn't say.) She asks how the gigs are going. (Fine. He says.) She asks if he's seen Rich lately. (Yeah, sometimes. He says.) Chris is still quiet.

### Beat 3: The Offer
Dawn breathes in.

> Dawn: "Honey. Your father and I have been talking. We — we want you to know that we can sell the farm. The whole thing. We've talked to a realtor. We could clear your debt and have a little left over. We could move into town. It would be okay."

Jace doesn't react for a beat. Then:

> Jace: "...what?"

Dawn says it again, slower. The numbers. The realtor's estimate. What it would cover. What would be left. They've thought this through. They are serious.

Chris finally speaks:

> Chris: "We can do it, son. It's just a farm. You're our boy."

It is not just a farm.

### Beat 4: Jace's Refusal
Jace, broke and defeated and sitting in a small apartment on Mars, becomes — for the first time in weeks — sharp. Officer's posture comes back into his shoulders. He looks his father in the eye.

> Jace: "No. Dad. No."
> Chris: "Jace—"
> Jace: "*No.* That farm is — what would you do? What would Mom do? Where would Athena come home to?"
> Dawn: "Sweetheart, Athena would understand—"
> Jace: "Mom. I'm not letting you sell Jinx. I'm not letting you sell the farm. I'm not. I'll figure it out."

The word *Jinx* lands harder than the rest of the sentence. Dawn's face goes still. Chris looks down at his hands again. They have, in fact, also been thinking about Jinx.

### Beat 5: The Plan Forms
Jace, sitting with the weight of having just refused the only obvious path, tells them about the Origin opportunity. He has been hesitating. He has been considering. The contract is real. The Rat (the Smuggler) has lined it up. The slot is open. It pays. It gets him out of debt over the contract term.

> Jace: "There's an asteroid contract. With Origin. It's — it's serious work. Long term. But it pays. I can do it. I can pay it off."
> Dawn: [tears] "Honey, the asteroid belt — Artemis is out there—"
> Jace: "Mom, Artemis is fine. The belt is fine. I'm going to take the contract."

He has just decided. He had not, before this call, fully decided.

### Beat 6: The Signoff
The conversation winds down. Dawn cries; Chris doesn't quite. Jace promises to call before he ships out. They tell him they love him. He tells them he loves them. He asks about Jelly. (Jelly is fine — sleeping in the barn.) He asks about Jinx. (Jinx is fine — slow this morning, but cantering well in the afternoon.) The call ends.

Jace sits in the small apartment. He looks at the ceiling. He has just made the decision that will define the next several years of his life — possibly the rest of it. He gets up, goes to the small bathroom, splashes water on his face, comes back, opens his HUD, and starts putting together what he needs to walk into the Origin meeting.

## Character States

### Jace
- **Entry:** Hollowed out, drifting through hard months, considering the Origin contract abstractly without commitment
- **Exit:** Decided. Spine straightened by the refusal. Knows what he is going to do and why. He has not become happy; he has become *committed*.

### Dawn
- **Entry:** Resolved to make the offer. Has been steeling herself for this call for weeks.
- **Exit:** Crying, but relieved that he refused. The offer was real, but neither parent wanted him to accept.

### Chris
- **Entry:** Resolved (would have done it). Devastated quietly that it has come to this.
- **Exit:** Proud of his son. Will not say so. Will say it years later in a different conversation.

## Dialogue Notes

- Dawn does most of the talking. Chris's silence is the biggest emotional beat in the scene.
- Jace is *not* tearful here. He becomes, briefly, a stronger version of himself. The refusal is the moment he resembles the officer he was again, just for a moment. The reader feels the return and feels what it costs.
- The word *Jinx* should land specifically. So should *farm*. So should *Athena would come home to.*
- No one mentions Artemis except Dawn, briefly, and Jace deflects. The Artemis comms problem is not the topic of this call.

## What This Scene Plants

- **The farm is preserved** — Jace's parents do not sell. Future references to the farm in the series remain accurate.
- **Jace's commitment to the Origin contract** is now motivated. The negotiation scene that follows shortly after is no longer a man making a hard deal in desperation; it is a man making a deal *to keep something specific safe*.
- **The bone disease's cruel layer** — Jace is preserving a place he will never see again — is now in the reader's awareness without being said.
- **Jelly and Jinx** are now established as load-bearing details, not just decoration.
