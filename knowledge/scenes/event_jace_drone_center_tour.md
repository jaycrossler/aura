# Scene — Jace's Drone Control Center Tour

## Summary

Rich brings Jace into the drone control center on the joint U.S. Air Force / U.S. Space Force base — a large command center room in the building. Jace gets his first look at the operational scale of what he'll be commanding: six drone operators at banks of HUD-integrated workstations managing fleets of large drones, fixed-wing vehicles, and hundreds of robots across the flight line and the surrounding base. This is the scene that sells Jace on the job — and quietly previews how much he'll lose when his HUD is deactivated.

It is also the scene where the **scale of corporate presence** on Mars first lands on Jace as a concrete number: the U.S. military flies roughly half of the American drones on Mars; the corporations fly the rest.

## Function in the Story

- **Establishes the scope of Jace's future command.** Six direct reports. Hundreds of drones and fixed-wing vehicles. Large-scale robotic maintenance operations. He will be responsible for all of it.
- **Plants the operators as recurring characters.** They glimpse their future LT here. When Jace returns later under different circumstances (or is discharged before he ever leads them), the reader feels the loss.
- **Sells the integration of HUD + command.** Watching the operators work, Jace sees what his own HUD upgrades have been training him toward. This deepens the wound when the HUD goes dark.
- **Quiet foreshadowing of Mei and Sylvester.** Through a secure-area window during this segment, Jace sees them in a meeting with the base commander. He files them away without knowing why.
- **Tee-up of the corporate presence on Mars.** The "almost a thousand American drones, half of them corporate" line plants the reader's awareness that Mars is a *jointly* military-and-corporate environment. Origin Industries, Stellar Dynamics, and others have their own air traffic. This becomes load-bearing for later corporate-power story beats.

## Setting

The drone control center is a hardened, low-light room inside the joint base — operations-grade lighting tuned to keep HUD overlays readable. Banks of physical screens wrap around six operator stations; each operator has a personal HUD overlay layered on top. Real-time feeds from the flight line dominate one wall: hundreds of drones in various states (charging, taxiing, in flight, in maintenance bays), with robot crews swarming over them. Other feeds show satellite tracks, orbital traffic windows, and surface logistics drones moving cargo between domes.

The room hums — equipment ventilation, the low murmur of operator voice traffic, the click of physical controls, occasional sub-vocalized commands picked up by throat mics.

## Beats

### Beat 1: Entry and Awe (~16:00)
Rich badges them through a secure door. The room opens up. Jace stops involuntarily — this is bigger than anything he saw at his Earth-side training. Rich watches his face and grins; he remembers his own first look.

> Rich: "Yeah. Welcome to the actual job."
>
> Jace: "I used to be one of these pilots — I was pretty good at handling a fleet of thirty."
>
> Rich: "Nice! I'm not so good at the 3D stuff. The comms and networking is more of my speed. I have a similar set-up downstairs where we watch all the satellites and cameras… it's been crazy busy lately with all the problems."
>
> Jace: "You mean the sunspots causing outages in the belt?"
>
> Rich: "It's a lot more than that. You'll see once you're read in to the program."

[Rich takes a sip from his coffee canister — see character notes — and lets the moment land.]

### Beat 2: The Six Operators
Six operators at their stations, four men and two women, mixed enlisted and junior officer. They notice the new LT entering and most of them stand briefly — not full attention, but acknowledgment. A sergeant nearest the door turns first.

> Operator (Sergeant): "LT. Welcome."
> Jace: [returns the nod] "Good to be here."

Rich walks Jace down the row, introducing each operator by name and station specialty:
- **Station 1:** Surface drone fleet (cargo, personnel transport)
- **Station 2:** Fixed-wing operations (long-range surveillance, atmospheric science)
- **Station 3:** Flight line robotics (maintenance and turnaround)
- **Station 4:** Base perimeter robotics (security, cleaning, dust management)
- **Station 5:** Inter-dome logistics drones
- **Station 6:** Reserve / training / shift handoff station

### Beat 3: The Scale Reveal
At Station 3, Jace lingers. The wall feeds show the flight line in real time — hundreds of drones, hundreds of robots crawling over them, dust blowing across concrete pads. He starts counting and stops.

> Jace: "How many machines are we running right now?"
> Operator: "Right now? About four hundred drones in the air or active across all categories. Closer to nine hundred robots if you count the cleaners and the small maintenance bots. Numbers spike when we have a launch window."

Rich, behind him, takes another sip from his canister and adds:

> Rich: "There are almost a thousand American drone aircraft on Mars total. Between the small drones and the big fixed-wing — your team probably flies half of them. The corporations fly the rest."

Jace turns.

> Jace: "Wait — *the corporations* fly half? Just the American corporate fleet? On *our* base?"
>
> Rich: "On Mars. Not all on our base — Origin runs their own pads at the launch facility, Stellar runs theirs near the fab district, and there's a handful of smaller players with leased slots. We share airspace, deconflict orbits, and pretend to play nice. Mostly we do."
>
> Jace: [quiet] "Christ."
>
> Rich: [smiling, rueful] "Welcome to Mars. Half of everything is corporate. The half that isn't, we run."

This line lands quietly. Jace files it away — *the corporations fly the rest* — without yet understanding how much it will matter.

### Beat 4: HUD Integration Reveal
One of the operators shows Jace how their HUD overlay layers on top of the physical screens — drone telemetry, robot health status, anomaly flags, predictive maintenance windows. Jace's own HUD attempts to handshake with the room's systems and gets partial access — enough to see the surface layer of what the operators see.

This moment lands hard for Jace: *his* HUD, the one he loves, the one he spent a year's wages upgrading, can do this. He's going to be doing this. He's going to be good at this.

(Reader knows: he won't get the chance.)

### Beat 5: The Glimpse
Through a secure-area window on one side of the room, Jace sees a meeting room: the base commander (a Space Force Colonel, black-and-silver uniform), an unfamiliar woman (Mei), and a nondescript man in civilian clothes (Sylvester). They're seated, talking. The window's glass is opaqued at the lower half; he can see heads but not papers.

Rich notices Jace looking and answers casually, the way he answers most things:

> Rich: "Intel folks. They come and go. I don't ask questions."

Jace files it away. Doesn't think about it again that day.

### Beat 6: Wrap and Forward
Rich pulls Jace away from the screens — Jace would stand and watch all afternoon if allowed.

> Rich: "Come on. You'll have plenty of time to memorize this. Lunch, then I'm dropping you at your quarters or you'll fall over."

[Rich finishes his coffee on the walk to the door. Refills from his canister. The canister is large.]

They exit. Jace looks back once at the wall of feeds before the door closes.

## Character States

### Jace (entry → exit)
- **Entry:** Tired but adrenalized from the tour, military mode engaged, slightly overwhelmed but functional
- **Exit:** Genuinely thrilled. This is the job he wanted. Confidence reinforced. Naïveté reinforced too. *Slightly more aware that the corporations are everywhere.*

### Rich (entry → exit)
- **Entry:** Enjoying himself, watching Jace's reactions, taking quiet pride in the workplace, working through his canister of coffee like always
- **Exit:** Even fonder of Jace, also more aware of how invested Jace already is — which makes Rich quietly worry about how hard the medical news (tomorrow) might land

## Dialogue Notes

- Rich uses Jace's first name throughout (peer-to-peer)
- Operators use "LT" as the friendly nickname when greeting Jace — Air Force compassionate-junior-officer convention
- Sergeants and senior enlisted treat Jace warmly but maintain the address pattern (LT or Lieutenant + salute when appropriate)
- Rich is **always carrying a large coffee canister** and drinks from it frequently throughout — small character beat, signals his hard-working / mildly insomniac baseline. See `/knowledge/characters/char_lt_rich_cullivan.md` for the full canister habit canon.

- **HUD-as-command-tool** — the deeper meaning of Jace's HUD beyond personal use

- **Rich's coffee canister** — small recurring character beat; reads as work-ethic and quiet insomnia
