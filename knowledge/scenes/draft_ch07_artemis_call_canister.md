# Chapter 7 — The Artemis Call

> **Epigraph (staged — author-approved 2026-07-15; add to spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but the loneliest distance in the solar system is the last one percent. Six years, I couldn't reach my sister. Now she was three weeks away, and I still couldn't reach my sister."*

The call window was eleven minutes long. Orbital mechanics had decided this, consulting no one.

Artemis appeared on his HUD in real-enough time — the lag down to two seconds now, close enough for actual conversation, which after four months of recorded messages felt indecently luxurious — and she looked, Jace thought, like someone doing an excellent impression of his sister. The smile was right. The crooked angle of it was right. Something behind it was working harder than it used to.

"There he is. Training Mode himself."

"You've seen it."

"Jace. *Everyone's* seen it. My boss has seen it. My boss doesn't watch anything and she's seen it." She laughed, and for a second the impression became the real thing. "How's the dog?"

"Asleep on my pillow. How's Metis?"

"Busy." A beat, a fraction too long. "Good busy. We're building things you wouldn't believe. Some of our ships have the biggest engines in the belt now — actually, don't worry about that. Point is, work's good."

"That's the kind of sentence people say when I should worry."

"It's the kind of sentence people say when their little brother worries professionally." She waved it off with a hand he noticed had a fresh scar across two knuckles. "You couldn't come here anyway — Metis is closed to civilians, it's all shipyard and clearances. But three weeks, Jace. Fortuna's three weeks from me. That's *nothing*. That's next door. We'll figure out a visit."

"On whose clearance?"

"On my charm." The smile again, crooked, working hard. "Speaking of your new home — you hear about the missing girl? On Fortuna?"

Something cold touched the back of his neck. "No."

"It's been going around the belt boards. Local woman, went missing off the station a little while back — just gone, apparently, no suit cycled, nothing. People were saying murder for about a day, but there's no body, no anything, so now they're saying accident, or she stowed away on an outbound, or—" she shrugged. "Small stations, big rumors. I only mention it because you're about to live there and you collect worries. Consider it a gift."

"Copy," he said. "One pre-owned worry."

"Oh — before the window closes. Maureen's launching our supply container this week, the big consolidated one. I'm the receiving officer on this end, so if anything in it is broken, I will officially know exactly who to blame."

"The cargo canisters are rated to fifty g. Nothing will be broken."

"Famous last words from Training Mode." The window countdown pulsed red behind her image. Nine seconds. Her face did the complicated thing again, all the way through this time. "Three weeks away, little brother. Closest we've been in six years."

"Closest we've—"

The window closed.

He sat for a while in the quiet, in the shush of the water in the walls, holding eleven minutes in his hands and finding it weighed almost nothing. Three weeks away. He could practically wave. He went and found his dog, because that was a thing that could be held.

---

The canister launch was, officially, the most routine event of the transit: a standard thirteen-meter cargo container, packed and sealed, kicked off the *Falcon*'s spine on a slow transfer trajectory to Metis while the ship itself continued to Fortuna. Delta-v budget: trivial. Maureen's involvement: a signature. The container would coast for weeks and arrive with the majesty of all freight, which is to say, nobody would look at it twice.

Jace found this unacceptable.

The idea arrived fully formed while he was reviewing the canister's spec sheet — the standardized berth interface, the same 13-meter module format as the ship's own pods, the blunt cylindrical aft face that would be pointed, for the entire multi-week transit, directly back along its departure vector. Directly, as it were, at everyone it was leaving behind.

He modeled it in an evening. He printed it over three shifts in the good plastic — and it had to be the good plastic; the cheap stock would outgas in vacuum and go brittle and chalky in weeks under raw UV, and if a thing was worth doing it was worth doing in a material rated for the environment. This was, he told himself, an engineering decision. He ran the numbers on mounting stress during the departure kick, selected bond points on non-structural aft plating, and confirmed with the spec that external aesthetic modifications under two kilograms required no approval he intended to seek.

The prototype comms board — warm, therefore functional — got one last transit job: a proximity trigger, so the finishing touch activated only after separation, outside the ship's inspection range.

The canister launched on schedule. It cleared the *Falcon*, oriented itself for the long coast to Metis, and presented its aft face to the ship's cameras: a large, anatomically enthusiastic, salmon-pink 3D-printed pig's rear end, mounted dead-center, with a small curly tail that — the proximity trigger being Jace's — began, at that moment, to slowly rotate.

For several weeks, a container of precision shipyard supplies would sail toward a classified military station mooning it the entire way.

Mei watched the departure footage in the galley with no expression at all, for a long time. Then she said, "The pigs would approve," and left, and Jace decided that counted as the best review of his engineering career.

He did not tell Artemis. Some gifts you let arrive.
