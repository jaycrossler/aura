# Scene — The Doctor's Office

## Purpose

The first pivotal path-change of Book 1. Jace's arrival arc has built him up at peak golden-boy confidence; this scene strips that identity in a single conversation. It is the inciting incident for the entire book. It must land.

It also plants several plot threads:
- The bone disease as a recurring physical and emotional constraint
- The state of communications with Odysseus (Artemis subplot)
- Athena's status (already cleared — the small mercy)
- The HUD as a separate wound

## Position in Sequence

After the arrival, the messages home, the Rich tour, and the gym scene. This is the pivot. Everything before it has set up the height of Jace's confidence; everything after is the fall.

## Setting

U.S. Air Force Mars Detachment medical facility. Clinical, cool, familiar. A standard exam room with a screen displaying scan imagery the doctor will reference. Soft hum of ventilation. The kind of room Jace has been in a hundred times for routine physicals — until this one.

## Characters Present

- Jace Grant (POV)
- The Mars Detachment medical officer (TBD — name and characterization to develop; should be experienced, not unkind, used to delivering hard news but not numbed to it)

## Emotional Beats

1. **Routine confidence.** Jace walks in expecting clearance. He has been running daily PT, his vitals are solid, he knows his numbers. He is mildly cocky — friendly with the medical staff, joking about the gravity adjustment. Maybe makes a comment about his strength relative to the Mars-born he saw at the gym.

2. **The shift.** Doctor's tone changes when she pulls up his bone scan. Not emergency-bad. "We found something." A slight pause that Jace doesn't immediately read as serious.

3. **The information.** Doctor explains: a degenerative bone disease, genetic origin, no known cure. Visible on the scan as small structural anomalies — micro-fractures and cavity formation, what the imaging team has informally been calling "bubbles" in the bone matrix. The condition causes progressive brittleness over decades.

4. **Mechanical questions.** Jace, still in shock, asks clarifying questions in his military-cadenced voice. "Will I be in pain?" *Not at this stage. Not for years.* "Can it be fixed?" *Treatments slow it. There is no cure.* "Will I be hurt now?" *No.*

5. **The Mars-vs-Earth pivot.** Doctor explains the gravity dimension. On Mars, with daily mandatory exercise and the lower gravitational load, Jace will be functionally fine for a long time — possibly decades. *"You can live a long, healthy life on Mars."* But returning to Earth, where bone load is more than twice what it is here, would accelerate the disease catastrophically. Five to ten years on Earth and his bones would begin failing in ways no treatment could fix. Returning to Earth gravity for any extended period is medically inadvisable.

6. **The implication landing.** Jace processes it slowly. Mars is fine. Earth is not. He understands what this means before the doctor says it out loud: he cannot serve in the active military, where any deployment could put him in higher-gravity environments, in centrifuge training, in combat scenarios with rapid acceleration. He cannot go home. The discharge is not a question, it is a mechanical certainty.

7. **The "why now" question.** Jace asks the question that has been gnawing at him: why was this not caught before? He went through medical screening for the Air Force. For the Mars Detachment. For the HUD surgery.

8. **The scanning explanation.** Doctor explains that the imaging suite at Mars Detachment is current-generation. Earth military medical, where Jace was last screened, is using equipment two generations older. The disease produces signatures only the newest scanners can resolve. *"This is a new diagnosis category. We're catching cases we couldn't have caught five years ago."* A small bitter mercy: it isn't anyone's negligence. It just is.

9. **The sister mentions.** Doctor adds, gently, that because the disease has a genetic component, the protocol is to screen first-degree relatives. She has already filed two requests. Athena's screening was done by her university health service — clear. *Athena does not have it.* The first thing in this conversation that has been good news.

10. **The Artemis problem.** The second request, for Artemis at Odysseus Station, has been filed but the comms situation is making it complicated. *"The signal to Odysseus has been bad lately. We've sent the request through their station medical office. If they receive it, they'll run the scans on their equipment and send results back. But..."* She trails off. Both of them know.

11. **The HUD blow.** Almost as a footnote — but devastating in its own register — the doctor mentions the HUD. Military equipment. Since he is being discharged, the device will be deactivated remotely. *"The hardware stays in your head. We can remove it surgically later if you choose, but most people just leave it. With deactivation, it becomes inert."* Jace watches the overlay go dark in real time during the meeting. He says nothing. Says "copy" when she asks if he understands.

12. **The terms.** Honorable discharge on Mars. Two years served of the planned twenty means the educational debt forgiveness is at 10% — he will owe approximately one million credits. No return-cycler berth is provided. Thirty days to vacate base housing. Medical stipend and severance fall well short of his obligations.

13. **The doctor's last attempt at care.** She says she's sorry. Means it. Tries to remind him that medically he is fine — *he can have a long life here.* Tells him to take a few days. Tells him about resources for transitioning servicemembers. Hands him a folder he will not read for two weeks.

14. **He walks out.** Hallway, stairwell, cold corridor toward his quarters. The HUD overlay is gone; the world looks washed. He passes another LT, who nods. He nods back. He doesn't go to his quarters. He walks the ring corridor of the base for ninety minutes. He has no job, no passage home, a million-credit debt, and no idea what to tell his parents.

## Tone and Pacing Notes

- **Slow start, slow finish.** This scene cannot be rushed. The reader needs to feel the news land in real time.
- **Clinical vocabulary, human delivery.** The doctor uses technical terms but is not cold. The horror is in the content, not the delivery.
- **Jace's verbal control.** He gets *more* military-cadenced as the news lands. Says "copy" instead of "okay." Says "negative" once. The crack is in what he doesn't say, not what he does.
- **No tears in the room.** The breakdown comes later, in private. In the room he is professional. The reader should feel the breakdown coming.
- **HUD-as-coda.** The HUD deactivation is the fourth-worst thing in this scene, and that fact alone tells the reader how bad the rest of it is.

## What This Scene Establishes

- **The medical reality.** Bone disease. Mars-okay. Earth-no. Sets the body-as-prison theme that runs through the series.
- **The Athena beat.** Small mercy, but a real one. The reader exhales for a second.
- **The Artemis thread.** The comms problem is now confirmed by an institutional source. Not just family latency — something is actually wrong.
- **The HUD wound.** Sets up the Sylvester deal later: the HUD reactivation becomes a real lever.
- **The financial catastrophe.** Million-credit debt. Sets up the smuggler relationship and the Origin contract.
- **Jace stripped.** The protagonist who walks out of this room is no longer who he was when he walked in.
