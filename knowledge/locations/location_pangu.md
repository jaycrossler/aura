# Pangu - Chinese Industrial City on Mars

## Summary

Pangu is the primary Chinese city on Mars, roughly **50 years old** and home to about **7,000 permanent residents**. It is located near prime mining and industrial territories, with strong links to fuel production, heavy metals, regolith processing, and rail freight. If Terminus is the older U.S. regional capital and Elysium is the international business city, Pangu is Mars's planned industrial engine.

Pangu is essential to the Martian economy. It produces a large share of refined fuel, industrial feedstocks, heavy metals, and bulk components required to keep surface transport, orbital logistics, and asteroid-belt supply chains functioning.

## Atmosphere and environment

Pangu's reputation in Terminus and Elysium is harsher than the city itself. Outsiders describe it as dirty, secretive, quota-driven, and polluted. Some of that is prejudice and political theater. Some of it is true. Pangu handles work that no city can make pretty: fuel refinement, mineral extraction, heavy industry, regolith handling, and industrial waste control.

The city is newer and more standardized than old Terminus. It has fewer patched pioneer tunnels and more modular, repeatable infrastructure. Corridors are wider, signage is more uniform, traffic flows are more controlled, and robotic systems feel more centrally coordinated.

Pangu can feel cleaner in public spaces while still dirtier at the industrial edge. Its transit concourses may look disciplined and well-maintained while its fuel districts smell of hot seals, machine oil, and oxidizer safety culture.

## Political and social character

Pangu is managed through Chinese state and corporate structures. It likely has tighter central planning, more visible surveillance, more synchronized logistics, and stronger integration between housing, work, transport, and industrial production than Terminus.

Terminus rumors often claim brutal quotas or forced labor. Treat these as politically charged claims unless a specific story source verifies them. A more grounded model: Pangu has high discipline, less tolerated disorder, restricted information flows, strong state labor expectations, and a civic culture that prizes coordinated productivity.

## Sensory baseline

**Sound.** More synchronized and mechanical than Terminus. Rail announcements, cargo carts, airlock cycles, cleaning bots, factory hum, and public alert tones. Public spaces suppress noise better than industrial zones.

**Smell.** Public concourses smell filtered and neutral. Industrial districts smell faintly of lubricants, hot electronics, oxidizer discipline, dust, and chemical scrubbers. Outsiders exaggerate the smell into jokes about greasy fuel film on coffee.

**Light.** Public areas use bright, uniform, cool-white light with red and white signage. Industrial spaces are high-bay, bright, and marked by safety colors.

**Motion.** Pedestrian traffic follows lanes. Cargo bots and humans share corridors with more explicit separation than in Terminus. Low-g movement is disciplined; handrails and foot loops appear where traffic pauses.
