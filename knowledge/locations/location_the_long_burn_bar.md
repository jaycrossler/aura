# The Long Burn — Bar, Terminus Underground

## Overview

The Long Burn is a bar in the old tunnel commercial zone of Terminus, underground at Level 1–2. It is not the lowest bar in Terminus — it has booths, it has a proper counter, it has a synthesizer unit old enough that the menu is limited but the prices are honest. It is not a place for people who are doing well, but it is not a place for people who have completely given up either. The regulars are workers, agricultural staff, lower-ranked contractors, and the occasional person from above ground who has decided they don't want to be recognized tonight.

The Rat has a usual spot. He has had it for years. No one sits there when he isn't in, even when he isn't in for a month.

---

## Location and access

Level 1–2 old commercial tunnel, approximately 400 meters from the nearest lift cluster. The tunnel here is original construction — the walls have been surface-treated but you can still see the original drill marks if you know where to look. The corridor outside The Long Burn is wider than average for this level: a former transit junction that was repurposed when the new lift shafts made it redundant.

Access: two entrances. The main entrance off the former junction corridor (heavy pressure door, always propped slightly open when the bar is running). A secondary entrance from a narrower service corridor behind — known to regulars, not hidden but not obvious.

---

## Physical space

A single large room, roughly rectangular with an alcove at the back that gets used for overflow on heavy nights. The bar counter runs along the left wall — salvaged composite material that has taken decades of glass rings and elbows without complaint. Eight stools, mismatched. Three are comfortable. The others are fine.

Booths along the right wall: six of them, each able to seat four if everyone is willing to be close. The booth nearest the back alcove has a privacy partition — a repurposed acoustic panel from somewhere in the base's construction history — that makes it slightly more enclosed than the others. The Rat's booth.

Free-standing tables in the center floor: four, each with mismatched chairs. This is the casual zone — short-stay, people passing through, groups that haven't decided how long they're staying.

Ceiling height is lower than the newer tunnel sections: approximately 2.4 meters, just enough to not feel oppressive but not enough to feel open. Overhead lighting is the original yellow-spectrum fluorescent tubes from the first decade of Terminus construction. Some are dimmed. One at the far end has a flicker that nobody has bothered to fix in two years. The bar's own lighting supplements with strip LED along the counter edge — a warm amber that makes everyone look slightly more alive than they feel.

## Sensory palette

**Sight:** Low, warm light with a yellow-amber cast. The ceiling feels closer than it is. Everything is slightly worn — not dirty, just used. The bar counter has the patina of something that has absorbed decades of conversations. The synth unit behind the bar is mounted into the wall, screen scratched but functional, menu limited to about 40 items.

**Sound:** The life support hum is present here but slightly masked by two things: the rumble of the synth unit doing its work, and the ambient sound of people talking. The Long Burn is never loud. It is a bar where conversations happen at table volume. Music plays sometimes — low, usually something with a slow rhythm. When it's on, it's from a pre-compiled playlist on the bar's local system. It stops at random intervals and nobody notices for a few minutes.

**Smell:** This is the most distinctive thing about The Long Burn. The air here has the recycled-mineral baseline of all the underground tunnels, but layered over it: the warm sweetness of the synth alcohol (a slightly artificial grain smell, slightly warm), the faint organic smell of people who have been working, and — very faintly, because the circulation in this tunnel section is slightly sluggish — the accumulated ghost of years of the same. It smells like a bar that has been a bar for a long time. Not unpleasant. Specific. Something the above-ground places don't have.

**Touch:** The bar counter is smooth and slightly cool. The booth upholstery is a dense foam composite — it gives just enough. The pressure door at the main entrance requires a deliberate push; it doesn't swing easily.

**Temperature:** The Long Burn runs slightly warmer than the tunnel corridor outside — the synth unit and body heat accumulate here. Not uncomfortable. Specifically: it feels like a room where people have been for a while.

## Social texture and atmosphere

The Long Burn has the atmosphere of a place that knows what it is and has stopped trying to be anything else. The regulars have a working familiarity with each other — not warmth exactly, but recognition. People here are often tired. They are often frustrated. They are occasionally angry in the specific way of people who cannot change the thing they are angry about.

This is where belt anomaly rumors circulate, when they circulate at all. The censorship effects of belt anomaly topic suppression are felt here before they are felt in the above-ground bars: topics that were discussable two months ago become not-discussed here first. The regulars notice. They don't talk about noticing.

The people who come here are not generally military officers, not generally Origin Industries management, not generally the people whose careers are intact. They are the workers, the long-timers, the people on rotation who got placed in underground residential and have started to feel like they fit here. The supply-chain workers who drink here know things about what isn't arriving before anyone else does.

The bar staff is two people: the owner (runs days) and one bar worker (runs nights). The owner knows everyone by sight, most by name, and will not repeat what they hear. This is known and trusted.

---

## The Rat's booth

The booth nearest the back alcove. Slightly more private than the others due to the acoustic partition. The Rat sits facing the entrance — always. He can see both entrances from this position. He is usually alone. If he is not alone, the person with him is also always someone who isn't drawing attention to themselves.

When the Rat is not present, this booth sits empty. There is no explicit reservation. Nobody takes it. It is just understood.

---

## The bar at different times

**Early evening (shift change):** Most crowded. The above-ground noise is some distance away and this is where the transition workers decompress. Belt anomaly conversations happen most freely now, with enough ambient noise to provide cover.

**Late evening:** Quieter. The regulars who stay are the ones with nowhere to be or something on their mind. The Rat is often here at this hour. The conversations at this hour are shorter and more specific.

**Slow afternoons:** Sometimes nearly empty. Two or three people. The synth unit hum is more noticeable. This is when the barkeep talks to the people who need to talk.
