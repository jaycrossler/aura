# Metis Station

## Overview

Metis Station is a working shipyard anchored to 9 Metis, a large S-type asteroid in the inner belt at approximately 2.39 AU from the Sun. 9 Metis is roughly 222 km in diameter, large enough to provide a stable anchoring body and extensive metal-rich silicate ore. Its surface gravity is tiny by Earth standards but useful by asteroid standards because the rock provides a fixed reference frame for ship construction.

Metis is not a second Fortuna. Fortuna is the regional hub, food system, manufacturing town, market, and political center. Metis is a shipyard with a settlement attached. It is smaller, more specialized, rougher, and much less interested in appearing welcoming.

The station began as a salvage outpost, picking over debris and derelict materials associated with a collision cluster near 9 Metis's orbital neighborhood. Over decades it accumulated repair bays, hull cradles, fabrication capacity, pressure test rigs, and a crew culture centered on making hard machines work. It is now the most capable shipyard in the outer inner-belt neighborhood, which is not the same thing as being famous or well-funded.

It is run, for practical purposes, by Dr. Eugene Hart. He did not found it, but he has shaped it for long enough that most people treat the distinction as academic.

## Relationship to Fortuna Station

Fortuna designated Metis as a regional shipyard partner early in its hub-building strategy. The relationship is practical:
- Fortuna supplies food, animal products, refined feedstock, people, parts, medical depth, and political cover.
- Metis builds, overhauls, and repairs ships that Fortuna needs.
- Fortuna uses Metis as a pressure valve for construction work too awkward, dangerous, or specialized to perform inside Fortuna's rings.
- Metis depends on Fortuna for goods it cannot produce locally.

Some Fortuna residents know Artemis Grant by reputation before Jace arrives. She is the ship architect at the place that fixes Fortuna's ships.

Dimensional travel distances from Fortuna to Metis:
- Physical space: about 80 million km in the working story model.
- Astral distance under distance-squared compression: about 9,000 km.
- Hyperspace distance under distance-cubed compression: about 450 km.

The 450 km Hyperspace distance is the basis for the Arc 2 hike. At sustained Hyperspace travel pace, two days is brutal but possible.

## Personnel

### Dr. Eugene Hart

De facto technical director. Large, belligerent, indispensable. Built the core architecture of the {Aura} system as a graduate project, released it publicly under his own name, and has been mostly uninterested in what others did with it since.

Hart runs Metis because running a shipyard suits his need for hard problems and minimal bureaucracy. He does not manage people well. He manages engineering problems extraordinarily well.

### Artemis Grant

Ship architect, Jace's older sister. Has been at Metis long enough that she and Hart have a functional working relationship based on mutual professional respect and avoidance of anything personal.

She is the reason the station's designs work as systems rather than as separate engineering tricks.

### Station Crew

Small, skilled, self-selecting. People come to Metis because they want to build ships without being told every step by corporate policy. Turnover is high among newcomers who last less than three months and low among those who stay.

## Physical Layout

Metis is built into and onto 9 Metis's surface rather than orbiting freely. Low surface gravity makes anchoring practical and provides a stable reference frame for precision fabrication. Orbital platforms drift, wobble, and require constant station-keeping. Rock does not.

### The Yards

External construction bays cut into or anchored onto the asteroid surface. The Yards currently hold two partially completed hulls and the skeleton of a third. Hull sections sit in giant cradles, held by clamps, tethers, gantries, and vacuum-rated construction robots.

Visual traits:
- Long hull frames under work lights.
- Dust-dark asteroid rock.
- Anchor cables and guide rails.
- Robotic welders.
- Suit crews moving slowly along tethers.
- Pressure test tanks.
- Temporary shelters.
- Tool lockers bolted to rock.
- Warning beacons rather than decorative lights.

### The Rock

Pressurized tunnels and chambers bored into 9 Metis. This is the primary residential and operational space. It is shielded by mass overhead and feels more like a mine, submarine, and workshop than a town.

The Rock includes:
- Crew quarters.
- Galley.
- Medical room.
- Control rooms.
- Fabrication shops.
- Storage vaults.
- Emergency shelters.
- Pressure test rooms.
- Dormitory tunnels.
- Old salvage chambers converted to living space.

### The Skin

Surface-mounted fabrication equipment, solar collectors, radiators, comms arrays, suit access points, and exposed mechanical systems. Maintenance requires EVA or sealed rovers.

### The Throat

Main pressurized docking corridor. Metis receives about one supply run per month from Fortuna and intermittent visits from independent operators. The Throat is not polished. It is practical, cramped by cargo, and marked by constant loading.

### Ship Assembly Spine

A linear industrial axis connecting design offices, parts warehouses, pressure test cells, assembly airlocks, and the exterior Yards. It is the closest thing Metis has to a main street, but nobody says that in front of Hart.

## Scale and Population Logic

Metis should feel smaller than Fortuna but strategically heavier than its population suggests.

Working model:
- Permanent residents: roughly 80 to 150.
- Transients: 20 to 80, depending on shipyard load.
- Active robots and drones: 1,000 to 3,000, mostly industrial, surface, and inspection units.
- Docked vessels: usually several small craft, one or two major hulls under work, and construction frames in the Yards.

Metis does not need Fortuna's schools, parks, and broad civic mix. It needs:
- Shipyard operations.
- Worker housing.
- Strong emergency response.
- Medical stabilization.
- Food storage.
- Fabrication.
- Surface construction.
- Spare parts.
- Shielded shelters.
- Enough social infrastructure to keep specialists from leaving.

## Daily Life

Metis daily life is ruled by shipyard rhythms, not civic rhythms. People sleep around test windows, hull rotations, cargo arrivals, and pressure certifications.

Social spaces are limited:
- One main galley.
- A small bar or off-shift room.
- A narrow exercise room.
- A few private workshop corners.
- Informal seating niches in old tunnels.
- A quiet room mostly used by people who claim not to use quiet rooms.

The station is not joyless. It is full of hard humor, technical pride, grudges, private projects, and people who would be miserable in a clean corporate settlement.

## Visual and Sensory Texture

Metis smells like metal, dust, lubricant, old insulation, welding residue, machine heat, and stored food. It has fewer plants than Fortuna and fewer children. Its lighting is practical. Its corridors are narrower. Its robots are larger and more dangerous.

The station soundscape includes:
- Ventilation.
- distant grinding.
- tools.
- clamps.
- alarms being tested.
- robots moving on exterior rails.
- pressure pumps.
- crew arguments that are half engineering review and half social ritual.

## Arc 2 - The Siege

When dimensional fauna breach the local Astral and begin targeting the station, Metis goes dark. The distress message Jace receives is partial and degraded: enough to confirm Artemis is alive and the situation is critical, not enough to show the full scope.

By the time the rescue mission arrives:
- The crew has improvised defensive positions throughout the tunnels.
- Artemis has sustained a serious leg injury. It is functional in low-g but not walk-out-on-your-own functional.
- Hart has been fighting alongside the crew and is furious, exhausted, and unwilling to concede that any of the situation exceeded his ability to solve given more time.
- Several crew members have begun manifesting early Will-adjacent abilities under extreme stress.
- The shipyard's half-built hulls, pressure doors, test cells, and maintenance tunnels have become defensive terrain.

The station held because the people who built it knew every corner of it.

## Image and Scene Prompts

### Prompt 1 - The Yards

A low-gravity shipyard on the surface of asteroid 9 Metis, with two partially completed spacecraft hulls held in giant anchored cradles. The asteroid surface is dark, dusty, and uneven. Vacuum-rated gantries, robotic welders, pressure test rigs, tether rails, tool lockers, and work lights surround the hulls. Suit crews move slowly on tethers while large construction robots handle heavy components. The scene should look like a real shipyard adapted to vacuum, not a sleek space fantasy dock.

### Prompt 2 - The Rock Corridor

A pressurized tunnel inside Metis Station, about 3 meters wide with rough rock behind reinforced panels, exposed but orderly conduits, scuffed floor plates, and pressure doors with hand-marked maintenance notes. Workers in heavy coveralls and emergency breathers pass a cargo robot carrying hull brackets. Lighting is practical and slightly harsh. The space feels like a mine, machine shop, and submarine combined.

### Prompt 3 - The Throat

The main docking corridor at Metis Station during a Fortuna supply arrival. Cargo pallets, sealed food crates, oxygen bottles, ship parts, and personal duffels crowd the walls. A pressure door cycles in the distance. Crew members check manifests while robots move crates. The corridor is functional, crowded, and slightly dirty, with no attempt at civic beauty.

### Prompt 4 - Ship Design Room

A cramped ship design room inside Metis Station, with rugged screens, physical printouts, hull diagrams, spare parts, mugs, cable trays, and a large table covered in system schematics. Artemis Grant and other engineers could plausibly work here. The room is practical, slightly chaotic, and deeply competent.

### Prompt 5 - Siege Tunnel

A Metis tunnel converted into an improvised defensive position. Tool carts, pressure panels, cargo crates, welding screens, and emergency lights create barricades. The lighting is low and harsh. Workers look exhausted but disciplined. The scene should feel like a shipyard under siege using its own infrastructure as defense.
