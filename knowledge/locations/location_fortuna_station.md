# Fortuna Station

## Summary

Fortuna Station is anchored to 3 Fortuna, a large carbonaceous asteroid in the inner belt. It is an American-anchored station with strong Origin Industries corporate management, positioned to become the primary agricultural hub of the inner belt. It is in growth phase at story start — actively expanding habitat, integrating new ferry shipments, building out its livestock capacity. Jace arrives as part of a substantial settler and animal shipment wave, the latest of several that have been arriving over the past two years.

It is not glamorous. It smells of recycled air, metal, and, increasingly, animals. The settlers who have been here two years already call the newcomers "greenies." Jace is a greenie.

## Geography and Layout

Fortuna Station is not on the asteroid's surface — it is a co-orbital habitat complex, tethered and station-keeping relative to 3 Fortuna, with the asteroid as a resource base and structural anchor. The asteroid provides: water ice, carbon compounds, silicates for construction, and proximity to mining operations that are the station's primary economic activity.

Key areas:
- **The Hub:** Central zero-gravity core. Command, communications, docking, primary engineering.
- **The Ring:** Two rotating habitat rings providing ~0.35g. Crew quarters, settler housing, medical, recreation.
- **The Barn:** The expanding livestock and agricultural section — the most recently built, smelling newest and most agricultural. The live feeds from the *Falcon*'s animals are eventually piped here. A community gathering point.
- **The Pit:** Mining operations coordination center. The economic heart.
- **The Market:** Informal trade and social space that has emerged organically from a weekly goods exchange.

## The Barn and Animal Culture

Fortuna was specifically chosen for livestock development because of its C-type composition (water ice access, organics for feed synthesis) and because the station needed food autonomy. The livestock program is central to Fortuna's long-term survival plan.

When the *Falcon*'s pig-cams and animal live feeds are patched into station screens, they become the most-watched content on the station almost immediately. People check on "their" pigs and goats the way Earth people checked social media — habitually, emotionally, as a source of normalcy. After the communications blackout cuts off external media, the animal feeds become the primary non-work entertainment. Station culture develops around particular animals: the pig who figured out the feeder mechanism, the goat who kept escaping her pen, the cattle calf that a handler named Duchess.

This is both charming and thematically significant. The animals are the uncomplicated life in a complicated world.

## The Blackout and Isolation

When communications with Mars and Earth are formally restricted, Fortuna Station's 300-450 residents become, effectively, a small isolated town. They have each other, their Auras, the stored media they brought, and the animal feeds. The formal communication links are gone; the belt-internal low-bandwidth network remains but is limited.

The isolation forces community in ways the corporate management structure was not designed to facilitate. The informal social structures that emerge — the market, the barn gatherings, the nightly common-area meals that become traditional — are the seeds of what will eventually be belter culture.

## The Anomalies at Fortuna

Fortuna is in a relatively high-flux zone for inner-belt standards. Anomalies began appearing here several months before Jace's arrival, reported sporadically and mostly suppressed by management (who categorized them as equipment issues pending investigation). By the time Jace arrives, every experienced station resident has a story they don't tell officially.

The first major on-station magical event — the one that cannot be suppressed — occurs in the months following Jace's arrival. See event files for details.

## Cultural Character

At story start, Fortuna culture is a mix of American frontier-corporate and genuine belter emerging identity. Origin Industries is present and visible; its management style is relatively benign but clearly extractive. The workers and settlers are developing their own norms: practical, direct, suspicious of anything that smells like Earth politics, proud of what they are building.

Cross-cultural texture is present. The American majority includes significant Chinese-American, South Asian-American, and Latin American populations. Several settlers came from non-American Mars backgrounds and hold dual cultural membership. Mei's cover as a Chinese-heritage farmer is plausible here; there are others like her.

## Sensory Texture

- **Visual:** Industrial, unfinished, busy. Temporary-looking fixtures that have become permanent. The Barn section is warmer and more chaotic than the rest.
- **Sound:** The station has its own hum — different from the Falcon's hum. Slightly lower, with a rhythmic component from the ring rotation. Animal sounds from the Barn section carry further than anyone planned.
- **Smell:** Recycled air is the baseline everywhere. The Barn adds organic complexity. The Hub smells like metal and ozone.
