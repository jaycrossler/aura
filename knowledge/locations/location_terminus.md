# Terminus — Mars City (U.S. 56th State)

## Overview

Terminus is the largest human settlement on Mars and the primary U.S. presence on the planet. Of the 12,000+ people currently living on Mars across three large cities, approximately 7,200 live in Terminus — roughly 60 percent of the total Mars population. The other two cities are not yet named. 

Terminus is where all events of book 01 take place prior to Jace's departure on the Falcon.

Terminus is the beating heart of the United States' presence on Mars, officially recognized as the 56th state. Located on the Martian equator to optimize orbital launches, it houses roughly 60% of the planet's human population. It is a bustling, heavily tracked, and highly militarized commercial hub that feels like a blend of a high-tech frontier town and a sprawling military base.

## Infrastructure and Atmosphere

Terminus is a city of contrasts. Parts of the settlement are 60 to 80 years old, requiring constant patching, robotic maintenance, and cobbling together of mismatched parts. Newer sectors are shiny, corporate, and pristine. 
* **The Redwood:** The centerpiece of the arrival Observation Dome is a massive, genetically adapted Earth redwood tree, heavily cultivated to provide incoming colonists with a sense of awe and life.
* **Military Presence:** The U.S. Space Force, Air Force, and Army maintain a massive footprint here. The military operates the heavy transport drones, oversees orbital logistics, and is deeply integrated into the daily corporate life of the city.
* **The Economy:** Terminus operates on a hyper-capitalist model. Everyone works, and there is a constant demand for skilled human labor. While no one is in absolute poverty due to basic safety nets, the wealth disparity is stark. 

## Daily Life

Citizens of Terminus are tracked relentlessly. Every breath of air and drop of water is metered. The streets and tunnels are filled with a mix of towering, low-gravity-adapted Mars-born citizens, Earth-born immigrants struggling with the timeslip, and thousands of robotic assistants polishing, fixing, and cleaning the omnipresent red dust.

Beyond the strictly utilitarian, Terminus contains a number of specialty domes built for cultural and emotional value as much as for production. The Coffee Tree Dome cultivates the famously expensive Mars coffee in conditions tuned for the trees — warmer, with a slightly altered atmospheric mix. The Bird Dome runs at higher air pressure and is built tall to give its birds room to fly; entry is 100 credits per person, geese hatch on a year-round livestream that draws millions of viewers, and the dome is a small but durable bright spot in Mars cultural life. These domes are part of why, despite the planet's harshness, Terminus feels like a place people want to stay.

## Physical layout

Terminus is split between two distinct environments that feel, smell, and sound completely different:

### Above-ground (the Domes)

Half of Terminus's inhabited space sits above the Martian surface under a series of UV-protective glass domes — some large enough to qualify as enclosed neighborhoods, others spanning entire functional zones (farms, industrial, residential). The dome glass is tinted slightly amber against UV radiation and gives all above-ground outdoor views a faint warmth that is disconcerting to new arrivals who expect Martian light to be cold.

**Dome types:**
- **Residential domes** — mid-rise prefab housing, green corridors, communal spaces. Designed to feel as little like a sealed vessel as possible.
- **Farm domes** — vast, some of the largest structures on Mars. Full-spectrum grow lighting running on Mars time. The air in the farm domes is noticeably different from the rest of Terminus: warmer, humid, with the smell of growing things pressing against the recycled-air baseline. Visible from across the city. The domes glow at night.
- **Commercial/transit domes** — older construction, patchwork repair, higher foot traffic.

The above-ground view from any dome: rust-red ground, the other domes reflecting light, the horizon closer than Earth's, occasional dust devils at the dome perimeter. At night, the supernova is visible on the horizon.

### Underground (the Tunnels)

The other half of Terminus is carved into the rock — tunnel networks at multiple levels, some original pioneer infrastructure from the first decade of settlement, some newer and wider. The tunnels run deep enough in places to be structurally significant: whole neighborhoods, the full livestock and processing operation, much of the industrial and maintenance infrastructure, and large portions of the working-class residential zones.

**Tunnel characteristics:**
- Older tunnels: narrower, rougher walls, lower ceilings, original conduit runs exposed along the ceiling, a feeling of compression and age. The smell here is unmistakably recycled — scrubbed air with a metallic undertone, the faint smell of rock. Nothing organic.
- Newer tunnels: widened, smoothed, better lighting, some with painted murals or surface texture to reduce claustrophobia.
- The deep tunnels: Below Level 3, things get older and quieter. These are the original digs — functional, minimal, used mostly by maintenance, agriculture support, and the people who don't care much for above-ground pricing.

**Levels:** Terminus uses a level system — Level 1 is shallowest (transition zones between surface and tunnel), deeper levels go down from there. The livestock operation sits at Level 2-3. The Long Burn bar is in a Level 1-2 old commercial tunnel area.

## Population and demographics

~7,200 permanent residents plus a rotating population of contractors, temporary workers, and military personnel. The permanent population skews younger than Earth average — the bone density issue that ends Jace's career is a known problem; experienced Mars residents have adapted bodies.

**Broad social stratification:**
- **Above-ground dome residential:** Better conditions, typically military, government, or Origin Industries personnel, senior technical staff. More expensive.
- **Below-ground residential:** Workers, lower-ranked military, agricultural staff, service workers, long-timers who prefer the tunnel quiet. Cheaper. The community is tighter.
- **Transient population:** Contractors on rotation, researchers on assignment. Often disoriented for the first weeks.

Terminus has been here long enough to have an *old* quarter — areas of the tunnels where the original construction is clearly decades old, where the maintenance record is patchy, where the bars have regulars and the food stalls have accumulated character. This is where belt anomaly rumors circulate.

## Sensory baseline

The sensory experience of Terminus varies sharply by zone, but these qualities persist across all of it:

**Sound:** Terminus is never fully quiet. The life support system — fans, scrubbers, circulation — creates a constant low background hum that residents stop noticing and new arrivals find unnerving. Above ground, the dome glass occasionally ticks with thermal expansion. Underground, sounds carry differently than on Earth: slightly deadened in the rock sections, slightly reverberant in the processed corridors.

**Smell:** The baseline smell of Terminus is *filtered* — scrubbed air with a faint mineral undertone and the very subtle smell of recycled human presence. It is not unpleasant. It is not organic. New arrivals notice it. Long-timers don't. In the farm domes, the smell changes completely.

**Light:** Above ground, amber-tinted through UV glass. Underground, artificial — functional white lighting in newer areas, older yellow-spectrum lighting in the original tunnels that gives skin a slightly sallow look. The farm domes are full-spectrum and aggressively bright.

**Temperature:** Narrowly regulated, but not perfectly. Dome surfaces run slightly cooler near the glass. Deep tunnels run slightly warmer from equipment heat. The livestock area smells warm. The medical bay is kept cool.
