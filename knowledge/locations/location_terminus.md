# Terminus - Mars City (U.S. 56th State)

## Overview

Terminus is the oldest and largest U.S. settlement on Mars. It is roughly **70 years old** by the story present and holds about **10,000 permanent civilian residents**, plus contractors, transient rail and launch crews, outpost workers, and nearby military personnel. It is one of three major Mars cities: Terminus, Chinese-owned Pangu, and the international/European business city Elysium.

Terminus is where all events of Book 01 take place prior to Jace's departure on the Falcon.

Terminus should not feel like a small dome outpost. It should feel like a **regional survival machine** with a city inside it: buried residential neighborhoods, surface domes, rail logistics, agriculture, livestock, fabrication, launch infrastructure, water and fuel vaults, and an adjacent U.S. military cantonment. It carries the civic age and institutional memory of Old Mars. Its old tunnels preserve the story of survival. Its new districts show money, robotics, and U.S. strategic ambition.

The local rule of thumb: **surface is for work, light, crops, launch, vehicles, and symbolic spaces; underground is for life, storage, medicine, schools, command, and emergency survival.**

## Strategic role

Terminus is more than a settlement of 10,000 people. It supports:

- U.S. government and corporate interests on Mars.
- Regional rail traffic and surface freight.
- Outpost logistics and emergency response.
- A large share of Mars food production.
- Livestock and biological exports to the asteroid belt.
- Launch operations, including a remote rail launch range.
- Heavy drone, robot, truck, and rail maintenance.
- Military-adjacent operations, orbital logistics, and communications.

The city has infrastructure sized above its resident population. A useful planning model is **10,000 permanent civilians, 500-1,500 nearby military personnel, and 500-1,500 transient workers or visitors at any time**. Its systems feel sized for 15,000-20,000 users because Mars cannot improvise capacity during crisis.

## Above-ground domes

Terminus has a number of above-ground domes. They are not simply transparent neighborhoods. They are managed pressure environments with radiation controls, storm shutters, mass shielding, airlocks, dust traps, and life-support obligations.

Leaded or treated glass is common in dome panels, but glass alone is not enough. The safest domes combine:

- Tinted radiation-filtering glass or laminated transparent panels.
- Water-wall columns or water tanks as shielding and thermal mass.
- Regolith berms around lower walls.
- Retractable shutters for solar events.
- Occupancy limits for children, pregnant residents, and high-exposure workers.
- Buried safe cores reachable within minutes.
- Airlocks with dust stripping, suit lockers, and emergency mask racks.

Dome types:

**Residential domes.** Better housing, green corridors, communal spaces, mid-rise prefab blocks, and more expensive air and water allocations. These are not fully safe during major solar events, so residents still have underground shelter assignments.

**Farm domes.** Some of the largest structures on Mars. Full-spectrum grow lighting, high humidity, crop racks, fruit trees, algae/protein systems, seed vault access, and visible water shielding. They glow at night and can be seen from across the city.

**Ranch and animal domes.** Industrial biosecure habitats, not open pasture. The above-ground ranch complexes include stacked animal decks, exercise lanes, feed conveyors, veterinary isolation, aquaculture tanks, methane capture, waste digesters, and processing rooms. The underground livestock facility remains the older and more intimate operational core.

**Commercial/transit domes.** Older construction, patchwork repair, higher foot traffic, restaurants, consumer technology, tour routes, rail access, and civic landmarks.

**Civic showpiece domes.** The Redwood observation space, amphitheater, public garden promenade, and ceremonial areas. These exist for morale and identity as much as function.

The view from a surface dome: rust-red ground, nearby domes reflecting amber light, maintenance robots on exterior seams, rail lines, utility berms, dust devils, remote truck movement, and the horizon feeling closer than Earth's.

## Underground tunnels and buried city

Most residents spend most of their lives underground. The tunnels hold apartment neighborhoods, schools, clinics, shops, bars, logistics routes, utility trunks, water vault access, data centers, emergency shelters, and older industrial spaces.

**Level system:** Level 1 is shallowest and contains transition zones between surface and tunnel. Deeper levels run down from there. The livestock operation sits at Level 2-3. The Long Burn bar sits in an old Level 1-2 commercial tunnel area.

**Older tunnels:** 2.5-4 meters wide in many sections, lower ceilings, rougher walls, exposed but labeled conduits, amber lighting, patched bulkheads, and local knowledge. These areas smell like filtered mineral air and old machinery. They are not unsafe, but they carry age.

**Newer civic corridors:** 5-8 meters wide and 3+ meters high, smoother walls, better lighting, wider cargo lanes, embedded cart tracks, pressure doors at intervals, and public signage. These feel like subway concourses fused with hospitals and ship corridors.

**Utility tunnels:** Narrower, usually 2-3 meters wide, with pipes, heat loops, water mains, data cables, waste systems, and service robots. Access is restricted.

**Emergency tunnels:** Pressure-isolated evacuation routes with masks, water taps, first-aid lockers, battery lights, and route maps. Children learn these before they learn surface geography.

## Storage vaults

Storage vaults define Terminus geography and politics. They are distributed to avoid single points of failure.

Named vault concepts:

- **The Blue Vaults.** Water and ice reserves, shielded corridors, pump rooms, emergency taps.
- **The Long Pantry.** Staple food reserves and ration blocks for Mars-wide crisis support.
- **The Cold Rooms.** Meat, embryos, seed lines, medical biologicals, and microbial cultures.
- **The Red Store.** Regolith blocks, shielding mass, ceramics, and construction feedstock.
- **The Black Tanks.** Methane, oxygen, and industrial gases outside the main city perimeter.
- **The Quiet Archive.** Data, legal records, settlement logs, seed genomes, and institutional memory.

Water is especially strategic because it provides drinking water, crop supply, cooling, fire suppression, radiation shielding, fuel feedstock, medical reserve, and political leverage.

## Agriculture and ranching

Terminus feeds a large share of Mars and exports high-value food and biological assets to the asteroid belt. It does not export much live livestock. More common exports include frozen meat, preserved fats, powdered egg, cheese, cultured meat blocks, microbial protein, embryos, breeding stock, feed cultures, seeds, and luxury cuts.

Common livestock and food systems:

| System | Use |
|---|---|
| Chickens | Eggs, meat, cultural familiarity, efficient protein |
| Rabbits | Compact meat source, emergency protein |
| Goats | Milk, cheese, meat, hardy but odor-heavy |
| Pigs | Meat and waste conversion, biosecurity concerns |
| Sheep | Wool, meat, cultural value, limited use |
| Cattle | High-status and expensive, probably limited herd |
| Fish | Aquaculture protein, waste-cycle integration |
| Insects | Feedstock, emergency protein, socially divisive |
| Lab-grown meat | Industrial baseline, competes with prestige ranch products |

Ranch workers carry their own status and smell. They may see themselves as the true guardians of Mars because food has no replacement during crisis.

## Launch and remote rail launch

Terminus sits on or near the equator to optimize launch operations. It has conventional launch pads, cargo prep areas, and a remote electromagnetic rail launch range separated from the city by distance, berms, empty crash zones, and guarded service corridors.

The rail launch range should mostly handle bulk cargo, rugged orbital transfer payloads, standardized freight containers, propellant tanks, and durable industrial loads. Crewed launches should use gentler systems unless the chapter explicitly explains why humans can tolerate the acceleration profile.

Rail launch components:

- Cargo prep yard.
- Long launch rail across cleared terrain or a slope.
- Power storage banks and grid buffers.
- Hardened launch control bunker.
- Abort and debris zones.
- Recovery yards for reusable sleds and failed payloads.
- Restricted freight line to the city.
- Military security overlay when payloads matter.

The rail launch range should feel like a rail yard, rocket range, and power plant combined.

## Economy and social texture

Terminus operates on a high-tracking, high-work, high-maintenance model. Everyone consumes air, water, heat, space, and spare parts. The city provides safety nets, but it does not feel economically soft.

Social classes often map onto infrastructure:

- **Surface dome residents.** Military officers, government staff, senior technical workers, corporate staff, families with strong allocations.
- **Tunnel residents.** Workers, long-timers, agricultural staff, service workers, junior military, lower-ranked technicians, and people who prefer safety over light.
- **Railhead and contractor residents.** Rotating workers, outpost crews, launch labor, truck drivers, and people with temporary credentials.
- **Old Core residents.** Long-term Mars families, bar owners, retired technicians, unofficial fixers, and people who know which systems fail first.

Wealth appears as private square meters, greenhouse access, quiet airflow, real coffee, better water allocation, private medical priority, surface-view windows, and the ability to avoid bad shifts.

## Sensory baseline

**Sound.** Terminus is never quiet. Fans, scrubbers, pumps, circulation, robot wheels, pressure doors, cargo carts, rail vibration, and distant machinery create a constant baseline. Above ground, dome glass ticks with thermal expansion. Underground, sound deadens in rock sections and reverberates in processed corridors.

**Smell.** The baseline smell is filtered air with a mineral undertone. Farm domes add humidity and plant smell. Livestock areas add warmth, fur, bedding, feed, and disinfectant. Railhead areas smell faintly of dust, lubricants, ozone, and hot electronics.

**Light.** Above ground, amber-tinted through treated glass. Underground, functional white in new corridors and older yellow-spectrum lighting in original tunnels. Farm domes are aggressively full-spectrum.

**Temperature.** Narrowly regulated but not uniform. Dome edges are cooler. Utility tunnels can be warm. Medical spaces run cool. Livestock zones are warm and humid. Surface transitions are cold unless vehicles and locks are heated.

**Dust.** Red dust concentrates at airlocks, truck depots, suit rooms, maintenance bays, vehicle seals, filter carts, and exterior service hatches. Clean interior spaces fight dust constantly.

**Gravity.** Low gravity changes motion. People use handrails, foot loops, cargo straps, seat rails, and deliberate turns. Children raised on Mars move fluidly. Earth-born arrivals overpush and rebound off walls.

## Image prompts and scene vignettes

Use these prompts for chapter imagery or graphics generation.

### Morning corridor in old Terminus

A wide underground pedestrian corridor in the old core of Terminus, Mars, about 6 meters wide and 3 meters high, with a gently arched ceiling cut into regolith and lined with pale composite panels. The floor is smooth, slightly worn, and marked with yellow emergency lanes and embedded cargo-cart tracks. Apartment doors, utility hatches, pressure bulkheads, and small shopfronts line the sides. The air looks clean but dry. People wear layered indoor clothing: fleece work jackets, soft boots, ID patches, wrist tablets, and lightweight emergency breathers clipped at the hip. Children carry compact school packs with clear emergency masks. Small maintenance robots, knee-high and boxy, roll along the wall checking seals and air vents. Ceiling pipes and cable trays are visible but neatly labeled. Lighting is warm-white and practical.

### Buggy ride outside the domes

View from inside a pressurized Mars buggy driving between Terminus surface domes at midday. The windshield is thick, slightly dusty, and has a projected route line, but most controls are physical switches and rugged screens. Outside, the terrain is ocher-red with tire tracks, buried utility berms, marker poles, and low service roads scraped through dust. Large agricultural domes rise ahead, their lower halves buried under regolith berms, upper panels made of tinted radiation-treated glass and metal ribs. Service robots crawl along exterior seams. The sky is pale butterscotch. Dust hangs low near the ground, kicked up by distant trucks. People outside wear practical pressure suits: tan and gray, dust-stained, with hard knee pads, chest tool mounts, and mirrored helmet visors.

### Greenhouse promenade under radiation shutters

Interior of a large agriculture dome during a mild radiation advisory. Rows of fruit trees, stacked hydroponic racks, water tanks used as radiation shielding, and a central pedestrian path about 4 meters wide. Partial storm shutters cover sections of the glass, casting striped shadows across leaves and wet walkways. The lower dome wall is buried behind thick regolith banks. Residents walk slowly in low gravity, with handrails along the paths for stability. Clothing is casual but practical: quilted vests, utility trousers, soft caps, emergency mask pouches, and washable overshoes. Agricultural robots move quietly between planting racks, pruning leaves and scanning fruit.

### Open amphitheater inside a civic dome

An open amphitheater in a public civic dome in Terminus, not open to vacuum but visually open under a broad shielded glass canopy. Seating is carved into a shallow bowl, with regolith-backed walls, water-shielding columns, and emergency shutter tracks overhead. The stage is simple: matte composite floor, acoustic panels, and a backdrop showing old Mars settlement maps. The audience sits in low-g posture, with some people bracing feet under seat rails. Families wear layered indoor clothing and carry emergency masks. Security and medical robots stand discreetly near exits. The dome glass shows a dusty orange sky and distant agricultural structures.

### Night maintenance walk in utility tunnels

A maintenance crew walking through a restricted utility tunnel under Terminus at night cycle. The tunnel is narrow, about 2.5 meters wide and 2.4 meters high, with exposed but orderly pipes for water, air, heat exchange, waste, and data conduits. Everything is labeled with stenciled tags, QR plates, and color-coded bands. Red dust collects near a service hatch. Workers wear insulated coveralls, hard caps, gloves, tool harnesses, and compact emergency air bottles. Their boots have grip soles for low-g stability. A spider-like inspection robot crawls overhead checking pipe joints.

### Remote rail launch range

A remote rail launch facility outside Terminus, seen from an observation bunker or control room. A long electromagnetic launch rail stretches across the Martian plain toward low hills, with service gantries, power banks, buried cable routes, warning lights, and cleared debris zones. The main city domes are distant on the horizon, separated for safety. A sealed cargo payload sits on a launch sled, surrounded by robotic arms and suited technicians. The control room has thick glass, physical controls, checklists, authorization panels, and calm operators in practical uniforms.
