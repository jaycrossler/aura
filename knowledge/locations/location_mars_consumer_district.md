# Mars Consumer District - Terminus

## Summary

The Mars Consumer District is the retail, cafe, and lifestyle zone of Terminus. It is part shopping promenade, part electronics market, part food hall, part service corridor, and part civic pressure valve. It is where Mars residents browse, repair, upgrade, socialize, spend scarce credits, and display their relationship with technology.

It should not look like a giant Earth mall transplanted to Mars. It is life-support constrained. Its storefronts sit behind pressure-rated walls, emergency bulkheads, dust mats, utility panels, cargo-cart lanes, air-quality sensors, and repair access hatches. It has style, but the style grew over practical bones.

## Location and layout

The district occupies a connected commercial dome and several buried corridor spines near the civic and railhead zones. Surface portions provide light, prestige, plants, and advertising. Buried sections provide cheaper food, repair shops, service kiosks, and older storefronts.

### Main promenade

The central promenade is about **6-8 meters wide**, with a dedicated pedestrian flow, a narrow cargo-bot lane, emergency wall cabinets, and pressure bulkheads at intervals. Storefronts use thick transparent panels. Real glass is expensive and mostly used in prestige shops; many storefronts use layered polymer or treated transparent composite.

Background music exists, but the life-support hum never fully disappears. Mars-born artists, product ads, safety announcements, train updates, and influencer review clips compete softly in the soundscape.

### Small cafes and food stalls

Restaurants and cafes are important because apartments are compact and private kitchens can be limited. Small cafes may be only **7 meters wide and 12 meters deep**, but they become social centers.

Common food offerings:

- Greenhouse coffee blends.
- Coffee substitutes.
- Yeast pastries.
- Algae-protein noodles.
- Goat cheese flatbread.
- Printed starch bowls.
- Rabbit or chicken skewers.
- Rare Earth chocolate.
- Terminus ranch specials that change by allocation.

Tables are often lightly bolted or weighted. Chairs have foot loops or rails because low gravity makes careless sitting awkward. Customers clip emergency respirators to chairs, belts, or table hooks.

### Electronics anchors

The district has several large electronics retailers. The flagship feels like a hybrid of a high-end technology store, repair depot, implant consultation center, and product theater.

Notable sections:

**HUD implant showroom.** Multiple manufacturers, yearly model comparisons, demo stations, surgical-consult booths, pricing tiers, insurance options, and discount shelves for last year's model.

**Wearables wall.** Watches, wrist nodes, smart rings, AR goggles, smart glasses, audio implants, fitness sensors, pressure-safety add-ons, and emergency locator tags.

**Gaming systems.** Long voyages, long shifts, and long tunnel evenings support a strong gaming market. VR rigs, Aura-paired games, low-g sports simulators, and Mars-first titles appear here.

**Computers and compute hardware.** Personal compute, professional workstations, ship-grade nodes, sealed cases, ruggedized equipment, and contractor gear.

**Communications gear.** This section has been quietly thinning. Some long-range relay products are out of stock, some signs mention quality holds, and some staff avoid details. This can foreshadow supply-chain and information-control anxieties.

### Apparel, lifestyle, and service

Smaller shops sell:

- Utility jackets.
- Thermal layers.
- soft boots.
- Surface-rated gloves.
- Emergency breather cases.
- Personal mask skins.
- Low-g furniture.
- Compact apartment storage.
- Children's safety packs.
- Custom clothing patches.
- Tools and home repair kits.

## Cultural notes

**Technology arms race.** Last year's HUD sits on discount. This year's model is under glass. Next year's model has a preorder kiosk. Jace's military-grade implant exists inside this market, not above it.

**Aura-mediated shopping.** Most residents use their Aura to compare specs, prices, safety ratings, repair history, and social recommendations. Jace without full Aura access experiences the district as harder, flatter, and more humiliating.

**Food as status.** Real coffee, actual cheese, and greenhouse fruit signal status more clearly than expensive clothes.

**Cross-city shopping.** Residents from Pangu and Elysium visit. Translation overlays and political friction both appear in normal retail life.

**Safety remains visible.** Every decorative feature has to share space with pressure doors, emergency masks, route signs, and fire/decompression protocols.

## Image prompts and scene vignettes

### Small cafe in a buried neighborhood

A compact cafe in a residential tunnel neighborhood of Terminus, about 7 meters wide and 12 meters deep, with low ceilings, warm lighting, recycled wood panels, and a view through an interior window into a small herb-growing room. The tables are lightly bolted to the floor, and chairs have foot loops because Mars gravity is low. Customers wear work layers: soft boots, zip-front jackets, tool belts, ID lanyards, and emergency respirators clipped to chairs or hips. The menu board lists greenhouse coffee blend, yeast pastries, algae-protein noodles, goat cheese flatbread, and rare Earth-import chocolate priced high. A waist-high delivery robot waits at the counter with sealed food drawers. The mood is relaxed but compact, with condensation on the herb-room glass and a faint industrial cleanliness to everything.

### Consumer technology promenade

A retail promenade in Terminus under a shielded commercial dome, about 8 meters wide, with thick storefront panels, a cargo-bot lane, emergency bulkhead doors, and small planter cases behind glass. Screens show competing HUD implant models, wearable devices, pressure-safety accessories, and Mars-born influencer reviews. Shoppers wear quilted jackets, soft boots, wrist tablets, and clipped breathers. Low, boxy delivery robots weave through marked lanes. A dust mat and air curtain mark the entrance from a surface lock. The scene should feel like a future electronics district built inside a pressure vessel, not a neon cyberpunk mall.

### Post-discharge Jace shopping discomfort

Jace walks through the consumer district several weeks into his hard months. Other shoppers see Aura overlays, discounts, identity tags, and product comparisons he no longer receives. The storefronts are bright, but his experience is flat and slightly delayed. A discounted HUD model sits on an endcap. A delivery robot waits for him to move because he is standing in a cargo lane without realizing it. The scene should show social loss through interface absence, not melodrama.
