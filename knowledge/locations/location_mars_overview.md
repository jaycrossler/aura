# Mars (Inhabited World Overview)

## Summary

Mars is humanity's second inhabited world and the first place where humanity learned to treat a planet as infrastructure. By the story present, Mars hosts roughly **22,000 permanent residents** across three major cities, with additional military personnel, contractors, outpost crews, rail workers, scientific teams, and transient launch/logistics traffic. The planet also supports a large robotic workforce. Most robots are not humanoid. They are cargo tugs, inspection crawlers, cleaning units, agricultural pickers, drone aircraft, tunnel maintenance bots, freight loaders, seal-checkers, and autonomous repair carts.

Mars is not a frontier camp. It is a constrained, mature, politically divided, industrial civilization with hard limits. Air, water, food, thermal control, radiation shielding, spare parts, and transport windows define daily life. The planet's economy links Earth, Luna, Mars orbit, the surface cities, the asteroid belt, and the outer belt logistics chain.

Mars's central tension: it is rich enough to feel civilized, but no resident forgets that civilization depends on pumps, seals, filters, valves, rail schedules, food growth cycles, and maintenance discipline.

## Major settlements

The population is primarily divided among three major city-states:

| Settlement | Approximate population | Age | Political identity | Core role |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| **Terminus** | ~10,000 | ~70 years old | U.S.-owned, often styled as the U.S. Mars state presence | Regional capital, agriculture/ranching, logistics, launch, military-adjacent operations, manufacturing |
| **Pangu** | ~7,000 | ~50 years old | Chinese state/corporate hub | Mining, fuel production, heavy metals, planned industrial systems, rail/freight reliability |
| **Elysium** | ~5,000 | ~40 years old | International/European business city | Treaty commerce, law, finance, biotech, diplomacy, specialty clinics, luxury services |

Small outposts support ice extraction, weather monitoring, rail service, science work, mining, fuel pipelines, traffic control, emergency shelters, and long-range truck operations. Most outposts rely on one of the three cities for medicine, major repairs, governance, food supply, or evacuation support.

## Intercity transport

Mars has an intercity train system linking Terminus, Pangu, and Elysium. The rail network carries passengers, sealed freight, emergency supplies, military cargo, food, propellants, spare parts, and outpost rotations. The train does not make Mars feel small. It makes Mars survivable.

Surface movement also depends on **Mars trucks**: rugged pressurized vehicles used for freight, outpost support, emergency response, construction, and long-distance maintenance. A truck stop on Mars is not a gas station. It is a pressure-rated depot with charging or fuel systems, heated bays, dust removal, suit lockers, rescue drones, medical kits, cargo cranes, radiation shelter, and bunkrooms.

The train and truck systems create a social map. Rail workers, convoy drivers, outpost crews, agricultural haulers, military logistics teams, and fabrication workers all have different status and habits.

## Habitat architecture: domes, tunnels, vaults

Mars settlements combine surface domes, buried buildings, reinforced tunnels, utility galleries, and storage vaults. Domes provide light, agriculture, public civic spaces, and prestige. Tunnels provide safety, thermal stability, radiation protection, and survivable neighborhoods.

The rule of thumb: **surface is for work, light, crops, launch, vehicles, and symbolic spaces; underground is for life, storage, medicine, schools, command, and emergency survival.**

Domes use a mixture of treated glass, radiation-filtering laminates, regolith berms, water shielding, shutters, and occupancy rules. Leaded glass helps with selected radiation and visible design language, but Mars radiation safety depends primarily on mass: regolith, water, polymers, buried structures, and time limits. During solar events, shutters close and people move underground.

Large vaults are common and strategic. Water, ice, food, seed lines, biological cultures, methane, oxygen, hydrogen, metals, spare parts, batteries, medical supplies, and emergency ration blocks are stored in distributed hardened spaces. A city with one central reservoir is vulnerable. A city with twelve distributed water vaults can survive a bad month.

## Resource economy: nothing is free

Survival on Mars has a price tag, even where governments provide baseline rights. Air and water are metered. Waste streams are tracked. Food allocation, atmospheric quality, pressure integrity, and recycling compliance generate bureaucracy, social pressure, and law enforcement.

For Americans in Terminus, a constitutional baseline of air and water is treated as a civic right, but usage remains tracked and taxed above baseline levels. Basic healthcare, education, emergency shelter, and a subsidized one-way return to Earth may exist in law, but implementation depends on logistics capacity, political status, and available transport.

Food tiers are visible everywhere:

- Basic nutritional pastes, printed starches, algae-protein blocks, and fortified rice analogs are cheap or free.
- Greenhouse vegetables, real coffee, fruit, dairy, eggs, and meat cost more.
- Earth imports, heritage crops, and Terminus ranch products are prestige goods.
- Livestock products from Terminus carry political and cultural weight because they connect Mars to Earth traditions and asteroid-belt luxury markets.

## Agriculture and livestock

Mars agriculture is not decorative. It is food production, oxygen buffering, water management, mental health support, biological research, political leverage, and export economics.

Terminus holds the largest agricultural and livestock complex on Mars. Its crop domes, buried bioreactors, seed vaults, feed systems, animal habitats, aquaculture tanks, and processing facilities feed a large share of Mars and export high-value preserved food, breeding material, embryos, seed lines, and specialty biological products to the asteroid belt.

Livestock does not mean open fields under glass. It means biosecure sealed habitats with animal decks, veterinary isolation, feed conveyors, waste digesters, methane capture, water recovery, and strict quarantine. Chickens, rabbits, goats, pigs, fish, insects, and limited cattle or sheep provide a mix of calories, culture, morale, and luxury trade goods.

## Manufacturing and trade

Mars is a manufacturing planet. It imports raw and semi-processed materials from the asteroid belt, refines them, fabricates high-value goods, and exports finished components, food, biological assets, specialty electronics, sealed vehicle parts, habitat components, agricultural equipment, and orbital cargo.

The Mars fabrication economy works because raw materials avoid Earth's gravity well, while Mars provides mature labor, stable power, high automation, sealed industrial environments, and established transport infrastructure. Mars is especially good at bulky space-rated equipment, pressure systems, rover/truck parts, agricultural machinery, life-support modules, and custom fabrication.

## Robots and drones

Mars is robot-dense. A mature city may have **5-20 robotic systems per human** if tiny inspection units, warehouse carts, agricultural robots, and drones are counted. Most are small, specialized, and practical.

Common categories:

| Robot type | Common use |
|---|---|
| Inspection crawlers | Pressure seals, pipes, exterior dome seams, utility trunks |
| Cargo tugs | Railheads, food halls, warehouses, airlocks |
| Maintenance bots | Filter swaps, panel replacement, floor cleaning, dust removal |
| Agricultural robots | Pollination, pruning, harvesting, nutrient monitoring |
| Animal-care robots | Feed movement, waste handling, biosecurity cleaning |
| Security drones | Restricted-zone monitoring, emergency guidance, perimeter checks |
| Medical robots | Pharmacy, sterilization, patient transport, telemedicine support |
| Surface drones | Mapping, convoy support, search and rescue, atmospheric sampling |

Residents judge one another by how they interact with shared machines. Blocking a repair bot, ignoring a filter warning, or modifying a pressure-related device without authorization marks someone as reckless.

## Time, sleep, and coffee

A Martian sol is roughly 39 minutes longer than an Earth day. Colonial clocks use a **timeslip** to reconcile Mars time with Earth-standard schedules. This affects sleep, schooling, shift work, rail operations, and Earth-born adjustment.

Coffee is a cultural and economic signal. Locally grown Martian coffee is expensive because it consumes greenhouse area, water, temperature management, and labor. Coffee substitutes are common. Real coffee identifies status, connections, luck, or poor budget discipline.

## Policing and emergency culture

Mars law enforcement protects people by protecting resources. Primary crimes and civic violations include airlock misuse, illegal water diversion, food theft, sabotage, pressure-door tampering, black-market parts, cyber intrusion, unsafe fabrication, contamination, and reckless surface travel.

Emergency response is not limited to fire and crime. Mars responders handle decompression, toxic atmosphere, cold exposure, radiation alerts, suit failures, tunnel fires, dust intrusion, vehicle rescue, industrial accidents, biosecurity breaches, and public-shelter movement.

Every schoolchild learns basic pressure safety. Every adult knows the difference between a drill tone and a true alarm.

## Visual baseline for chapter writing

Mars should not look like chrome science fantasy. It should look like a built environment maintained by engineers, logistics crews, cleaners, agricultural technicians, and tired supervisors. Use practical details:

- Corridors are often 4-8 meters wide in civic areas, 2-3 meters in utility spaces, and 8+ meters in major transit concourses.
- Surface dust is omnipresent near airlocks, truck depots, suits, wheels, filters, and exterior maintenance bays.
- Interior spaces are clean but not sterile unless they are medical, semiconductor, or biosecurity zones.
- Clothing uses layers: fleece, quilted jackets, utility trousers, soft boots, ID patches, wrist tablets, clipped emergency breathers, and washable overshoes.
- Surface workers wear dust-stained pressure suits with hard knee pads, tool mounts, safety tethers, grip soles, and mirrored or coated visors.
- Low gravity changes posture: people use handrails, foot loops, seat rails, cargo straps, slower turns, and deliberate body control.
- Cold is managed by heated entry locks, insulated surface suits, thermal underlayers, heated vehicle bays, warm gloves, and strict exposure rules.
- Robots appear in the background of most scenes but do not dominate visually unless the scene is in a depot, farm, launch range, or military flight line.

## StoryBot consistency checks

When writing Mars chapters, flag possible inconsistencies if:

- A character sleeps long-term in an exposed surface dome without explaining shielding, exposure limits, or status.
- A scene treats glass as sufficient radiation protection without mass shielding, shutters, or occupancy rules.
- A surface trip lacks dust, cold, route discipline, pressure gear, vehicle checks, or emergency planning.
- Mars cities depend on a single point of failure for water, power, oxygen, or food.
- Robots disappear from logistics, cleaning, agriculture, inspection, and rail operations.
- Food appears casually abundant without cost, allocation, greenhouse origin, or import status.
- A large crowd gathers without pressure zoning, emergency exits, medical/security presence, or air-handling implications.
- Military, Chinese, U.S., and international jurisdictions share survival-critical resources without political friction or redundancy.
