# Mars (Inhabited World Overview)

## Summary

Mars is humanity's second inhabited world. By the story's present, the planet hosts approximately 15,000-20,000 permanent residents distributed across multiple national and multinational settlements, plus orbital infrastructure, surface industry, and substantial agricultural capacity. It is no longer a frontier in the early sense — it is a developed, increasingly self-sufficient civilization with its own politics, culture, and emerging Mars-born identity.

This document is an **overview**. Specific settlements should eventually have their own location files.

## Geographic Distribution

Mars settlements are scattered across the surface but cluster around certain favorable conditions:

- **Water ice access.** All major settlements are located near subsurface ice deposits.
- **Low elevation.** Lower elevations have higher atmospheric pressure (still vacuum-thin, but slightly less so), more atmospheric protection from radiation, and milder dust storms.
- **Favorable latitudes.** Mid-latitudes preferred — equatorial regions are too hot; polar regions are too cold and dark.
- **Geological stability.** Avoidance of seismic and volcanic risk zones (such as they are; Mars is geologically quiet).

## Habitat Architecture: Triple Redundancy

A defining feature of Mars settlement design at story start is **triple redundancy** — three independent layers of pressurized habitation, any one of which can sustain life support if the others fail. The standard pattern, refined across decades of operational experience and the lessons of several near-catastrophes, runs roughly as follows:

- **Surface domes and pressurized buildings.** Above-ground construction in pressurized domes and connected modules. Provides daylight (filtered for radiation and dust), psychological openness, and access to surface industry. The visible face of any Mars city.
- **Mid-level mid-shell construction.** A second layer, often built into hillsides, partial buried structures, or multi-story buildings whose structural shell is independent of the surface dome. Sealed against the surface; can hold pressure even if the dome above it fails. Houses much of the mid-priority infrastructure: secondary life support, medical, comms, primary industrial.
- **Underground tunnels and deep habitat.** A third layer dug into the regolith and bedrock — substantial subsurface volume housing essential infrastructure (power generation interfaces, atmospheric reserves, secured archives), some residential capacity, and refuge facilities. Survives even total surface destruction.

In an emergency, residents can retreat from layer to layer. A surface dome breach is uncomfortable but survivable; a multi-layer cascade is rare but planned for. Most Mars residents move between layers on a daily basis without thinking about it — the apartment block one layer down from the dome, the workshop one layer below that, the metro lines linking sublayers across the city.

This pattern repeats across the major settlements with national variations. Chinese cities lean more heavily on extensive subsurface construction. American cities favor the surface-dome layer for lifestyle reasons. Indian-led cities mix freely.

## Infrastructure

- **Surface habitats** are extensive — pressurized domes, buried tunnels, prefabricated modules. Construction is increasingly local using regolith-based materials and Mars-fabricated components.
- **Atmospheric processing** — there is no terraforming, but local atmospheric management within domes is mature. Some experimental subsurface bio-domes are testing limited atmospheric closure.
- **Power generation** — primarily fission reactors (large surface installations) and solar arrays. Mars has fewer treaty restrictions on nuclear power than Earth's near-orbit space.
- **Surface transport** — rover networks, increasingly autonomous, connect settlements. Long-distance transport is via reusable suborbital hoppers.
- **Orbital infrastructure** — substantial. Mars orbit hosts shipyards (where ferries are built), refueling depots, the cycler rendezvous traffic, communications relays, scientific platforms, and the orbital catch network that retrieves asteroid-belt material shipments.
- **Fabrication and manufacturing.** Mars hosts substantial heavy fabrication capacity — large-scale additive manufacturing, semiconductor fabs, smelters processing belt-mined ingots, robotic assembly lines. The U.S. settlement's Fabrication District is one of the most economically significant industrial zones off Earth. (See `/locations/location_mars_fabrication_district.md`.)
- **The orbital catch network.** Belt mining stations send raw materials and processed ingots toward Mars on slow, fuel-efficient trajectories. Mars-orbit space tugs catch these payloads and deliver them to surface drop facilities — small reusable landers that bring the materials down to fabrication districts. From the surface on a clear day, observers can watch the regular descent of materials lander vehicles. The network ties Mars manufacturing directly to belt mining; disruption in either propagates within weeks.

## Agriculture and Life Support

Mars has graduated from importing most food to producing most of it locally. Greenhouse complexes are extensive. Hydroponic and aeroponic systems are mature.

**Livestock infrastructure.** Mars livestock has scaled significantly in the past decade. Pigs, goats, poultry, fish, and assorted fowl are present in significant numbers; cattle are present but in smaller herds (because of feed-and-space economics); insects are heavily farmed for protein. The U.S. settlement alone operates multiple livestock farms with thousands of animals across species. The Chinese settlement, the Indian-led settlement, and several smaller settlements all maintain their own livestock programs, with regular cross-settlement professional cooperation among handlers (one of the channels Mei's intelligence cover legitimately operates within).

The livestock infrastructure on Mars is the basis for the ferry-to-belt livestock program. Mars handlers are among the most experienced livestock managers in the off-Earth environment, and the breeding stock for belt stations originates in Mars herds. The Origin Industries livestock-primary ferry pipeline (the *Falcon* and her sister ships) draws directly on Mars farm stock, with some farms specializing in producing animals that will ship outbound and others maintaining the stable Mars population.

## Climate and Environment

Mars is hostile. Surface conditions remain lethal — atmospheric pressure too low for unprotected human survival, temperatures range from frigid to merely cold, dust storms threaten infrastructure, radiation exposure (already a concern, soon to be much worse) is substantial.

Settlements are entirely indoor environments. Surface activity requires pressure suits. Children grow up understanding airlock procedures the way Earth children understand traffic lights.

The arriving radiation front from the supernova will, within 10-15 years of story start, change the surface radiation environment in unprecedented ways. Mars is tracking this anxiously.

## Political Architecture

No planet-wide government exists. Mars settlements operate under a patchwork of:

- National sovereignty (the United States of Mars is U.S. soil; the Chinese settlement is Chinese soil; etc.).
- International treaties governing space activity.
- Bilateral and multilateral agreements between settlements.
- Informal coordination through professional bodies, scientific consortia, and common operations groups.

This works, mostly. Disputes are managed through diplomatic channels and corporate intermediation. Major crises invoke ad hoc planetary councils. There is increasing pressure for more formal Mars-wide governance, particularly from Mars-born residents who see themselves as Martians first and nationals second.

## Cultural Character

Mars culture is increasingly its own thing. Earth holidays and traditions are observed but blended with Mars-specific commemorations. Cross-faction relationships are common, especially among the Mars-born. Languages are mixing — English remains a lingua franca, but Mandarin, Hindi, and Spanish are widely spoken, and Aura translation makes serious cross-faction conversation possible.

The Mars-born generation now coming into adulthood is distinct in measurable ways:

- **Physiologically:** Adapted to lower gravity. Taller on average, less bone density, longer-limbed. Cannot easily survive Earth gravity without preparation.
- **Culturally:** Less deferential to Earth institutions, more cooperative across factions, more comfortable with Aura-mediated cross-cultural interaction.
- **Politically:** Increasingly advocating for Mars-specific interests, including some advocates for eventual Mars independence or confederation.

## Mars and the Returning Magic

The radiation front from the supernova is projected to reach Mars in meaningful intensity within 10-15 years of story start. Mars governments and corporations are aware. Research is intensifying. Sensitive individuals on Mars at story start may be experiencing very early manifestations — easily dismissed as imagination, intuition, or coincidence — but will not begin to manifest in clearly recognizable ways until the radiation front intensifies.

The communications blackout cuts Mars off from belt experience and observation, which slows Mars's ability to learn from the belters. By the time comms are restored, belt practitioners will have decades of experience that Mars lacks.
