## Exterior — Structure and Silhouette

### Architecture
Fortuna Station is a skeletal truss spine with three side-by-side rotating rings
mounted along its length, each separated by a ~10m gap. The gaps between rings are
not empty — they are slowly being filled in with rooms, corridors, storage areas, and
connecting infrastructure that started as skeletal mounting frames and grew into
permanent inter-ring city. From a distance this gives the station a progressively
denser middle zone and two outer rings that are still cleanly separated from it.

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| Spine | Skeletal truss framework — not a single solid cylinder but an open lattice of structural members with all the utility runs, docking infrastructure, and radiators visible between them |
| Rings | Three parallel rings, ~100m radius each, ~628m circumference; stacked side-by-side along the spine like three coins held face-on; 10m gaps between each |
| Ring spacing fills | The gap between Ring One and Ring Two is now substantially filled with the Inter-Ring City; the gap between Ring Two and Ring Three is still being built out |
| The asteroid | 3 Fortuna is approximately 1km below — the station hovers above it to avoid surface dust and debris while remaining close enough for regular shuttle access and mining operations |

### Color and Livery
**Origin Industries uses bold blue on stark white.** The station should look clean and
bright — not the industrial grey-and-patch aesthetic of the Falcon, but the deliberate
corporate identity of a company that wants its belt presence to look like investment
rather than improvisation.

- Primary hull: bright white
- Blue highlights: structural accent elements, docking collar markings, the Origin logo
  on the spine's prominent face
- The cleanliness is slightly aspirational — it is cleaner-looking than the inside
  would suggest, and anything that hasn't been recently repainted shows the underlying
  grey of age

### What the Station Looks Like from ~20km
A white truss spine with three bright rings, blue-accented. Small ships (shuttles,
utility craft) docked at landing pads along the spine's surface. Large ships — the
Falcon, visiting freighters — parked at a safe distance and connected by shuttle. The
asteroid surface below is dark grey-black carbonaceous regolith with the rectangular
footprints of factory buildings, surface warehouse structures, robot hive installations,
ore bins, and crawler tracks visible from altitude. A shuttle is always somewhere in
the visual field, either ascending or descending between the surface and the station.

### Docking
**All ships dock on the spine.** The rotating rings spin at ~1.7 RPM — matching
velocities for ring docking is a safety challenge avoided except in emergencies. The
spine has:
- 20 small pads for shuttles, with direct elevator/walkway access to the rings
- Larger docking collars for cargo vessels and transient ships
- A zero-g cargo transfer hub at the spine's mid-section

Large ships like the Falcon park at a safe distance and shuttle in. The Falcon
and Victoria each have assigned berths when the station is expecting them.

**Suzi's exception:** Station security officer Suzi has a small fast ship mounted
directly to the ring (a special dispensation for emergency response) — the only
civilian vessel docked ring-side.

## Interior Lighting Character

**The LED cloudscapes are the defining visual element of Fortuna's inhabited spaces.**
They are not decorations — they are medical infrastructure. Psychological stability in
a sealed asteroid-belt station depends on simulated sky.

The cloudscapes run on an Earth-standard 24-hour cycle synchronized to the Northern
Hemisphere:
- Dawn: warm orange light slowly brightening from simulated horizon
- Day: soft blue sky with slow white clouds
- Summer: warmer lighting temperature; the station "heats up" slightly
- Night: dark sky, artificial stars, the blue of pre-dawn before sunrise
- Major Earth holidays sync: residents experience a Northern Hemisphere seasonal year

**The Arc 1 glitch progression:** As the story develops, the cloudscape displays begin
behaving incorrectly — wrong colors, wrong times of day, sudden flickering, brief
displays of things that are not clouds or sky. This starts as a maintenance nuisance
and escalates. The progression of glitches is a visual timeline of the Arc 1 story.

## Zone-by-Zone Visual Character

### Drone Ops
Half air-traffic-controller control room, half repair bay.

**Control side:** Screens covering every wall. Curved seating pods for operators —
padded, surrounded by screens at comfortable viewing distance, designed for extended
shifts. Each pod is an individual cockpit for managing hundreds of drones via high-level
interface, monitoring a dozen critical units manually, and piloting or intervening when
any drone malfunctions. Jace and Carlos share this space; the feeling is focused and
professional, similar to a civilian air traffic control tower.

**Repair side:** An open floor with workbenches, parts bins, charging cradles, and
diagnostic equipment. When a drone comes in damaged or dirty, it goes here. The floor
is scuffed from drone wheels and the benches are covered in components in various
states of disassembly.

The two halves share a space and the smell of electronics and faint ozone is constant.

### The Barn — Entry Experience
A double-airlock biosecurity entry — the outer door is standard, the inner door opens
into a smell transition that is gradual rather than immediate:

1. **Outer airlock:** Standard. A biosecurity screen, suit cleaning pads, instructions
   on what not to bring in.
2. **Inner airlock:** The smell begins here — warm, animal, hay-adjacent. Not
   unpleasant if you're used to it; immediately distinctive if you're not.
3. **Entry corridor:** Children's viewing windows at knee height looking into the
   nearest animal bays. Video screens at adult height showing: funny facts about each
   animal species, live "pig cam" feeds from inside the pens, feeding schedules, care
   information. The corridor is bright — full-spectrum animal-husbandry lighting,
   warmer and more natural than the civic corridors.

**Siren:** Siren has claimed the Barn entry area as her primary station. She
accompanies every child who enters to see or feed the animals. She has developed
strong opinions about incorrect feeding technique and will bark (once, clearly, not
aggressively) if a child is doing it wrong. Station children have learned to listen.

### Drone Ops — Sensory Profile
- **Sound:** Constant low fan hum, the click and chirp of drone telemetry, occasional
  alert tones, the sound of whatever Carlos has playing on background audio
- **Smell:** Electronics, faint ozone, coffee from whoever's on shift
- **Light:** Screen-glow dominant; the ceiling cloudscape is turned off during work
  shifts — too distracting

### Night Cycle Aesthetic
The station feels meaningfully different at 0200 vs. noon:
- **Cloudscape:** Dark blue sky with slow artificial cloud movement; simulated stars
  visible on the "clear" areas of the ceiling display
- **Lighting:** Corridor overheads dimmed to 30%; warmer color temperature
- **Traffic:** Cleaning robots outnumber humans; the cargo bot lanes are almost empty;
  a few late workers and insomniacs
- **Sound:** The station hum is more audible without the human traffic noise; the
  plumbing and ventilation systems are louder in the quiet
- **Open establishments:** Noodle counter (Nick's preferred late-night venue), one
  bar, a 24-hour maintenance kiosk

## Observation Gallery

Fortuna has a **non-rotating observation gallery** attached to the hub/spine — so the
view is stable, not spinning. Large protected windows looking out at 3 Fortuna below,
station trusses, radiators, docked small ships, and distant construction lights. Zero-g
or very low g; residents grip handrails.

There are also a few **windows with auto-shutters** in some ring corridors and modules.
The shutters close automatically when the ring is spinning and open when the spin is
off (emergency de-spin, maintenance). The social norm: everyone opens them at least
once and then never opens them again. The view of stars wheeling past is beautiful
and nauseating in approximately equal measure.

The cloudscape ceiling displays also frequently show exterior views — real-time camera
feeds of the asteroid surface, docking activity, nearby space — as one of their
rotating programs. This serves the same function without requiring a physical window.

## Erin's Inn

Carlos warns Jace about Erin's Inn on arrival day. The warning is accurate.

**Physical layout:** A restaurant-bar with fake-wood bench tables, then a hallway
connecting to five repurposed living containers, each divided into two or three
rooms. The rooms are functional. The dividers are thin. Erin's cooking is excellent
and chaotic and sometimes dangerous.

**Erin:** Red hair, energetic, a little unhinged, brilliant, absent-minded, and
intense. She is never actually seen by Jace — she is a character who is constantly
mentioned offstage, referenced by her regulars, and visible only in the effects she
produces on the people around her. She plays chess. She wins.

**The unnamed innkeeper Jace actually stays with:** Also has red hair (an unrelated
coincidence that nobody remarks on because they are belt people and have more important
things to think about). Runs a quieter bar where people gather each evening to drink
and tell stories. Jace prefers this — the atmosphere is social but not chaotic.
The innkeeper's name is never learned; he is simply the man with red hair who makes
good coffee and doesn't ask questions.

## Manufacturing — The Surface Zone

The asteroid surface manufacturing district is a physically distinct installation
approximately 1km below the station. Rectangular industrial zones visible from above:

- **Heavy factories** for smelting, metal processing, and large-scale fabrication
- **Storage buildings** for ore, volatiles, feedstock, and finished goods
- **Floating precision platforms** for crystal growth, chemical processing, and other
  work requiring no atmosphere contamination, no vibration, and exquisite temperature
  control — these platforms are at a slight distance from the main surface structures
- **Robot hive installations** — densely automated, minimal human presence
- **Surface warehouses** for equipment, spare modules, and construction materials

The station also has manufacturing embedded within the rings: the Foundry in the
Inter-Ring City area, smaller machine shops and print farms distributed through the
Civic Ring, and specialized fabrication labs throughout.

Shuttle traffic runs continuously between surface and station — materials, robots,
crew, samples.
