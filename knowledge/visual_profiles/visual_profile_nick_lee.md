### What People Notice First
He is 4'6" and looks seventeen, in a station where people take him seriously anyway
because of what he can do. The AR glasses are thick and always cracked somewhere.
The earrings look like large pearls at first glance until you hear the faint tinny
sound coming from them. His hands are always moving — keyboard gloves, constant
air-typing, small gestures that are commands. He is visibly managing many things
simultaneously and makes it look almost effortless.

His hair is the one vanity he is committed to: always styled, always tinted some
interesting colour. He takes time on this. Everything else about his appearance is
functional; the hair is for himself.

### What He Doesn't Look Like
Not physically imposing. Not someone anyone's first instinct is to defer to. The
mismatch between his appearance and his actual role as the belt's most important
AI infrastructure architect is a constant background fact of his life. He has
decided not to perform seniority.

### The Server Room
His natural environment. The chair is everything:
- A very nice ergonomic chair with a built-in back massager
- Keyboards that extend from the armrests
- A small built-in fridge
- A noodle holder
He plops into it, reclines back, pulls out his monitors, sighs contentedly, and
gets to work. Jace once asked if that was bad for his spine. Nick didn't answer.

## The Glasses Breaking Habit

A running station joke. He always sits on his glasses, or someone opens a door into
them, or someone steps on them. He looks dejected for a moment, then has a new pair
the next day. He's always testing the next model anyway.

## The Earring Instances

Not decorative. Purpose-built hardware dressed as pearl earrings. Each runs a
specialised {Aura} agent. The faint tinny sound they produce — audible to people
very close to him — is them projecting responses and alerts into his ears. From
the Astral, they appear as two tiny crystalline AI-Will masses flanking his head glow.

## The Keyboard Gloves

He's always typing or gesturing. The gloves let him:
- Enter commands for his agents via air gestures
- Type on the small screens on the back of each hand
- "Draw" commands and notes on his own skin

He is constantly muttering and twitching his fingers mid-conversation. When an alert
lands from one of his 20-30 agents, there's a brief micro-pause and a specific finger
movement before he responds to whatever he was saying. People who know him learn to
recognise the agent-flag pause.

---

## The Noodles

Every meal. Specifically ramen — he rotates between types and spices, always trying
different combinations. He preorders from noodle stalls as he's walking up to them.
He walks through the station's "Chinatown" section daily to stop at food kiosks.
A robot hands him a bowl and compostable chopsticks without breaking stride.

He keeps a small supply in the server room fridge for late-night sessions.

---

## San Francisco Residue

His monitor background image cycles through photographs of the San Francisco Bay
Bridge — different weather, different lighting, different times of day. Many of the
pictures were taken by his father, before the bay waters rose a couple of feet over
the last 90 years. He'll occasionally remark, unprompted: *"Most of those are from
before all the flooding."* He doesn't sound sad about it exactly. More like it's a
fact he's carrying.

---

## The 90-Second Crossing Aftermath

He went in once. For 90 seconds. He vomited up noodles and sat on the floor for
five minutes. His assessment: *"That's not like in the games."*

The specific wrongness was the 4D view — the sense that he could fall through the
walls, that nothing was shaped the way it should be. He does not want to repeat
this. When the memory comes up, he makes the joke about the games and then changes
the subject. The subject stays changed.
