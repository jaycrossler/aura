# Prologue

> **Epigraph (staged — author-approved 2026-07-15; add to spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but a space station is held together less by welds than by the kind of person who checks a seal twice for no reason. Every station has one. We had Helena."*

Helena Reyes woke before the lights did, the way she had every morning of her life, in the only home she had ever known.

She was twenty-six years old, and Fortuna Station had been exactly one year older than her for all of them. Her parents had come out with the second wave, when the station was three modules and a prayer, and she'd been born in a medical pod that was now a seed library. She had watched the rings go up. She'd been small enough, once, to fit inside the ventilation shrouds while they were still stacked in the cargo bay, and she was one of about four people alive who knew from personal experience that they smelled like crayons.

Her rounds took her spinward, because they always took her spinward.

Nobody paid Helena to do her rounds. That was the point of them. She stopped at the junction by the hydroponics bulkhead and laid her palm flat against the seal there, because eleven years ago it had wept a bead of condensation and she had never fully forgiven it. Dry. Good. She keyed the reading into her bracelet anyway, and {Penny} — the station system that noticed things, which was both its job description and the polite version of what people said about it — logged the reading and appended, as it had for years, a note that this seal was being inspected on a schedule that existed in no maintenance plan anywhere. {Penny} had long since stopped asking why. Helena considered this the closest any of the station's AIs came to understanding her. Two corridors on, she scattered a handful of feed into the chicken run and stood for a moment in the warm animal-smelling light while the flock argued about her. In the Barn, she topped off a water line, scratched a goat that headbutted her with what the goat probably considered affection, and traded gossip with the morning-shift keeper: the *Falcon* had cleared its final course correction and would dock inside the week. New settlers. A shipment of pigs, allegedly famous ones. A dog.

"An actual dog," the keeper said.

"I know," Helena said. "I've seen the channel."

Everyone had seen the channel.

Below her feet — a full kilometer below, through the deck and the ring structure and a lot of nothing — the asteroid turned its slow gray shoulder. 3 Fortuna. The rock they all lived on top of, technically; lived *above*, in practice, strung on a spine of trusses with the mining district winking on its surface like a small stubborn constellation. She'd been down twice in her life. She preferred it up here, where the gravity was manufactured but the community was real.

At the tenth hour she taught her class.

Yoga in one-third gravity is a different discipline than the Earth videos suggest — slower, longer, more honest. Her class met in the park under the cloudscape ceiling, fourteen regulars on mats between the planter beds, and she took them through the sequence she'd been refining for a decade. Near the end, in the long-held final pose — hips grounded, spine long, breath moving through her in tides — something happened that had never happened before.

Everything aligned.

She would not have been able to explain it and didn't try. It was as if her body had been a stiff hinge her whole life and someone had finally oiled it — a warmth rising up her spine, a sense of her own edges going soft and vast at the same time, every breath arriving like a small tide of light. It lasted maybe thirty seconds. It was the most beautiful thing she had ever felt. When she came out of the pose she laughed out loud, and half the class laughed with her without knowing why, because that was the kind of effect Helena had on a room.

*I have to tell Sophia about that,* she thought, rolling her mat.

Which reminded her: tonight.

Sophia Lotte turned thirty today, and the party at the bar was going to be the best thing to happen to Fortuna since the greenhouse expansion. Helena had helped plan it, obviously — she'd helped plan everything on this station since she was nine — and she had two objectives for the evening. One was to make sure Sophia, who worked too hard and had been looking gray and exhausted for a month, actually enjoyed her own birthday.

The other was Jin.

Jin Luong, who moved through the mining crews like a quiet mountain, who said maybe forty words a week and made every one of them count, whom she had been orbiting at a careful, cowardly distance for the better part of a year. Tonight she was done orbiting. She'd decided during the class, somewhere in all that warmth: life was good, and short, and she was going to walk up to that enormous serious man and make him laugh if it took her all night.

She showered. She wore the green top. She checked one more pressure seal on the way to the bar, out of pure habit, and it was fine, everything was fine, and she pushed through the door into light and noise and half the station singing something off-key, and her friends turned toward her the way flowers do.

That is the last anyone saw of her.

Helena Reyes — first child of Fortuna Station, keeper of its seals and its gossip and its animals, beloved of essentially everyone — died that night.

No one knows this yet. Not her friends, still singing. Not the station, turning patiently above its asteroid. Not the *Falcon*, six days out, carrying a tired man and a small brindle puppy toward all of it.

She is simply gone.
