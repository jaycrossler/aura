# Chapter 6 — Learning Mode

> **Epigraph (staged — author-approved 2026-07-15; add to spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but my reputation across the entire asteroid belt was established eleven weeks before I arrived, in nine seconds of security footage, none of which were my fault. Approximately four of which were my fault."*

Two weeks after the crisis, with the pipe sleeved and the sensors replaced and the ship settling back into its long fall toward Fortuna, Jace decided to fix the waste recycler's flow regulator, because it had been running eight percent inefficient and eight percent was the kind of number that followed him to bed.

The job needed the line purged. Purging the line needed Thing Two on the manual valve while Jace held the regulator housing. Origin's drones, he had learned, had a calibration state for unfamiliar manual tasks — the firmware called it *Learning Mode*, and in Learning Mode a drone performed each action at one-quarter speed while streaming full telemetry home to Origin for what the documentation called "experience harvesting," a phrase Jace had elected not to think about.

"Thing Two. Valve to closed. Slow is fine."

*ENTERING LEARNING MODE,* said Thing Two, and began closing the valve with the exquisite, geological patience of a student driver.

What Jace did not know — what the maintenance schematic did not show, because the schematic predated a retrofit by two owners — was that the regulator he was holding was the only thing standing between the open line and the recycler's backflush reservoir, and that the reservoir was full, and that "full," in the context of a livestock ferry's waste recycler, is a word containing multitudes.

The regulator housing came free in his hands.

There followed nine seconds that would, in time, be viewed by an estimated forty thousand human beings across the settled solar system, because the maintenance bay's social camera — the crew-morale channel, the same infrastructure that ran the pig-cam — was live, and the pig-cam's audience had learned there was a second channel, and the algorithm knew what people liked.

The footage shows the following. A tall blond man is struck at close range by a pressurized column of a substance the internet would spend two days cheerfully debating the exact composition of. The man does not fall. The man does not swear. The man stands in the stream with the rigid, doomed dignity of an officer on a parade ground in the rain, holding a regulator housing like a ceremonial sword, while a maintenance drone beside him continues closing a valve at one-quarter speed, placidly narrating *LEARNING MODE: 34 PERCENT COMPLETE* — and a small brindle puppy at the edge of frame flees the scene at maximum acceleration, feet scrabbling, achieving briefly and gloriously zero traction on the wet deck.

The valve closed. The stream stopped. The man stood dripping.

The footage ends with the man looking directly into the camera — he knew exactly where the camera was; he had installed the camera — and saying, in the flat tone of a formal report:

"Learning Mode: complete."

He spent forty minutes in the wash station. He spent three days pretending the crew wasn't calling the recycler bay "the classroom." He drafted, and deleted, a request for the footage to be pulled — deleted it because he'd read the channel terms months ago and knew the answer, and because some corner of him understood, with grim clarity, that fighting it would only feed it.

At the workbench that night, toweled and philosophical, he told the tray of Sparky prototypes about it. "This is why we're building you," he said, tapping the sensor board. "Local control. No Learning Mode. No experience harvesting. A drone that works for the person standing next to it." The board, which worked when warm, said nothing, but supportively.

*seen it,* Nick's message said, before Jace had mentioned anything. *EVERYONE'S seen it. you're famous. they're calling you Training Mode on the boards. also Dog Man, because of the dog. don't fight it. the belt decides your name before you get here, it's tradition, mine took THREE YEARS to live down and I'm not telling you what it was*

*What was it?*

*I said I'm not telling you. anyway good news, everyone at Fortuna already likes you. bad news, it's for this*

Jace looked at Cerberus, who was asleep on the clean, dry, heroic side of the bunk.

"Traitor," he said. "You ran."

Cerberus's tail thumped twice without him waking up, accepting the compliment.
