# X-00b — The Woman Who Wasn't Glowing

> **Proposed epigraph (staged — new entry for spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but the entire human return to magic began as
> two exhausted strangers in a cafeteria, failing to lift a sandwich."*
> — attributed narrator voice, dry-snark register, status: staged

---

Sophia has been pulling for four minutes. On the other side of the boundary — anchored to the wall of the real prep station, physical and heavy and *there* — a meal tray strains toward her. Her forearms tremble. Sweat beads at her temple. Her teeth are gritted like she's mid-rep on a bench press with a weight that will not budge.

Just a little longer. Just—

She hears breathing that isn't hers.

Her head snaps up and her concentration shatters; the tray drops back to the real side with a thunk she feels more than hears. There's a man standing near her table. A man and a dog — a floppy fifteen-pound brindle puppy, all ears and oversized paws, with a fitted mask sealed around his muzzle. And they are not like the others — not the blurred, fractured, glowing ghosts of the crew she watches drift through the station every night. They are *sharp*. Defined. Normal.

Like they're really here.

Cerberus writhes out of Jace's arms, hits the deck, and bolts — tail whipping, crashing into her legs, nose frantic at her hands, her fingers, her face. Real weight. Real contact. *Real.*

She breaks. She's on her knees with her face in his fur before she decides to be, tears running, and Cerberus licks her cheek through the gap above his mask seal, and for the first time in weeks she is not alone in this place. Jace crouches beside them and — without thinking, because it's already reflex — resets the dog's mask, which all that enthusiastic sniffing has torqued half off his snout.

"*Mon Dieu,*" she whispers. "You're here. You're actually here."

Jace stares at her the way a man stares at the only fixed point in a collapsing room. Behind him, Torres glows and scrolls and chews, three meters and one universe away.

"What's going on?" Jace manages. "My mask — I've got maybe ninety minutes of O2 left, and I don't—"

"You're in the Astral," she says, wiping her eyes. "You crossed over. I don't know *how*, but—" She stops. Steadies. Fails to steady. "Your oxygen. There are spare bottles racked by the prep station. I can pull one across. It's heavy — it's going to take time. Maybe twenty minutes. Pulling is—" she gestures at her still-shaking arms — "it is *lifting*. The whole time."

She locks on. Eyes closed, arms extended toward the rack on the real side, body rigid. The bottle doesn't move.

"Come here," she says through her teeth. "Help me. Add your weight. Your — intention. I don't know how else to say it."

Jace steps behind her, sets his hands on her shoulders, and leans — like pushing a door that won't open — and the moment his weight joins hers, something *shifts*. The bottle lurches. Slides. Then comes across in a rush, tumbling into her hands.

They both gasp.

"*Wow,*" she breathes, half a laugh, half disbelief. "It's so much easier when both of us— how is that *possible*?" She's already checking the seal — intact, pressure full — already swapping his cartridge, then crouching to Cerberus, working the little mask with hands that know their way around gear. The dog stands patient, tail sweeping.

"You keep looking at everything," she says gently, watching Jace's eyes skid across the room — the prep station and through it, the storage bay beyond it, Torres from an angle he shouldn't have, the back of a jacket on a man facing him. "Pick one thing. Me, or the dog. Otherwise your brain tries to hold all of it and—" a small exploding gesture at her temple. "It gets better. It took me a week to stop getting sick."

"A week. How long have you been doing this?"

"Every night. For weeks." She stands, stretching muscles clearly wrecked. "Come on. I'll walk you back. You shouldn't cross alone your first time."

The corridors are worse than the cafeteria — long sightlines where he can see three sections at once, junctions that bend in a direction his eyes keep sliding off of. Twice he stops dead because the floor ahead seems to drop away into layered brightness, and twice she touches his arm: "It's flat. I promise. Keep walking." Cerberus trots between them and her hand rides on his back the whole way, absently petting, like the dog is the one solid thing in the universe. Around them, through every wall, the station sleeps in soft light — bunks full of glowing sleepers, one insomniac's tablet, the couple with their silent midnight meal.

"Nobody believes me," she says quietly, somewhere past the hydroponics junction. "I tried to tell someone once. Early on. So now I collect data. Measurements. Timings. Photos, when I can pull a camera across — which is brutal; cameras are heavy for their size. I come every night. I'm building a case, so when I show people it's undeniable, or they'll just—" a shrug. "Say I'm tired. Say I'm crazy."

"An hour ago I was pretty sure I was having a stroke."

"See? And it was happening to *you*."

At his quarters they stop. His room is right there — and also the room beyond it, his bunk visible through the bulkhead like the station is made of smoked glass.

"What if I can't get back?" It comes out flat, but his hands aren't steady. "What if I'm lying in there right now with a burst vessel and none of this is real? What if Cerberus gets stuck — on this side, or half across, or—"

"Hey." She crouches, takes Cerberus's head in both hands, scratches behind his ears. He leans into her. "He crossed with you once already. Bodies remember the way home. I have gone back every single night, and I have never once been unable to." She looks up. "Hold your dog. Close your eyes. Think about your bunk — the weight of the blanket, the sound of the recycler. Something boring and solid and real."

"Tomorrow," she says. "Real world, first meal shift. I'll bring my notes—"

"I have an early work shift tomorrow." He hears how absurd it sounds even as he says it. "I'm going down to the asteroid."

Sophia laughs — a real one, cracked with exhaustion. "Okay. Then you can buy me dinner. For saving your life."

He gathers Cerberus against his chest. The dog's heartbeat is fast but steady through the harness. He closes his eyes. Thinks about the blanket. The recycler hum.

The brightness behind his eyelids goes dark, and the cold ordinary weight of the real world comes back like a held breath released.

He does not sleep. He lies in his bunk with Cerberus warm against his side and stares at a ceiling that is, for the first time in his life, *only* a ceiling — mercilessly, unforgivably opaque — while the whole night reorganizes itself in his memory into the shape of a dream. A vivid one. A hypoxia dream, maybe. There'd been a pressure event, he'd fumbled his mask in his sleep, his brain had done the rest. By the time his alarm goes off, he has nearly convinced himself.

Then his HUD — clock correct, radios green, blandly innocent of everything — surfaces two messages, timestamped 0247.

*Made it back to the Victoria. Told you bodies remember. — S*

*You still owe me dinner.*

He sits on the edge of his bunk for a long moment, reading them again. Then the clock catches up to him and he's pulling on his work gear one-handed, exhausted, late, feeding Cerberus with the other hand, due on a shuttle down to the asteroid in forty minutes.
