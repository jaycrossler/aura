# Chapter 1 — Departure

> **Epigraph (staged — author-approved 2026-07-15; add to spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but you can hear a spaceship breathe. A thousand tonnes of water moving through the walls to balance every human footstep. I spent four months falling asleep inside a very large animal that was politely pretending not to notice me."*

Mars let go of the *Falcon* the way Mars let go of everything: reluctantly, and with a great deal of paperwork.

Jace felt the departure burn in his teeth. Nuclear-thermal drives don't roar — the movies lie about that, the way the movies lie about most things worth doing — they *press*, a long firm hand in the small of the back that goes on for hours. He sat webbed into an acceleration couch in the ring's number-four common module with a fifteen-pound brindle puppy strapped against his chest in a harness he'd printed himself, because Origin Industries' livestock manifest classified Cerberus as cargo and cargo did not get couches.

"You're not cargo," he told the dog, quietly.

Cerberus, who had opinions about the vibration, expressed them by trying to burrow through Jace's sternum.

Through the module's one small viewport, Mars did its slow trick of becoming a place he used to live. The domes of Terminus caught the light. Somewhere down there was a base that had spat him out, a bar full of rumors, a breeder's pen where a mother dog was raising the rest of her litter, and a debt with more digits than his service number. He watched until the planet was a coin, then a marble, then an inconvenience on the glass.

Twenty-nine years old today. He hadn't told anyone on board.

---

The ship talked to itself constantly. That was the first thing he learned about the *Falcon*.

Not in words — {Falcon}, the ship's majordomo AI, spoke words only when spoken to, and then in the flat contractor-grade voice of a system that had been bought secondhand and knew it. The real conversation was in the walls: a perpetual soft *shush* and gurgle, water on the move, thousands of liters of it sliding through the ballast tanks that lined the ring. Every time a human being walked spinward, somewhere a pump nudged mass antispinward to compensate. Every time the pigs held a meeting at one end of their berth, the ship quietly disagreed with them in the opposite tank. Balance as a continuous argument. Radiation shielding as a side effect of the argument.

{Falcon} was not, however, the only AI aboard, because Origin Industries had paid for this run, and where Origin's money went, {Alex} went with it. {Alex} lived in a sanctioned partition with no executive access to anything — Maureen had seen to that in the contract, reportedly across three days of negotiation — and from that partition it cheerfully managed Jace's compliance scores, offered him premium sleep packages, and congratulated him on his hydration. Jace once watched Maureen listen to {Alex} suggest an "efficiency optimization" to her loading schedule. She let it finish. She said, "Noted." She did the opposite. It was the closest thing to a hobby he ever saw her have.

The first three nights it kept him awake. By the second week he couldn't sleep without it.

He learned the rhythm of the place. Maureen — captain, owner-operator's proxy, a woman whose warmth was so thoroughly classified that he had only rumors of its existence — ran the ship with six crew, a half-empty settler section, fifty-four animals, and a tolerance for nonsense that rounded to zero. The settler berths were half-empty because a dozen contracts had cancelled at the last minute, all at once, which nobody discussed and everybody thought about. Mei kept to the animals. Dr. Lanchee kept to her clinic. Jace kept to the maintenance queue, which was where a junior drone engineer with a two-million-token debt belonged, and found — to his genuine, embarrassing relief — that he was good at it, and that being good at it still counted for something even out here.

On day six his HUD chimed with a message routed through three relays and eleven light-minutes.

*FROM: A. GRANT, METIS STATION.*

He took it in his bunk with the door shut. The lag made it a message, not a call — her face recorded hours ago, smiling in that crooked way that had been getting them both in trouble since childhood.

"Happy birthday, little brother." A beat. Her smile did something complicated. "I know what today was supposed to look like. Captain's bars and Mom crying on the comm and all of it. I'm sorry it's this instead." She glanced off-camera, came back. "But hey — you're in the belt now. My belt. You're closer to me than you've been in six years, do you realize that? Weeks away instead of years." Another pause, and something in her face he couldn't read, something new. "Things are busy here. Good busy. Mostly. Eat something that isn't printed today, that's an order, I outrank you now — I have a *desk*."

The message ended. He watched it twice more, drafted four replies, sent the shortest one, and lay in the dark listening to the water move through the walls. Down the corridor, faintly, a pig thumped against a partition. Closer than he'd been in six years. It didn't feel close. It felt like standing outside a house with the lights on.

His head ached, low and hot, right behind the eyes — the HUD running warm again, probably. He filed it under *tomorrow's problem*, which was becoming a large file.

---

The email arrived on day nine, flagged routine, from M. CHEN, LIVESTOCK.

*Subject: did you eat my duck*

*One (1) duck is missing from Enclosure 03. Headcount 11, manifest 12, third consecutive day. You are the only crew member with unsupervised enclosure access on the maintenance rotation. Please advise.*

Jace read it three times. He looked at Cerberus. Cerberus looked back with the profound innocence of a puppy who could not yet reach a duck if his life depended on it.

He walked down to Enclosure 03 personally. Eleven ducks regarded him with contempt. He counted them four times. He checked the vents (sealed), the water reclaim (screened), the waste line (fifteen-centimeter diameter, and he measured a duck to be sure, which was an experience). There was no place on a sealed rotating spaceship for a duck to *go*. Ducks do not sublimate. And yet: eleven.

*I did not eat your duck,* he wrote back. *I have inspected all duck-egress routes. There are no duck-egress routes. I don't know how to say this professionally, but your duck has left a sealed vessel in interplanetary space, and I would like to formally register that this bothers me.*

Mei's entire reply, four hours later: *It bothers me too.*

It was the longest conversation they'd had.

---

Nights were for Nick.

Nick Lee lived on Fortuna and existed, as far as Jace could tell, primarily as a voice, a stream of message bursts, and a mecha with absurd proportions in a turn-based arena. You couldn't play anything twitchy across four light-minutes and growing — his first week out, Jace had tried a shooter out of pure habit and spent the match shooting at where people had been twelve seconds ago, a form of archaeology, not combat. So the belt played its own games: command-queue battles, simultaneous turns, everything designed around the speed of light like a house built around a tree.

*you're getting better,* Nick sent, after their forty-first match, which Jace had lost.

*I lost.*

*yeah but SLOWER. also I saved a mech for you for when you get here. it's short. you'll see why that's funny when you meet me*

Jace queued his next turn, rubbed the ache behind his eyes, and let the ship's water shush him toward sleep. Four months to Fortuna. He was twenty-nine, a million kilometers from anything he'd planned, in debt to a corporation for more than his life was insured for, sharing a bunk with a snoring puppy, losing chess-with-robots to a stranger.

It should have felt worse than it did.
