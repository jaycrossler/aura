# Chapter 9 — The First Week

> **Epigraph (staged — author-approved 2026-07-15; add to spec_chapter_epigraphs.md):**
> *"Most people don't know this, but one-third gravity has a learning curve, and the curve is shaped exactly like a man walking into a doorframe at speed because his legs got a memo his brain never read."*

One-third gravity, it turned out, was a practical joke that reset every morning.

The *Falcon*'s ring had run three-tenths; Fortuna's ran the same on paper and differently in the body — a bigger ring, a slower spin, less Coriolis whispering in his inner ear, and some deep animal ledger in Jace recalculated everything nightly and got it wrong every dawn. He misjudged the first stair of the morning and arrived at the bottom of the flight in one bounding step like an apology. He reached for his cup and lifted it with the force required by a heavier universe, sending a lens of coffee-adjacent liquid into a slow, majestic arc that he then had to stand under, waiting, like a man who has thrown himself a funeral. Cerberus adapted in a day and a half and spent the rest of the week performing low-orbit raids on furniture, radiant with betrayal-free joy.

The routine assembled itself the way routines do, out of repeated accidents.

Breakfast happened at the long tables near the noodle counter, and on the second morning an old man at the end of the table — silver stubble, machinist's forearms, a face like a well-maintained tool — broke a corner off his biscuit and held it down at his side without looking, and Cerberus, who had been conducting a survey of the floor, materialized under his hand and took it with the delicacy of a bomb technician.

"Hayes," the man said, to Jace, by way of complete self-introduction.

"Jace."

"I know." He went back to his breakfast. The whole exchange had cost him nine words of which three were biscuit-related, and by Friday it was liturgy: Hayes at the table's end, the biscuit corner broken and lowered, Cerberus's silent arrival, the transaction, no eye contact, total mutual satisfaction. Jace never once saw him arrive or leave. He appeared to be issued with the table.

The girl was different.

He noticed her on the third day — twelve, maybe thirteen, dark serious eyes — standing at the mouth of a cross-corridor watching him work an access panel. Not the way station kids watched the new man with the dog, which was openly and with petitions. From a distance. Perfectly still. When he raised a hand in greeting she was gone, not hurried, just *elsewhere*, like a correction in his vision. Carlos, asked, said: "Mira. She watches. She's alright," in a tone that closed the topic while leaving something unlocked at the back of it.

Cerberus would not go near her.

That was the strange part — his dog, the diplomatic phenomenon, greeter of two hundred strangers, who had a scheduled biscuit liturgy and a fan club and a stuffed pig. The one time their paths crossed hers in the ring corridor, Cerberus stopped, moved with quiet precision to put Jace's leg between himself and the girl, and stayed there, pressed close, until they were well past. Not shaking. Not growling. Just: *no.* Jace looked back. Mira was watching the dog, not him, with an expression he couldn't file anywhere.

And under all of it, under the new-job paperwork and the gray soup and the low-gravity pratfalls, the station hummed at a frequency he couldn't name.

He'd expected frontier-town loud, and it was, in the corridors. But conversations stopped when they turned certain corners — not because of him, he checked; because of the corners. The Chinese detachment's section kept to itself with a completeness that read as more than cultural. Doors here and there took four seconds too long to open, and everyone waited out the four seconds with a studied patience that told him the doors had been doing it a while and nobody could say why. Twice he heard the tail ends of the same conversation: *—still nothing, and Suzi's been over it how many—* and then the speakers saw the new man and found their soup fascinating.

He slept badly. Which was expected — new bed, new gravity, new hum — except that the dreams weren't the insomnia kind. They were the transit dreams, the crystal ones, back and *bigger*: hallways of layered glass going down forever, geometry with too many directions in it, and now, new, the sensation of standing in his own quarters and seeing through the wall of them, out, into a bright and jumbled forever. He woke from these with the morning headache banked low behind his left eye — quieter since arrival, actually, which perversely he found he missed, the way you'd miss a bad neighbor who knew things.

On the sixth day, security came to him.

She found him at the corner table with the soup — gray-haired, compact, mid-fifties at a guess and in better shape than he was, with the unhurried carriage he knew from every good sergeant he'd ever served under. She sat down across from him without asking, which in Jace's experience was a right that certain women earned around their twenty-fifth year of being correct.

"Suzi Gonzales. Chief of security, which on this station means chief of everything nobody else wants." She looked at Cerberus, and one corner of her mouth conceded a millimeter. "The famous dog."

"He accepts biscuits."

"I know his rate." She folded her hands on the table, and the small talk was over that fast. "You saw the jacket in the drone bay. Carlos says you didn't push. So I'll give it to you straight, once, because you're going to hear nine crooked versions of it by Friday, and because you're the one person on this station I can be straight with." She held up a finger. "You docked six days ago. That matters. Everything I'm about to say happened while you were forty million kilometers away on a documented vessel, which makes you the only adult on Fortuna with an alibi I didn't have to check."

Jace put down his spoon.

"Helena Reyes. Twenty-six. Born on this station — first child ever born here; there are two hundred and fifty people on Fortuna and I'd estimate two hundred and fifty of them loved her." The facts came in the flat cadence of a report given many times, worn smooth. "A few weeks back, night of a birthday party, she went back to her pod alone. Pod sealed from inside. Morning: no Helena. Comms bracelet on the bed. Breathing gear gone. No hatch cycle, no EVA record, no suit out of inventory, no camera gap, no body." She paused, exactly one beat. "You're an engineer. Tell me how that's done."

He ran it, because she was watching him run it, and because he couldn't not. Sealed pod. No cycle. Sensor spoofing — logged. Maintenance crawlways — cameras. Vented — impossible without a breach flag. He came up where every engineer must have come up.

"It isn't."

"It isn't," Suzi agreed. "And yet." She stood, unhurried as she'd sat. "So here's my ask, Grant, and it's small. You'll be running drones through every duct and crawlspace on my station — places no one's looked since they were welded. Eyes open. Anything that doesn't fit — a scuff where nothing scuffs, a tool out of inventory, a panel that's been off — you bring it to me before you bring it to Origin. That's all."

"You have a theory," Jace said, because she was the kind of person you could say that to, once.

Suzi's jaw worked, briefly. "I have a station where one community keeps to itself behind doors I don't have keys to, on a case where somebody would've needed keys I can't account for." She said it flat, like inventory. Then, to her credit, she heard herself. "Which is also a description of me having nothing, dressed up. Twenty years in the Army teaches you your own tells, and that one's mine." She squared her cap. "Eyes open. That's the whole job."

She left. Cerberus emerged from under the table and looked at Jace.

"Yeah," Jace said. It was becoming their word for a category of thing. He looked at his gray soup, and at the corridor where conversations stopped, and thought — with the tired clarity of a man six days into a new life — that he had moved forty million kilometers to a small town with a locked-room mystery, an unexplained hum, a girl his dog wouldn't walk past, and doors that took four seconds too long.

That night he dreamed of crystal hallways, going down forever.
