# Pinta

## Summary

The *Pinta* is the second of the three cyclers, commissioned roughly a decade after her older sister *Niña*. She is slightly larger, slightly faster, and carries a slightly larger passenger complement. She has been refit twice but less radically than her older sister, leaving her systems more internally consistent.

Her crew culture is distinctly different from *Niña*'s. Where *Niña* is ceremonial and traditional, *Pinta* is pragmatic and irreverent. The crew describe themselves as "the working ship" — a label that mildly insults both of their sister vessels.

## Design Philosophy

*Pinta* incorporates lessons from a decade of *Niña*'s operation. Her propulsion is more efficient. Her life support is closer to true closure. Her passenger accommodations are slightly more generous. She is the practical evolution of the cycler concept — fewer compromises, more capability, but not a fundamental redesign.

## History

(*Outline.*) Commissioned in the 2080s. First major refit early 2100s, focused on propulsion efficiency and additional cargo capacity. No second major refit yet at story start; she is in her prime.

## Crew Culture

The *Pinta* runs less formally than *Niña*. The captain is approachable. Crew traditions are fewer. Passenger interactions are more relaxed. Some find this welcoming; others miss the structured culture of *Niña*. *Pinta*'s crew are also the most likely to take side jobs during the deep-space stretches — manufacturing zero-g specialty goods, running research for hire, hosting paying entertainment productions.

## Capabilities and Limitations

- **Strengths:** Operational efficiency, larger capacity, more flexible crew culture, good handling of unusual cargo and special charters.
- **Weaknesses:** Less institutional depth than *Niña*; less recent refit than *Santa María*. Sometimes seen as the "safe but unremarkable" choice.

## Voice and Personality

The shipboard AI (name TBD) is more conversational than *Niña*'s — speaks more often, with a slight wit. Passengers regularly hear the ship address them directly during routine announcements.
