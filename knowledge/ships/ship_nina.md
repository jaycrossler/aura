# Niña

## Summary

The *Niña* is the first commissioned of the three Cycler-class deep-space liners that connect Earth and Mars. She has been in continuous service for several decades and has carried more colonists to Mars than any single vessel in human history. Older than her sisters, she is also the most refit — the *Niña* of today shares a name and orbital trajectory with the original launch but little else; her systems have been rebuilt three times.

Marcus Hale rode the *Niña* from Earth to Mars approximately six months before the story begins. His memory of the voyage is mostly of nausea, military-officer-class accommodation, and a deeply boring four months. He does not realize, at story start, that this voyage was the most consequential travel of his life — and not for the reasons he assumes.

## Design Philosophy

The cyclers were designed for *endurance*. Every system on a cycler must be repairable in flight, replaceable on rendezvous, and operable for decades. The *Niña* prioritizes redundancy and modularity over performance. She is not a fast or graceful ship; she is a reliable one.

Her age shows. Newer cyclers feel cleaner, more integrated, more thoughtfully designed. *Niña* is a museum of three generations of engineering — the original 2070s build philosophy in her hull, the 2090s refit in her propulsion stack, the 2110s overhaul in her life support. The mismatched generations are part of her character.

## Technical Description

### Propulsion

Chemical thrusters for course corrections; ion drives for slower trajectory shaping. *Niña*'s ion drives are an older generation than her sisters' — more reliable but lower thrust. She is the slowest of the three to make orbit adjustments, which her crew take as a point of dignity rather than a flaw.

### Life Support

Closed-loop with hydroponic gardens contributing to atmospheric processing and supplemental food. Recent refit improved efficiency to roughly 95% closure — only 5% of consumed water and atmosphere needs replenishment per cycle. The gardens are extensive and beautiful; passengers regularly visit them as a recreational space.

### Habitability

The rotating habitat ring provides approximately 0.5g across most passenger and crew accommodation. Inner core is zero-g, used for cargo, command spaces, and specialized recreational areas. Cabins range from austere (steerage colonist berths) to comfortable (corporate and military officer accommodations).

The ship is *crowded* on the Earth-to-Mars leg, with full passenger complement plus crew. On the Mars-to-Earth leg, with the colonists disembarked, she carries fewer paying passengers and feels almost spacious.

## Layout

Key spaces aboard:

- **Bridge:** in the zero-g core forward, traditional layout but expanded for the larger crew complement.
- **Engineering:** spans the propulsion stack aft, accessed through a long zero-g spine.
- **Crew quarters:** ring habitat, dedicated crew section with priority access to gardens and gymnasia.
- **Passenger quarters:** ring habitat, segmented by class. Steerage at the lowest-comfort tier; transit class in the middle; "officer's berth" (military, corporate, government) at top.
- **Mess and common areas:** several tiers, with the largest being the central passenger commons in the ring.
- **The Garden:** the largest hydroponic space, also serving as the primary social and recreational area.
- **Specialized spaces:** medical bay (substantial; full surgical), library (physical and digital), chapel/multi-faith room, gymnasia, theater.

## Crew Culture

The *Niña* crew are known among cycler personnel as the most traditional of the three. They have the longest accumulated institutional culture and they are the most ritualized — departure ceremonies, arrival ceremonies, midpoint observances on each leg, annual remembrance of crew who died aboard.

Outsiders find *Niña* culture slightly culty. Cycler crews from *Pinta* and *Santa María* tease *Niña* crew about their ceremonies, and *Niña* crew tease back about their sisters' lack of seriousness. The rivalries are good-natured.

The captain (name TBD) commands by tradition rather than strict discipline. Newer crew are mentored into the culture; those who do not adapt often transfer to the other cyclers.

## Capabilities and Limitations

- **Strengths:** Reliability, accumulated institutional knowledge, robust life support, deepest passenger accommodation traditions.
- **Weaknesses:** Slower than her sisters, older systems, occasional refit-mismatch issues.
- **Edge cases:** A crisis requiring rapid course change is harder for *Niña* than for newer cyclers. A crisis requiring deep institutional response — managing a passenger panic, navigating a complex political situation aboard — is something *Niña* is uniquely good at.
