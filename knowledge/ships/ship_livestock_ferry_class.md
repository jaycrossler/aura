# Livestock-Primary Ferry (Class Document)

## Summary

The livestock-primary ferry is the workhorse of the Mars-Belt corridor for stations in growth phase. She carries breeding herds of juvenile female animals, settlers, and modular construction infrastructure on a roughly four-to-five month transit from Mars to a designated belt station. Most of the ship is one-way: ~95% of the payload is dropped off and integrated into the destination station, while a minimal core (propulsion stack, command module, small return crew) burns back to Mars on a fast trajectory for reuse.

The specific ship that carries Marcus is to be named and developed; this document covers the class and serves as a template for individual ferry profiles.

## Design Philosophy

Ferries are designed around three uncomfortable constraints:

1. **Long transit time.** Crew and animals must survive 4-5 months in deep space.
2. **One-way payload.** Most of the ship's mass is destined to become station infrastructure on arrival.
3. **Nuclear propulsion in compliant territory only.** All operations must respect the inner-system nuclear prohibition.

The result is a ship that looks unlike anything in the cycler class — squatter, more modular, with the rotating livestock cylinder amidships and the propulsion stack mounted aft on a long, detachable spine. On approach to a belt station, the ferry visibly disassembles: the cylinder detaches and is integrated; the cargo containers separate and dock; the propulsion stack and command module reattach in a "core return" configuration and burn for Mars.

## Technical Description

### Propulsion

Nuclear thermal for high-thrust departure and arrival burns. Nuclear electric ion drives for cruise. The thermal stage is detachable and can operate independently for the return trip with reduced cargo. A typical transit profile:

- **Departure burn (Mars):** ~3-7 days of thermal acceleration.
- **Cruise (ion):** ~3-4 months of slow continuous acceleration.
- **Arrival burn (belt):** ~3-7 days of thermal deceleration.
- **Drop and reconfigure:** ~2-4 weeks at the station.
- **Return burn (Mars):** lighter ship; faster transit.

### Life Support

Closed-loop atmospheric and water systems. Substantial hydroponic capacity for the human crew. Animal canisters are self-contained: each canister has its own atmosphere processing, food delivery, water, and waste management. Cross-contamination is prevented by physical isolation.

### Habitability — For Humans

The crew quarters are arranged in the forward section of the rotating cylinder, providing 0.3-0.5g for sleeping, eating, and most living activities. Settlers (when carried) have similar accommodations, more austere. The command module in the zero-g core has work stations and the bridge. Engineering is aft on the spine, accessed through a long zero-g passage.

The atmosphere on a livestock-primary ferry inevitably smells like animals. Filters help. Filters do not eliminate. Crew adjust within the first week. New settlers complain for a month, then stop noticing.

### Habitability — For Animals

The livestock cylinder is the heart of the ship. Each species has its own canister with conditions optimized for its life cycle:

- **Pigs:** larger canister, intensive waste management, social grouping for pigs' welfare.
- **Goats:** climbing structures, rougher feed, smaller herd.
- **Cattle:** largest canister; calves only at this age; carefully controlled gravity gradient.
- **Poultry:** stacked layers, automated egg collection if any are old enough to lay.
- **Insects:** dense, low-attention, high-efficiency biomass.
- (Other species variable per shipment.)

Handlers spend most of their working hours in the cylinder, moving between canisters via airlocks. Handler stations include observation, feeding, cleaning, and direct-contact ports for hand-feeding and socializing the juvenile animals.

## Layout

Key spaces aboard a livestock-primary ferry:

- **Bridge / Command:** Forward zero-g core. Captain, pilot, sensors, communications.
- **Engineering:** Aft, on the propulsion spine. Reactor controls, drive systems, primary power management.
- **Crew Quarters:** Forward of the rotating cylinder, in the rotating section. Small private cabins (more like ships' bunks than rooms), shared sanitation, common mess.
- **Settlers' Quarters:** When carried, austere bunks in a dedicated rotating section.
- **Mess / Common Areas:** Single shared mess, doubles as recreation. A small library of physical books is traditional.
- **The Cylinder:** The rotating livestock habitat. Handlers' work area. Most of the human-animal interaction time happens here.
- **Cargo Bay:** Aft, under the spine. Modular shipping containers for infrastructure.
- **Medical Bay:** Small but capable; serves both human and animal needs (with separate sterile zones).

## Crew Culture

Ferry crew culture is distinctive — much closer than cycler culture, much rougher than Earth or Mars surface culture. The 4-5 month transit forces intimacy. Crew know each other's habits, irritations, and dreams. They eat together every meal. They cannot leave.

Roles overlap by necessity. The captain may help with animal feeding during a busy week; the cargo specialist learns to operate the ion drives. New crew (like Marcus) are mentored intensively because there is no margin for unfamiliarity.

There is also a strong handler-animal bond culture. Handlers name animals (informally; the manifest lists numbers). They develop favorites. They mourn losses. By arrival, a good handler will have raised a generation of breeding stock that they know individually — and it is common for handlers to follow "their" animals from the ferry to the station, taking shore positions to continue caring for them.

## Capabilities and Limitations

- **Strengths:** Long-duration self-sufficient operation, integrated livestock care, modular cargo handling, reliable nuclear propulsion.
- **Weaknesses:** Slow, vulnerable mid-transit, no significant defensive capability, dependent on Mars infrastructure for the return-trip configuration.
- **Edge cases:** Reactor failures, livestock disease, crew psychological breakdowns, and (newly relevant) magical anomalies in transit.

## Voice and Personality

Ferry-side Auras tend toward practicality and warmth. They learn the crew quickly, mediate small conflicts, track the animals, and maintain the kind of running operational consciousness that the small crew cannot maintain unaided. By midpoint of a transit, the ferry's Aura often feels like an additional crewmember.

## Multimedia Notes

- **Visual:** Industrial, modular, lived-in. Less elegant than a cycler. The rotating cylinder is the dominant external feature. Hull is unpainted or minimally painted; weathering is real after even one transit.
- **Audio:** The constant low rumble of nuclear systems, punctuated by animal sounds from the cylinder. Distant clanks and hisses. A ferry is never silent.
- **Scale:** Bigger than feels intuitive from inside; the long zero-g passages between sections create a sense of distance even within the ship.
