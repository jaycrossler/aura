# Livestock-Primary Ferry (Class Overview)

## Current Status

There is currently **one operational vessel** in this category: the *Falcon*. She is
not a production class ship. She is a 30-year-old one-of-a-kind Omphalos-configuration
vessel that has been continuously modified, patched, and extended since her original
launch.

Newer Omphalos-class designs exist on paper and in prototype stages. None are
operational. Until a successor vessel is commissioned, the *Falcon* is the sole means
of moving large quantities of breeding livestock and agricultural cargo to the asteroid
belt.

## The Omphalos Configuration

The *Falcon* uses what engineers call an Omphalos configuration — formally a
spine/spoke/ring vessel, colloquially "a bicycle wheel wrapped around a nuclear
tugboat" (older crews still also call her "the Merry-Go-Round"). Key features:

- **Stationary central spine** (the Candle) — carries propulsion, reactor, fuel and
  water tankage, and all thrust loads
- **Three structural spokes** connecting the spine to the ring — elevators, pipes,
  power and data lines
- **80-meter rotating ring** (the Donut), 40m radius, spinning at ~2.6 RPM to provide
  0.3g via centrifugal force
- **Conical forward debris shield** of layered fiber composite at the spine's leading face
- **~36 AI-managed fluid tank zones** lining the ring — water and argon, ~1,000 tonnes
  combined at departure, providing both dynamic ballast and radiation shielding while
  doubling as propellant reserve
- **36 standardized 13-meter module berths** (18 per side) — interchangeable habitat,
  cargo, clinic, and livestock units, relocatable between ship and station
- **Exterior drone maintenance swarm** — continuous hull repair

## Why This Configuration

Moving live animals through deep space requires:
- Sustained artificial gravity (0.3g is the proven minimum for most livestock)
- Radiation shielding beyond standard hull armor (animals are more radiation-sensitive than electronics)
- Dynamic mass balancing (animals move; ships must compensate)
- Large enclosed volume for species-isolated enclosures, feed storage, and waste management

The Omphalos configuration addresses all of these. The fluid system handles both
radiation and balance in a single integrated (if temperamental) solution. The
standardized berth system means the same physical modules that house livestock in
transit can be repurposed as station shelters or permanent structure on arrival.

## A Note on Scale

The *Falcon*'s ring is smaller and faster-spinning than a comfort-optimized design
would be — a deliberate tradeoff from her original era, prioritizing structural
simplicity and radiation margin over rider comfort. The result is a ship where mild
space-sickness and elevated bearing/plumbing stress are part of normal operation, not
a sign something has already gone wrong. Newer designs (and Fortuna Station itself, at
100m radius) move firmly in the opposite direction — larger, slower-spinning, built
for long-term comfort rather than minimum viable transit.

## Newer Designs

Next-generation Omphalos-class vessels are in development at Mars shipyards. Design
goals include:
- A larger-radius ring to bring spin rate down and reduce rider space-sickness
- Modular fluid system with cleaner pipe routing (the *Falcon*'s 30 years of add-on
  plumbing is a known failure risk)
- Improved majordomo integration across all ship systems
- Reduced crew requirement through better automation

None are commissioned. The belt's agricultural supply chain remains entirely dependent
on the *Falcon* until they are.
