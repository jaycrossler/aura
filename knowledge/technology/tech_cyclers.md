# The Cycler System

## Summary

The cycler system is the backbone of human travel between Earth and Mars. Three large ships — *Niña*, *Pinta*, and *Santa María* — operate in continuous heliocentric orbits that swing past Earth and Mars on a regular cycle. They never land, never enter low orbit. Passengers and cargo board and depart via dedicated taxi craft that match velocity, dock briefly, and detach. Each cycler functions as a permanent deep-space habitat, with rotating sections for partial gravity and accommodations for hundreds of passengers and crew.

By the story's present, the three cyclers have been running continuously for several decades and have moved tens of thousands of colonists to Mars. They are the Mayflowers of the Mars era — staid, indispensable, and slightly mythologized.

## Orbital Mechanics

A pure Aldrin cycler delivers passengers to Mars approximately once every 26 months (one synodic period of Earth and Mars). Three cyclers staggered in their orbital phases produce roughly one Mars arrival every 8–9 months across the fleet — about 1.3 Mars arrivals per year.

At 150 colonists per arrival (typical capacity), this delivers approximately 200 colonists per year via cyclers alone. Cargo and additional crew arrive via supplementary direct-transit ships during favorable launch windows.

The cyclers themselves never stop. They scream past Earth and Mars at significant relative velocities. Boarding requires a taxi craft to match velocity, dock briefly, transfer passengers and cargo, and detach to decelerate back to orbit. This takes hours and is one of the more dramatic moments of the journey — particularly for first-time passengers.

Between encounters, each cycler spends most of its orbit in deep space. This time is used for maintenance, training, social activity, and (controversially) profitable side activities like research, manufacture of zero-g specialty goods, and entertainment production.

## Propulsion and Power

Cyclers use chemical and ion propulsion only. **Nuclear thrusters are prohibited within the inner solar system** by international treaty — a regulatory reality that shapes Mars-and-inward transportation. Cyclers maintain their orbits primarily through gravitational mechanics, with chemical and ion drives used for course corrections, station-keeping, and rare orbit adjustments.

Power is provided by large solar arrays supplemented by small-scale chemical and battery systems. Solar power is reliable in the inner solar system; the cyclers do not need or use fission reactors.

## Habitability

Each cycler is a deep-space city. Working specifications:

- **Length:** 400–600 meters (working figure).
- **Mass:** Several thousand metric tons.
- **Crew:** 80–120 permanent crew, including command, engineering, medical, hospitality, and life support.
- **Passengers:** Up to 150 colonists per Earth-Mars leg, plus a smaller number of paying transit passengers and corporate personnel.
- **Rotating habitat ring:** Provides ~0.4–0.7g over much of the passenger and crew accommodation. Inner core is zero-g.
- **Gardens and life support:** Substantial closed-loop life support with hydroponic gardens contributing to atmospheric processing and supplemental food.
- **Medical:** Full suite including surgery; constrained by transit times.
- **Recreation:** Significant given long transits — gymnasia, gardens, theaters, libraries, full Aura-based entertainment.

The crew of a cycler is a distinct subculture. They are the most-traveled humans alive, but they almost never set foot on a planet. Some career crew go years without standing on solid ground. Cycler culture is often described as monastic — disciplined, ritualized, somewhat closed to outsiders, with strong internal hierarchies and traditions developed over decades of continuous operation.

## The Three Ships

- ***Niña*** — First commissioned. The senior ship. See [Niña](../ships/ship_nina.md).
- ***Pinta*** — Second commissioned, slightly larger and refit more recently. See [Pinta](../ships/ship_pinta.md).
- ***Santa María*** — Third commissioned, the largest, with the most modern systems. See [Santa María](../ships/ship_santa_maria.md).

The Christopher Columbus naming was, in-universe, controversial when proposed and remains contested. The names stuck because the corporate consortium that financed construction wanted the historical resonance and outvoted the academic objections. Some belters and Mars-born civilians refer to them by Spanish-only names or by working nicknames.

## Ownership

The cyclers are owned by a multi-corporate consortium with complex stakeholder structures. Major stakeholders include the largest American, Chinese, and European space-infrastructure firms, plus several national governments through proxy holdings. No single entity controls all three. Operating crews are technically employed by an international maritime-style authority that handles licensing, safety, and dispute resolution.

This shared ownership has stabilized the cycler system through several geopolitical crises that might otherwise have disrupted Earth-Mars transport. It also makes the cyclers somewhat politically neutral — they serve everyone, and disrupting them is in no major faction's interest.

## Cultural Importance

For a colonist headed to Mars, boarding a cycler is the closest modern analog to crossing the Atlantic on a passenger liner in the 19th and 20th centuries. The transit takes months; relationships form; people emerge changed at the other end. Cycler crews have developed traditions around departure, arrival, and the long deep-space stretches between. Marriages, deaths, conversions, and feuds all happen aboard the cyclers.

The cyclers do not run to the asteroid belt. Belt transit is handled by a separate class of nuclear-thrust ferries based at Mars (see [Nuclear Ferry System](tech_nuclear_ferries.md)). The cyclers are an inner-system institution.
