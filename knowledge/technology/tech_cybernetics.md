# Cybernetic Augmentation Systems

## Summary

By the story's present, cybernetic augmentation has progressed from medical prosthetics to a broader category of integrated neural and physiological enhancement. The technology is buggy, expensive, and unevenly regulated, but transformative for those who have access. Military, corporate, and elite users are typical early adopters. Civilian implants exist but are rarer and lag a generation behind — though, increasingly, only by months rather than years.

The most common implant class is the **HUD implant** — a heads-up display projected directly onto the user's visual field via retinal or optic-nerve interfaces, paired with a local compute node and a personal Aura instance. Other categories include neuromuscular augments, sensory extensions (infrared, ultraviolet, low-light), and biometric monitors.

## Power Sources

Implants must be powered. Two dominant approaches:

**Electrical power.** Inductive charging via a wearable device, or direct battery replacement. Requires user to manage charging cycles. Adds bulk to wearables. Most common in commercial implants.

**Glucose metabolism interface.** Implants harvest energy from the user's blood glucose, similar to a biological fuel cell. Eliminates charging requirements but increases caloric demand on the user. The user must eat more — typically 20–30% more daily calories for a heavily-augmented person. Failure modes can include hypoglycemia, especially under stress. Most common in military implants where charging logistics are impractical.

Jace's HUD uses the glucose interface — one of the reasons his caloric needs are higher than civilian baseline, and a vulnerability if food is short.

## The Military HUD Implant (Jace's Class)

Jace carries a current-generation U.S. Air Force-issued HUD implant. He had the surgery a short time before his Mars voyage and spent the *Pinta* transit recovering from the procedure and getting used to the system. By the time he arrives on Mars he has been augmented for several months but is still genuinely new to the device — calibration occasionally surprises him, and he is still learning what to suppress and what to invite.

Specifications (working):

- **Visual overlay.** Retinal projection providing tactical data, navigation, target identification, sensor feeds from connected systems.
- **Audio.** Subvocal microphone (reads throat micromovements) and bone-conduction audio output.
- **Neural interface.** Limited — provides command-level control of paired drones and systems, not full neural integration.
- **Aura integration.** Hosts a military-licensed Aura instance with restricted federation and security-cleared knowledge stores.
- **Sensor suite.** Biometric monitoring, environmental sensing (oxygen, radiation, temperature).
- **Power.** Glucose interface; supplemental electrical charging available.
- **Persistence.** Runs continuously while the user is conscious; goes into low-power "dreaming" state during sleep.
- **Recovery profile.** Surgical implantation requires several weeks of physical recovery and several months for full neural integration. Most patients begin with limited overlay and gradually expand functionality as the brain adapts. Edge cases (rejection, integration failure, cognitive complications) occur in a small percentage of installations.

When Jace is discharged, the implant is permitted to remain — it is medical equipment, not government property — but its security clearances are revoked, the military Aura instance is wiped, and a commercial Aura license is provisioned in its place. The hardware is still a generation ahead of consumer; the software and access are now civilian. Later, under the Sylvester arrangement, the device is re-elevated to civilian-contractor mode: most apps, games, courseware, and media restored; the secure military circuits and clearances do not return.

## The Consumer HUD Market

The civilian HUD market in 2105 is competitive, fast-moving, and visible everywhere on Mars and Earth. Multiple manufacturers — established medical-tech firms, defense contractors with civilian product lines, newer entrants from the consumer electronics sector — compete on form factor, software, Aura skin compatibility, surgical experience, and price.

**Update cadence.** New consumer models ship every twelve to eighteen months. Each generation adds incremental features (better resolution, expanded sensor suite, longer battery life on electrical models, improved cosmetic surgical scarring). The result is a permanent feeling of impending obsolescence — the model you implanted twelve months ago is now mid-tier; the model on the showroom wall today will be mid-tier within a year.

**Pricing tiers.** Consumer HUDs span an enormous price range:
- **Budget tier.** Basic visual overlay, minimal sensor suite, electrical power, limited Aura integration. Affordable for working-class users with savings or financing. Cosmetic scarring is more visible.
- **Mid tier.** Solid visual overlay, decent sensors, Aura integration with most popular skins. The most common civilian configuration.
- **Premium tier.** Approaches military specification on most metrics. Extensive sensor suite, glucose interface options, top-end Aura compatibility. Used by corporate professionals, surgeons, athletes, content creators, the affluent.
- **Bespoke tier.** Custom builds for specific use cases (research, extreme environments, specialized professional needs). Effectively unlimited cost.

**The upgrade economy.** Beyond the implant itself, users spend continuously on:
- Aura skins and personality configurations.
- Specialized app subscriptions (workout coaching, language tutoring, technical references, gaming).
- Premium media services that integrate with the overlay.
- Courseware and certifications delivered through the HUD.
- Cosmetic personalization of the overlay aesthetic.

For an enthusiastic user — particularly a young professional like Jace at Mars arrival — annual spending on HUD-related services can rival or exceed the cost of the device itself. Jace has spent close to a year's wages on this layer.

**Showroom culture.** The Mars Consumer District (and equivalent districts on Earth and at major belt stations) hosts dedicated HUD showrooms where buyers can preview competing models, try Aura skins, test overlay configurations on demo rigs, and consult with surgical providers. The retail experience is theatrical — display walls of competing manufacturers, side-by-side comparisons, in-store demonstrations by influencers and product specialists. (See `/locations/location_mars_consumer_district.md`.)

**Implant retention and resale.** When users upgrade to a new model, their old hardware is typically removed and either traded back to the manufacturer for credit, sold into a secondhand market, or — increasingly — donated to medical programs that refurbish older units for users who cannot afford new ones. A modest grey market also exists for stolen or salvaged implants, which the Rat's network occasionally touches.

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Phantom data.** Users often "see" overlay elements briefly when the implant is offline, the way amputees feel phantom limbs.
- **Dependence.** Heavy users find unaugmented life difficult and disorienting.
- **Glitching.** Buggy implants can produce visual artifacts, false alerts, or transient blackouts. The technology is still maturing.
- **Surgical complications.** Implant installation is significant surgery, with measurable rates of infection, rejection, and neurological complication.
- **Psychological effects.** Long-term integration of an Aura at the cybernetic level — voice in your head, data in your vision, biometrics shared — produces a relationship with one's own body and mind that is unprecedented and incompletely understood.

## Implants and Magic

This is the part that will matter.

A cybernetic HUD implant does not block magical sensitivity, but it does *mediate* it. A sensitive user with an implant may experience early flux phenomena as sensor anomalies, software glitches, or Aura uncertainty flags before they recognize them as magical. The implant is, in effect, a translator that filters the magical experience through a technological vocabulary.

This may delay recognition. It may also provide a measurement substrate that traditional practitioners never had. Implant-augmented practitioners, once they understand what they are perceiving, may have unique advantages — and unique blind spots.

There is preliminary speculation (story-internal, contested) that AI-paired implants in high-flux environments may amplify or stabilize a user's magical sensitivity. No one has tested this rigorously. By story's end, someone will.

## Cultural Distribution

- **Military and security forces:** High implant penetration. Standardized, supported, regulated.
- **Corporate elites:** High penetration, individualized, top-tier hardware.
- **Working professionals:** Moderate penetration, basic models common.
- **Belters:** Variable. Many belters have basic implants for harsh-environment work; high-end implants are rare. Maintenance and replacement parts are scarce.
- **General civilian population:** Low to moderate penetration. Many people remain entirely unaugmented by choice or cost.
- **Mars-born:** Higher penetration than Earth-born civilians of similar age, partly because Mars's information-environment culture pushes earlier adoption.
