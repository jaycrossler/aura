# The Information Environment (2105)

## Summary

The information environment of 2105 is the descendant of the early-21st-century internet evolved into something simultaneously richer, more fragmented, and more Aura-mediated. It is not information-scarce; it is information-abundant in ways that make the information itself less reliable. Anything can be found. Few things can be trusted. Most users navigate this through their personal Auras, which curate, summarize, and triangulate across an effectively infinite sea of content.

This document captures the everyday texture of how characters in 2105 access, consume, and produce information — to keep the future feeling lived-in across multiple scenes and books.

## Significance to Story

- **The future is information-abundant, not information-scarce.** When Jace looks up the people on Fortuna Station before he ships out, he can find them — message boards, video diaries, interviews, public-facing AI assessments of the community. This is not a lost world he's heading to. It's a documented one.
- **Sanitized vs. real.** What the AI recommends and what the actual experience will be are not the same. The information environment is honest in volume and dishonest in curation. By the time Jace arrives at Fortuna, he has a head full of preview content that bears partial but incomplete resemblance to the place he finds.
- **Censorship is invisible.** Filtering happens upstream of users. Most people do not notice that certain topics are quietly de-prioritized in their feeds, that certain sources don't reach them, that their Aura's "balanced summary" of a contested issue has been shaped by upstream content moderation. (See [Communications Filtering and Censorship Architecture](tech_communications_filtering.md) for the censorship side of this picture.)
- **The producers are everywhere.** Every Mars resident, every belter, every cycler crew member can (and most do) produce content. The amateur-producer to professional-producer ratio is enormous. The information environment is dominated by amateurs whose authority comes from access, not credentials.

## Major Components

### Aura-Mediated Search and Recommendation

Most information consumption begins with a question to one's Aura. *"Tell me about Fortuna Station."* *"What's the political situation on Psyche?"* *"What are people saying about the Origin contract on the belt boards?"* The Aura returns synthesized answers drawing on its base model knowledge, recently-fetched sources, and the user's stated and inferred interests.

This is the dominant interface. Direct search is still possible — most users still occasionally browse — but Aura-mediated information access is the default.

Auras are honest about their uncertainty when prompted. They distinguish primary sources from secondary, news from opinion, recent from dated. They are capable of high epistemic care when the user asks for it. They are also capable of glossy summary when the user does not ask, which is most of the time.

### The Successor to Social Media

Multiple platforms exist; no single one dominates. Common patterns:

- **Microvideo platforms.** Short-form video, the descendant of TikTok and YouTube Shorts. Where most influencer content lives. Strong algorithmic curation. Most users consume passively.
- **Long-form video platforms.** The descendant of YouTube. Where in-depth content, lectures, vlogs, documentaries, and serious reviews live. Lower volume per user, higher engagement.
- **Text-and-voice forums.** Topic-specific communities. The belter community boards. The Mars-born culture forums. The cybernetic-augment user groups. The off-Earth livestock-handler community. These are robust, often opinionated, and the source of much of the texture Jace can find about Fortuna before he goes.
- **Aura-to-Aura federation.** Some social interaction is mediated entirely Aura-to-Aura — your Aura introduces itself to a contact's Aura, they exchange context, your respective Auras suggest you might want to meet. Common in professional networking, less common in personal contexts.

### Influencer Culture

Mars and the belt have their own influencer ecosystems, often parallel to (and sometimes interleaved with) Earth's.

- **Lifestyle influencers** documenting daily Mars life — the dome culture, the food, the Mars-born experience.
- **Technology reviewers** doing genuine deep-dive reviews of the latest HUD models, Aura skins, gaming systems, and ferry-class small craft. Some are sponsored; most disclose; reader trust is calibrated accordingly.
- **Belter "frontier" content creators** — workers on belt stations producing video diaries about life there. Some have substantial Earth and Mars audiences. The pre-comms-blackout period sees a peak of belter content; the blackout cuts much of it off.
- **Niche specialists** — animal handlers documenting livestock care, fabrication shop owners walking through builds, ferry crew narrating transits. The niche layer is enormous.

When Jace researches Fortuna Station before shipping out, he is largely consuming belter influencer content. The picture is curated and overall positive. The reality will be more complicated.

### News and Journalism

The traditional news organizations of Earth still exist and still operate Mars and belt bureaus, but their reach is competing with the influencer ecosystem and with state-affiliated propaganda streams that are increasingly sophisticated.

- **Major Earth outlets** maintain Mars correspondents and belt stringers. Their content reaches Mars on a delay; users know to expect this.
- **Mars-native outlets** focus on Mars-relevant stories with Mars-native perspectives. Some are extensions of Earth brands; some are new institutions.
- **State-affiliated content** from various national governments. Chinese state media, U.S. state-funded outlets, EU public broadcasters. Generally clearly labeled but whose framings vary.
- **Corporate communications** dressed as news. The trade press for the megacorporations runs sophisticated content that is not quite advertising and not quite journalism.

A media-literate user in 2105 has to actively triangulate across these layers. Most users do not. Most users let their Aura do it for them.

### Aura Personalization

Each Aura is shaped by its user's history. Two Auras asked the same question will return subtly different answers, weighted toward each user's interests, expertise, and (worryingly, increasingly) their existing beliefs. Echo chambers in 2105 are softer and more pervasive than in 2025 — they are not platform-specific; they are personal, mediated by the AI you have spent years shaping.

This is one of the major intellectual themes the story can engage. Jace's Aura tells him certain things; Mei's Aura tells her different things; Artemis's Aura at Odysseus has been telling her things she has not been ready to believe. None of these Auras are lying. They are reflecting what they think their user wants and can absorb.

## What Users Do With All This

A typical Mars resident in 2105 spends meaningful daily time in the information environment:
- Morning Aura summary of overnight news and personal updates.
- Aura-mediated communication with family, work, friends.
- Microvideo browsing during transit or breaks.
- Long-form content (one or two pieces per week of substantial length).
- Forum participation in 1-3 communities of interest.
- Influencer subscriptions — both consuming and, for many, producing.
- Aura conversation throughout the day on questions large and small.

The information environment is not separate from life. It is a layer over life.

## Limitations and Failures

- **Aggregation distortion.** What a user's Aura tells them about a topic is a synthesis of many sources, weighted by the Aura's training, the upstream content ecosystem, and the user's history. Bias accumulates in ways individual users cannot easily detect.
- **Censorship invisibility.** Upstream filtering — by states, by corporations, by platforms — shapes what reaches users. Most users have no direct sense of what is missing. (See [Communications Filtering and Censorship Architecture](tech_communications_filtering.md).)
- **Bandwidth and latency.** Belt residents experience the information environment with significant latency — Earth content reaches them with hours of delay, sometimes more, depending on light-time and relay capacity. Belt-native content circulates locally faster than Earth-native content can reach the belt. This shapes belt culture.
- **The blackout impact.** When the communications blackout falls, the belt loses its real-time access to the broader information environment. Belters' Auras still work, but their information becomes increasingly dated, increasingly local, and increasingly belter. This accelerates belter cultural divergence.
