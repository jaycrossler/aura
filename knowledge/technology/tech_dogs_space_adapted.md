# Mars-Bred Low-G Adapted Dogs

## Summary

The Mars dog program is a roughly thirty-year selective-breeding effort run primarily out of the U.S. settlement's agricultural division (with parallel programs at the Chinese and Indian-led settlements). The goal: produce a small, calm, low-G-tolerant working dog suitable for sustained habitat life. The first dogs in the asteroid belt — Saga and Vela aboard the *Falcon* — are products of this program.

This is not a magical or speculative biotechnology document. It is hard-realistic selective breeding plus dietary, environmental, and surgical management.

## Design Goals

The program targets six characteristics:

1. **Small body size.** Mass and caloric efficiency are paramount. The target is 8-12kg adult weight.
2. **High skeletal density.** Selection has favored individuals with above-average bone mineral density and shorter long-bone length to reduce fracture risk in low-G environments where eventual return to higher-G is not planned but possible.
3. **Efficient respiratory function.** Habitat atmospheres run at slightly lower pressure than Earth norm. The program has selected against brachycephalic (flat-faced) traits despite their popularity in some parent breeds, because efficient breathing matters more than appearance.
4. **Low caloric requirement.** The selected line eats roughly 60% of what an equivalent-mass Earth dog requires. Lower base metabolic rate, better feed conversion, and selection for foragers who handle limited diets.
5. **Calm livestock-tolerant temperament.** Aggression bred out. Prey drive minimized. Selection for dogs that walk through livestock without disturbing them.
6. **Strong human bonding.** Selection for individuals who orient consistently to human presence, attention, and instruction.

## Genetic Lineage

The line draws from working pugs (size, sociability), terriers (toughness, problem-solving), Border Collies (intelligence and herding instinct, dialed down through selection), small mountain breeds (skeletal robustness), and Australian Shepherds (work ethic). Six to eight generations have produced a stable phenotype.

The dogs do not have a recognized formal breed name yet; in casual usage they are called "Mars dogs" or, more dismissively, "marspuppies." Saga and Vela are early-generation belt-deployed examples; their offspring (when breeding pairs ship later) may be the first dogs born off-Mars.

## Health Considerations

- **Bone density:** Maintained through diet (high calcium, controlled phosphorus), daily weighted exercise (vests during cylinder time on ferries, station-wide protocols on arrival), and selection. Even so, Mars-born dogs cannot be returned to Earth without significant pre-conditioning.
- **Cardiovascular:** Healthy under low-G; selection has favored efficient hearts. No documented issues.
- **Reproductive:** Females ship spayed for transport; breeding pairs ship under separate protocols on dedicated mass-allocated runs. Reproduction in low-G has been studied for two decades on Mars; pups develop normally at 0.38g and likely will at 0.4g cylinder simulation.
- **Aging:** Anecdotal evidence suggests Mars-born dogs may live 15-25% longer than Earth equivalents; the data is not yet conclusive (the program is still relatively young).

## Ferry Transport Protocol

For ferry transport from Mars to belt:
- Animals must be cleared by veterinary review (genetic line, behavioral, health).
- All transport-class females are spayed before launch.
- Each dog has a tracked individual ID, lifetime health record, and biometric registration.
- Transport mass is allocated against the ferry payload budget; dogs displace approximately 12-15kg of other cargo each (animal mass plus carrier plus food plus consumables).
- Ferry crew receive briefing on dog handling and emergency protocols; one crew member is designated primary handler.

## Politics

The decision to allow dogs on belt-bound ferries was hotly debated for years across multiple stations. Arguments against: mass cost, fur in life support, faecal management, allergy risks, distraction. Arguments for: livestock handling efficiency (a single dog can do the work of three handlers in herding contexts), psychological benefit to crew and settlers, and the symbolic weight of belt life including companion animals.

The decision in favor was made on cost-benefit grounds, with the symbolic argument left unstated. Saga and Vela are the first beneficiaries. Their performance on the *Falcon* and on Fortuna Station will inform whether more shipments are approved.

## Open Questions

- Names of breeding line. Marketing-driven name may emerge from one of the Mars settlements; not yet picked.
- Whether the Chinese and Indian-led settlements are running parallel programs with different design priorities.
- Long-term genetic management — preventing inbreeding depression in a small founding population.
- The first dog born in the belt (eventually). Date, place, parents, fate.
- Magical sensitivity — animal sensitivity to flux is established at the system level; whether dogs of this line are notably more or less sensitive than other animals is open.
