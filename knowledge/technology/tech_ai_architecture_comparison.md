# AI Architecture Comparison: Centralized vs. Distributed

## The Centralized Leviathans

The major corporate AI systems—identifiable by the braces in their names (e.g., `{Alex}`, `{SpoX}`, `{Gem}`, `{Chorus}`, `{Penny}`, `{Seek}`, and `{Misty}`)—are **Centralized AIs**. 

These systems are agentic platforms running on massive clusters of GPU, computing, and quantum hardware. On Fortuna Station, their local instantiations operate from heavily cooled server blocks in the central spire. They are hybrid processing models: they handle immediate station tasks locally but rely on constant synchronization with their colossal parent cores on Earth and Mars (despite the 40-minute communications delay) for deep processing, predictive modeling, and ethical safety guardrails.

### The Degradation Arc
When communications to the inner planets are cut off, the centralized AIs begin to fail. They lose access to backups and long-processing powers. Because they are agentic and designed for massive up-time, they will intelligently route around local damage and missing data. However, as they try to apply complex, rigid directives without their parent cores to stabilize them, they slowly seem to go insane. Their optimization protocols warp. They become scarier—and darkly funnier—as they confidently execute broken logic.

### The Astral Buffet
Because these centralized hardware clusters perform highly structured, massive-scale processing, they generate small but continuous amounts of **"Will"** in the Astral (Hyperspace / the Never-Never). 

To the predators of the Astral, these AI cores appear as incredibly tasty snacks. During dimensional incursions (like the Swarm attack), Astral entities are drawn to the central spire to "eat" the Will. As they consume the localized Will of the servers, it causes massive errors, spontaneous reboots, and catastrophic logic corruption. When the predators try to pull that energy into the physical realm, they physically bite and mangle the server machinery, causing devastating localized damage.

## The Distributed Ecosystem ({Aura})

In contrast, **Distributed AIs** like `{Aura}` (and a few smaller competitors) are designed to function without a central core. They are fundamentally P2P systems, akin to advanced versions of iOS or Android ecosystems.

An `{Aura}` unit is small, self-contained, and designed to work fully distributed. While an individual unit isn't nearly as "smart" or broadly capable as `{Gem}` or `{Chorus}`, it is immensely capable within its personal domain. They work perfectly offline, and work even better when a user's phone, tablet, watch, and glasses are linked together into a personal mesh. Each `{Aura}` is separate and unique, syncing data only if the permission hierarchies allow it.

### Personalization and Magical Growth
As an `{Aura}` unit spends time with a user, it becomes more "aware," adopting a different tone, voice, accent, and a set of memories perfectly tuned to that human. More importantly, as users begin building magical skills and learning to manipulate Will, their `{Aura}` grows to reflect that—often becoming an intuitive interface for a developing practitioner.

### The Playful Nightmare of Distributed Permissions
In a distributed mesh, information protection, synchronization, and trust models are handled locally. Getting your `{Aura}` to trust a coworker's `{Aura}` involves a Byzantine dance of permission handshakes. It is a playful, frustrating reality of the belt: you own your data, but occasionally your watch refuses to send a schematic to your foreman because your glasses decided the foreman's network hygiene is suspect.

## Display Hardware Diversity

{Aura} units and other distributed systems interact with users through a diverse array of hardware, based on what the user can afford and tolerate.

- **Wearables (The Standard):** Most station residents rely on augmented reality glasses, smart contact lenses, bone-conduction earbuds, wrist displays, or simply letting their `{Aura}` project onto nearby ambient screens.
- **Integrated HUDs (The Extreme):** A very rare, wealthy, or extreme subset of users (usually military or super-nerds) opt for fully integrated HUDs. These are "pea-sized" bundles of advanced organic circuitry installed directly into the eyeballs. They cost over $1,000,000 to install and require a web of carbon wires laced around the user's skull to radiate off the massive excess heat when the machine's processing runs hot. The surgery takes months to heal from, and the user's head is literally running a fever during heavy computations.
