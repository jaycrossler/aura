## Descent

Harsh white light floods the shuttle's cabin, casting long shadows as Mars's horizon comes into view, a jagged line against the dusty red landscape. Jace leans closer to the window, his breath fogging the glass momentarily before the ship's air circulation whisks it away. The planet looms beneath them, vast and unyielding, a world both alien and tantalizingly close.

His fingers drum nervously against his thigh, a habit he can't quite shake even now, as the shuttle shudders and begins its descent. The artificial gravity shifts, and Jace feels the weight of Mars press down on him, a gentle but insistent reminder that this is not Earth.

The shuttle touches down with a soft thud, the cabin's lights dimming to a more natural hue. Jace closes his eyes for a moment, feeling the hum of the engines settle into a quiet vibration beneath his feet. When he opens them, the hatch is already sliding open, a rush of cooler air washing over him.

Rich Cullivan is waiting at the landing pad, his silhouette sharp against the Martian sky. He steps forward with an easy, professional handshake. "Welcome to Mars, LT," he says, his voice carrying a warmth that cuts through the chill.

Jace steps out onto the surface, his boots crunching softly on the hard-packed ground. The air is thin and metallic against his tongue, filtered through layers of technology that make it breathable. He takes a deep breath, savoring the moment. "It feels... different," he admits, glancing at Rich. "Lighter, but heavier at the same time."

Rich nods, a knowing smile playing at the corners of his lips. "You'll get used to it," he assures Jace, gesturing towards the rover parked nearby. "Let's get you settled in."

As they drive through the U.S. settlement, Jace takes in the sights with wide-eyed wonder. The sky is a muted red, dust swirling in lazy eddies outside the thick polycarbonate window. Pedestrians, tall and thin in their adaptive suits, move with a languid grace, their movements dictated by the planet's gravity.

Rich points out landmarks as they pass, his tone casual yet informative. "That's the main hub," he says, nodding towards a cluster of domes connected by translucent tunnels. "You'll find most of what you need there."

Jace nods, absorbing the information as best he can. His gaze drifts to the horizon, where the faint outlines of Mars's twin moons are just visible against the dimming sky. "It really is everything I imagined and more," he murmurs, half to himself.

Rich chuckles, steering the rover with practiced ease. "Wait until you see it at sunrise," he replies, his eyes on the road. "That's when the magic happens."

The conversation shifts to more practical matters, Rich explaining the economic realities of Martian life. "See, the HUD's more than just a map," he says, glancing at Jace. "It's a lifeline out here."

Jace's face lights up. "I love the HUD," he confesses. "It's probably my favorite part of the whole military package."

Rich raises an eyebrow, amused. "How much have you spent on upgrades?"

The question catches Jace off guard, but he recovers quickly. "Close to a year's wages," he admits with a sheepish grin. "But it's worth it."

Rich's laughter is genuine, but there's an undercurrent of something else—perhaps a hint of concern. "I've got kids back on Earth," he says. "I could never afford something like that."

Jace shrugs, unfazed. "I've got a plan," he insists, his confidence unshaken. "Six-year tour, bonuses pay off the debt and tuition. By the time I'm a Captain, I'll afford a nicer model."

Rich's silence speaks volumes, a quiet head-shake as he turns his attention back to the road. They pass a public park, a splash of green in the otherwise stark landscape. People congregate around the few real plants, a reminder of Earth's distant embrace.

"People miss plants more than they realize," Rich comments, half-joking.

Jace watches the scene pass by, his excitement tempered by a growing awareness of the complexities awaiting him. As the rover pulls to a stop, he steps out, staring through the windshield at the settlement stretched before him, the faint glow of Mars's moons watching from above.

It's a new beginning, he tells himself, a promise written in the red dust beneath his feet.
